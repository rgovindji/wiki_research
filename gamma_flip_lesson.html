Section 1

What's in it for the dealer?

"If market makers sell options, don't they lose money on the ones that move against them? What's in it for them?"

Here's the key idea that unlocks everything else: a dealer (market maker) is not betting on which way the market goes. They're a shop. When you buy an option, they sell it to you and pocket a small edge — they sell it for a hair more than it's "worth" (selling implied volatility a touch rich), plus the bid/ask spread, plus a little time decay every day the option ages (called theta).

But selling you that option leaves them exposed to direction — and they don't want that. So they immediately hedge it away by buying or selling index futures, to get back to neutral (zero directional exposure). Move the index below and watch: every time price moves, the dealer trades futures to re-flatten. They genuinely don't care if it goes up or down — they just keep collecting the edge + decay.

Index: 7,450

So what's in it for them? The spread + selling volatility slightly rich + daily time-decay — collected over thousands of trades. They hedge to remove the gamble. They lose only when the market moves more violently than the option price implied (re-hedging then costs more than they collected). Hold that thought — it's exactly why "below the flip" is dangerous for them too.

Section 2

Delta & Gamma, in plain English

To hedge, the dealer needs two numbers:

Delta = "if the index moves $1, how much does my option position move?" It's the slope. To be neutral, the dealer holds an offsetting amount of futures so the slopes cancel.

Gamma = "how fast does my delta change as the index moves?" It's the curvature. This is the troublemaker: because delta keeps changing, the dealer must keep re-hedging — and gamma tells you how much futures they're forced to trade per point of index move.

Drag the spot below. The white line is an option position's value; the blue tangent is delta (the slope right under spot). Notice the slope changes as you slide — that change is gamma. Flat curve = low gamma = little re-hedging. Curvy = high gamma = lots of forced re-hedging.

Section 3 — the core

Why "against you above, with you below"

"Why do they lean against the market above the flip and with it below?"

It comes down to one thing: is the dealers' combined book long gamma or short gamma? Re-hedging to stay neutral forces opposite behavior in each case:

🛡️ LONG gamma (above the flip)

Index rises → their delta drifts positive → to re-flatten they SELL futures. Index falls → delta drifts negative → they BUY. Result: sell rallies, buy dips = they push price back toward where it was = dampening. Calm, shallow dips.

🔥 SHORT gamma (below the flip)

Index rises → their delta drifts negative → to re-flatten they must BUY. Index falls → they must SELL. Result: buy strength, sell weakness = they shove price further the way it's already going = amplifying. No shock absorbers.

Same shock, opposite outcome. Both tracks below get the identical nudge — the top dealer is long gamma, the bottom is short gamma. Use Step to go one move at a time and read what the dealer does at each step.

The whole mechanism: dealers don't choose to dampen or amplify — they're forced to, by re-hedging to stay neutral. Long gamma forces buy-low/sell-high (stabilizing). Short gamma forces buy-high/sell-low (destabilizing). The flip is just the line where the book switches from one to the other.
Section 4

So what IS the flip level (7,450)?

"What does the level actually represent?"

Every option at every strike contributes some gamma to the dealers' books. Add it all up across the whole chain and you get a total dealer gamma that changes depending on where the index is. The gamma flip is simply the index level where that total crosses zero — above it the aggregate book is net long gamma (stabilizing), below it net short gamma (destabilizing).

It is not a price target and not a magic line — it's the zero-crossing of dealer positioning, calculated from the live options chain. It drifts as positions are opened, closed, and expire.

Read it like this: as the index slides left (down) past 7,450, total dealer gamma goes from positive (green, stabilizing) to negative (red, destabilizing). That crossover point is the flip.

Section 5

Which "close" — minute, daily, or weekly?

"When you say it closed below the flip — close of what? A minute? A day?"

We mean the daily close. Here's why each timeframe matters differently:

TimeframeWhat it tells you
Intraday (minute/hour)Mostly noise. Price pokes above and below the flip all day; a single tick across the line is a head-fake, not a regime change. Don't act on it.
Daily close ✅The signal we watch. A daily settle below the flip means dealers are positioned short-gamma going into the next session — so tomorrow's catalysts land on an amplifying market. That's why we want a daily close back above the line before trusting a bounce.
Weekly / OpExThis is when the flip level itself can jump. On big options-expiration days (monthly "OpEx," quarterly "triple-witching"), huge amounts of gamma expire and roll off — so the flip can move sharply afterward. Watch these as level-reset events.
Rule of thumb: intraday crossings = noise; the daily close = the regime signal; OpEx weeks = when the line itself can move.
Section 6

Don't they steer the price to make options expire worthless?

"If they sold the most options at a level, wouldn't they want to keep the index there so those expire worthless? Isn't that the whole game?"

Great instinct — and it's half right, but the cause is backwards. There IS a real tendency for the index to gravitate toward big option strikes near expiry (traders call it "pinning" or "max pain"). But it's not dealers deliberately steering price to pocket your premium. It's an emergent side-effect of the long-gamma hedging you saw in Section 3: near a heavily-traded strike, the buy-dips/sell-rips hedging mechanically tugs price toward that strike. No conspiracy — just neutral re-hedging, magnified as expiry approaches.

And remember Section 1: the dealer is already hedged, so they're largely indifferent to whether your specific option expires worthless. Their profit is the volatility edge + decay, not your option's payout. Slide "days to expiry" toward 0 and watch the pull toward the strike strengthen:

Bottom line: pinning toward big strikes is real, but it's a consequence of neutral hedging, not deliberate steering. Dealers aren't gambling that your option expires worthless — they hedged that bet away and earn the spread either way.

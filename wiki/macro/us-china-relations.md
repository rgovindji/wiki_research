# US-China Relations

The single most important geopolitical relationship for tech, semiconductors, energy, and consumer-cyclicals positioning. Distinct from [[inflation-tariffs]] (which covers the tariff regime's macro pass-through) and [[fed-policy]] (which covers domestic monetary stance) — this page tracks the **bilateral relationship arc** and what it means for stock-level positioning.

## What this is

The structural bilateral relationship between the world's two largest economies, mapped to:
- **Trade flows** (tariffs, quotas, Phase deals)
- **Technology decoupling** (export controls, especially on AI chips and EUV)
- **Capital flows** (US restrictions on Chinese listings, ADRs, outbound US investment)
- **Geopolitical flashpoints** (Taiwan, South China Sea, Iran, North Korea)
- **Critical materials** (rare earths, processed metals)

## Why it matters for equities

Most of the wiki's AI-tech-heavy positioning is on names that **either depend on the US-China relationship staying functional ([[AAPL]], [[TSLA]], [[TSM]])** OR **on it staying restricted ([[NVDA]] / [[AMD]] capturing margin via export surcharge; [[ASML]] EUV monopoly preserved via export ban)**. **Which means:** a swing in either direction creates winners and losers within the wiki — there is no "uniformly bullish" or "uniformly bearish" outcome for the AI complex.

> **What this means:** US-China news doesn't move all tech stocks the same direction. Some companies (Apple, Tesla, Taiwan Semiconductor) benefit when the relationship is friendlier. Others (ASML, Nvidia) actually benefit from US restrictions — their monopoly value depends on it. So you can't just say "trade war = bad for tech" — you have to look at each stock individually.

## Current regime (May 2026)

### Trade
- Average US tariff rate **~12%** (up from ~2% pre-2024 baseline)
- Goldman estimate: **consumers absorb 67%** of tariff cost; remainder split exporters/importers
- Trump administration floating a **"Board of Trade"** institutionalized mechanism to replace ad hoc tariffs (per USTR Greer, May 2026) — substance unknown
- See [[inflation-tariffs]] for macro pass-through detail

### Technology export controls
- **NVDA H200 to China:** APPROVED with 25% surcharge + per-deal inter-agency review (since Dec 2025 / Jan 2026)
- **NVDA Blackwell, Rubin to China:** RESTRICTED
- **AMD MI300 to China:** RESTRICTED
- **ASML EUV to China:** RESTRICTED (this is the chokepoint Washington is least likely to concede)
- **ASML DUV to China:** Partially restricted; pre-2024 already-installed systems grandfathered
- **Status:** China has been holding out on buying H200s — wants Blackwell access as negotiating lever
- **Nvidia preparing 82,000 H200 GPU shipments** to China (March 2026 announcement)

### Trump-Xi Beijing summit outcomes (May 14, 2026)

The summit delivered Scenario A (Theater) outcomes with partial Scenario B (chip carve-out at H200 level only) — directionally consistent with the wiki's pre-summit framework in [[2026-05-12-trump-xi-summit-investment-implications]].

**Verified outcomes:**

- **"Strategic stability" framework agreed** — to guide US-China relationship for next 3 years and beyond (Beijing's preferred framing)
- **"Board of Trade" mechanism announced** (USTR Greer's pre-summit concept now formalized) — comprising senior officials from both countries to oversee Chinese commitments for significant purchases of US goods and services
- **US cleared H200 chip sales to 10 Chinese firms** including Alibaba, Tencent, ByteDance, JD.com (Reuters exclusive, May 14) — but **NO SHIPMENTS YET**. 25% revenue arrangement requires chips to pass through US territory first. **Blackwell + Rubin remain restricted.**
- **Strait of Hormuz** — both sides agreed it must remain open to support energy flow; Xi expressed interest in buying more American oil
- **Xi tells Nvidia, Tesla, Apple CEOs that China will "open wider"** — diplomatic framing aimed at US business delegation
- **Jensen Huang joined Trump trip last-minute** at Trump's invitation; picked up in Alaska en route

**Investment read:** Scenario A "Theater" outcome was the 60% probability case the wiki pre-priced. Net = stabilization (positive for [[TSM]] tail-risk reduction), incremental positive for [[NVDA]] (H200 China clearance), no change to Blackwell/Rubin restrictions, no rare-earth retaliation surfaced. Markets reacted modestly positive (NASDAQ +1.2% to record close 26,402 May 14).

### Active DoJ enforcement of export controls (NEW 2026-05-13)

The export-control regime is no longer just policy posture — it's active prosecutorial enforcement:

- **March 19, 2026 DoJ indictment** of Supermicro ([[SMCI]]) cofounder + sitting board member **Wally Liaw** (+ Taiwan GM Steven Chang + fixer Willy Sun) for conspiring to illegally export **≥$2.5B in NVDA AI tech to China** via SE Asian intermediaries. SMCI -33% on the news; Liaw left the board.
- **May 2026 Culper Research short thesis on NVDA** alleging the pattern extends through Megaspeed (Singapore), Speedmatrix, Novagate Cloud, Aivres Systems (NVDA Elite OEM), Giga Computing (NVDA Elite OEM), and YTL AI Cloud Malaysia. NYT (Paul Mozur) published parallel reporting. See [[2026-05-13-culper-nvda-short-thesis]].
- **Pattern:** Southeast Asian shell companies / data centers / OEM partners → repackaged servers → China. The Malaysia / Singapore / Taiwan corridor is the active investigation surface.

> **What this means:** US-China relations isn't just summit theater (Trump-Xi May 14-15) and policy announcements anymore — it's an active law enforcement regime that's prosecuting senior tech executives and could plausibly produce more indictments in 2026-2027. For NVDA, this is a management-risk vector. For [[SMCI]], it's the dominant overhang. For the broader supply chain, it's pressure on Elite OEMs (Aivres, Giga Computing) and SE Asian data center partners (YTL AI Cloud).

### Capital flows
- US restrictions on outbound investment into China-based AI and quantum (Treasury Outbound Investment Program, 2024)
- China VIE-structured ADRs trading at discounts; periodic delisting threats from SEC
- Chinese strategic disinvestment from US Treasuries (long arc, multi-year)

### Geopolitical flashpoints
- **Taiwan:** Underlying flashpoint for [[TSM]] thesis; status quo holds for now. Any meaningful escalation = single largest tail risk in the wiki.
- **Iran / Hormuz:** Active conflict 2026 cycle; energy spillover into [[inflation-tariffs]]
- **South China Sea:** Quiet relative to Taiwan
- **North Korea:** Quiet in this cycle

### Rare earths (the underappreciated lever)
- China controls **~85% of global rare earth processing capacity**
- **Where rare earths flow into the wiki:**
  - Permanent magnets in data center motors / generators → [[VRT]], [[ETN]], [[GEV]] supply chain
  - Neodymium magnets in EV motors → [[TSLA]] cluster
  - Phosphors / catalysts in semi manufacturing → fab operations long-tail risk
- **Base rate:** China has threatened rare earth restrictions many times since 2010, executed rarely. But the *capacity to disrupt* is real and underpriced by markets.
- **Coverage gap:** MP Materials (US-listed), Lynas (Australian) are the Western capacity plays — not yet on the wiki watchlist.

## Historical regime shifts (for analogue calibration)

| Period | Regime | Equity implication |
|---|---|---|
| 2001-2015 | **WTO accession + Engagement** | Multinationals' China-leverage was a tailwind; mega-cap tech (AAPL, MSFT) saw structural margin expansion |
| 2016-2019 | **Trump Phase 1 trade war** | Tariff escalation cycle; semis sold off on every headline; eventually Phase 1 signed Jan 2020, China underdelivered |
| 2020-2024 | **Biden continuity + decoupling** | Tariffs kept; export controls escalated (CHIPS Act, EUV ban, AI chip rules); [[ASML]] / [[NVDA]] become geopolitically protected monopolies |
| 2025-present | **Trump II selective re-engagement** | H200 export reauthorized with surcharge; Blackwell/Rubin held back; "Board of Trade" mechanism floated 2026 |

**Which means:** the relationship is on a **decoupling trend with selective re-engagement detours**. Each Trump-Xi summit creates volatility but rarely reverses the structural arc. The right framing for positioning is "**chronic, not acute**" — this isn't a one-event narrative; it's a multi-year backdrop.

> **What this means:** The US and China are slowly pulling apart on technology — and that trend has continued under both Biden and Trump. Individual summits create market drama but rarely change the long-term direction. Don't trade these meetings as if they're going to fix the relationship; treat them as noise on top of a multi-year decoupling.

## Event-driven moments (for tactical positioning)

Catalysts that historically move tech equities materially:

1. **Trump-Xi summits / leader meetings** — see [[2026-05-12-trump-xi-summit-investment-implications]] for the May 14-15, 2026 instance
2. **US semiconductor export control announcements** (typically October / new fiscal year)
3. **CCP Party Congresses + Two Sessions** (March every year)
4. **Taiwan presidential elections** (next: January 2028)
5. **Chinese stimulus announcements** (especially property + consumer)
6. **TSMC quarterly results / Taiwan strait incidents** (high-frequency)

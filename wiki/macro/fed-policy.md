# Fed Policy

## What this is
The path of US short-term interest rates as set by the FOMC. The single biggest macro variable for equity multiples.

## Current state (May 2026)
- **Federal funds target range: 3.50–3.75%** (held at the **April 2026** meeting)
- The April vote was **8–4** in favor of holding — the **largest dissent since October 1992**
- This was widely framed as **Powell's final meeting** as Chair — succession is a 2026 macro variable
- FOMC statement: "Inflation is elevated, in part reflecting the recent increase in global energy prices"

## Key drivers
- **Energy-driven inflation** — Middle East tensions are a wildcard; oil & gas pass-through is the proximate inflation source
- **Tariff pass-through** — average US tariff rate up from 2% to 12%; Goldman estimates consumers absorb 67% of the cost (see [[inflation-tariffs]])
- **Soft labor market** — the dovish dissenters lean on this; the hawks lean on sticky inflation
- **Chair succession** — post-Powell era starts mid-2026; the new chair's reaction function is unknowable until policy is set

## Forward path (consensus)
- **JPM Global Research:** Fed holds rates steady through the rest of 2026, with the next move likely a **+25bp HIKE** in Q3 2027 (note: this is a hawkish outlier — many forecasters still see cuts in 2026)
- **Market-implied (CME FedWatch):** mixed — rate paths are conditional on Middle East / energy / labor data
- **Hawks (Hammack, Kashkari, Logan):** warning persistent inflation requires patience or hikes
- **Doves:** worry about labor market deterioration

## Implications for equities
- A **higher-for-longer rates** regime compresses growth-stock multiples — a real risk for [[market-concentration]] in the Mag 7 at current valuations (see [[valuation-environment]]).
- AI capex is largely **internally funded** by hyperscaler cash flows, so direct rate sensitivity is limited at the project level — but multiple compression hits the same names.
- Defensives and value stocks (lower duration) outperform if rates stay elevated longer than expected.

## Open questions
- Who is the next Fed Chair, and what is their reaction function?
- Does energy revert (de-escalation) and free the Fed to cut, or does it stay sticky and force the next move to be a hike?
- How much of the 8–4 dissent reflects genuine policy disagreement vs. positioning ahead of a Chair transition?

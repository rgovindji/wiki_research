# Defensive Sectors (stub coverage)

## Why this page exists

The wiki's macro framework repeatedly flags **late-cycle 5/6 indicators firing**, **30y above 5%**, and **valuation concentration risk** — yet has zero coverage of the traditional defensive sectors that historically outperform when growth multiples compress. This is the **rotation-target sector page**: where to look if the wiki's bear case (multiple compression + cycle peak) plays out.

## When to look here

**Trigger conditions for rotation INTO defensives:**

1. **Late-cycle indicators 5+/6 firing** ✓ (current state)
2. **30y Treasury above 5% with soft demand** ✓ (May 14, 2026 — already triggered)
3. **June 10 CPI accelerates** → multiple compression → growth → defensive rotation (see [[2026-06-10-cpi-binary]] Scenario 3)
4. **NVDA earnings disappointment** ✗ (probability ~20%) → AI-complex sell-off makes defensive sectors relatively attractive
5. **VIX spike sustained above 25** for 5+ sessions → defensive bid
6. **Recessionary economic prints** — payrolls miss, ISM contraction, etc.

**Trigger conditions for rotation OUT of defensives:**

1. June 10 CPI cool → rate-cut trade → growth back in favor
2. Fed pivot dovish (Warsh capitulates) → reflation trade
3. Indicators drop from 5+/6 to 3-4/6 → risk-on resumption

## Why defensive sectors are NOT the wiki's first hedge

Per [[hedging-risk]] Tier 2 (situational, not currently recommended for the structural rotation):

1. **Opportunity cost dominates.** Rotating 5% of portfolio from NVDA/MSFT/GOOGL (high-conviction structural compounders) into NEE/PG/UNH means giving up the AI capex curve to buy 0.6-beta. The math has historically favored holding bull/high names through volatility.
2. **Defensive sectors have rate sensitivity too.** Utilities (especially regulated) are negatively correlated to rates — and the wiki's rate forecast is higher-for-longer.
3. **Better hedging instruments exist.** VIX call spreads, QQQ put spreads, and cash provide cleaner risk-off exposure with defined cost.

**When defensives DO make sense:** if you're already maxed on hedging budget (10%+ of portfolio in non-cash hedges) and want additional rotation exposure, defensives are the next tool. Otherwise, they're an asset-allocation decision, not a hedge.

## Key questions / what to watch

1. Does the **defensives spread vs. S&P 500** widen on rate shock? (sign of rotation in process)
2. Does **CEG/VST/TLN** behave like utilities or like AI-capex plays during the next sell-off? (separates AI-utility wrapper from true defensive)
3. **UNH** specifically — managed care has regulatory risk in 2026 (election year); separate single-name risk
4. **Dividend-yield premium vs. 10y Treasury** — when defensives yield <2% with 10y at 4.5%, the case weakens

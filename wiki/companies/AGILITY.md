## One-line thesis
Agility is the **"real revenue" Western humanoid OEM** — Digit is arguably the only humanoid doing repeated, paying, commercial work in live warehouses (GXO, Amazon pilots), backed by Amazon's Industrial Innovation Fund and a US factory (RoboFab) built for 10,000 units/yr — but it is a deeply cash-hungry, sub-scale (~100 units shipped) hardware business racing far better-capitalized rivals ([[TSLA|Tesla Optimus]], Figure at a ~$39B mark, Apptronik at ~$5B, Unitree at Chinese price points), so the bull case is "first to durable warehouse unit-economics" and the bear case is "best product, smallest balance sheet, in the most capital-intensive race in tech."

## What it does (and why it could be huge)
Agility Robotics was founded **November 2015** as a spin-out of **Oregon State University's Dynamic Robotics Lab**, by **Jonathan Hurst** (now Chief Robot Officer; the legged-locomotion academic behind the bird-leg "Cassie" dynamic-walking lineage) and **Damion Shelton** (Chairman). **Peggy Johnson** — ex-Qualcomm, ex-Microsoft (business development), ex-Magic Leap CEO — became **CEO in 2024**, a deliberately commercial/operator hire over a founder-CEO.

The product is **Digit**: a ~**5'9", ~140 lb** bipedal humanoid with two arms, **~35 lb payload** (rising to **~50 lb in v5** to hit OSHA manual-lift limits), **~2–4 hr runtime** with **autonomous charging**, purpose-built for **warehouse/logistics tote handling, stacking, palletizing/depalletizing**. Crucially, Agility's positioning is **"industrial-first"** — it deliberately narrowed to a boring, repeatable, paid task (move totes) rather than chasing the general-purpose demo race. It ships two layers around the robot:
- **RoboFab** — a ~70,000 sq ft **Salem, Oregon** factory (operational 2023), the **first purpose-built humanoid factory in the US**, with a stated **>10,000 units/yr** capacity at full ramp.
- **Agility Arc** (2024) — a **cloud SaaS fleet-management platform** (deploy/assign/monitor, WMS/WES integration) — the software layer that makes a **Robots-as-a-Service (RaaS)** model possible.

**Why it could be huge:** if humanoids become real industrial labor (see [[robotics]] timeline: 2-5 yr to first commercial inflection per Levine/Fan/Patel), the company that *first* nails durable warehouse unit-economics — repeatable task, paying customers, a financeable RaaS recurring-revenue stream, and domestic manufacturing — owns the logistics beachhead of a multi-trillion-dollar TAM (Morgan Stanley's long-horizon framing). Agility has the **earliest real commercial traction** of any Western humanoid OEM. That is the bull dream.

## Bull case (load-bearing)
1. **Earliest real commercial traction of any Western humanoid.** 100K+ totes moved at GXO is the closest thing the cohort has to *recurring, repeatable, paying* humanoid work — vs demo-reel rivals. "Boring warehouse task done reliably" is exactly the wedge that scales.
2. **"Industrial-first" focus is a feature, not a limitation.** By refusing the general-purpose race, Agility attacks a *financeable* unit-economics problem (tote handling vs $30/hr labor) — the only humanoid use case with a credible near-term ROI story.
3. **Strategic cap table that doubles as demand.** **Amazon (Industrial Innovation Fund)** and **Schaeffler** are investors *and* customers; **NVIDIA (NVentures)** and **Sony** add platform/sensor signal. Amazon's involvement is the single biggest latent catalyst (and biggest dependency).
4. **Domestic manufacturing already standing.** RoboFab (10K/yr nameplate) means the scale-up bottleneck is *demand and capital*, not "can we build a factory" — a real head start if orders come.
5. **A cheaper, sober mark.** At ~$2–2.5B, Agility is priced at a **fraction** of Figure ($39B). If humanoid-logistics is real, the risk/reward on the lowest-priced *Western leader with revenue* is more favorable than on the hype names — *if* you can get a real seat.

## Bear case / risks (load-bearing)
1. **Smallest balance sheet in the most capital-intensive race in tech.** ~$641M total raised vs Figure (multi-billion), Apptronik ($935M Series A), Tesla (effectively unlimited internal funding), and Chinese OEMs with state backing + $16K price points. Humanoid OEM-ing is a **bottomless capital sink**; Agility is structurally under-capitalized for a multi-year war of attrition. **This is the dominant risk.**
2. **~100 units shipped is *tiny*, and the Amazon pilot is narrow.** The flagship strategic relationship (Amazon) is an **R&D/"tote recycling" pilot, not a broad warehouse rollout** (per [[humanoid-oems]]) — which contradicts any "about to scale" narrative. Pilot-to-rollout cycles in warehousing run 18–30 months and routinely stall.
3. **Undisclosed unit economics = buying blind.** No revenue, margin, or burn disclosure. A humanoid sold at ~$150–250K almost certainly ships at a **loss per unit** today; RaaS only works if the lifetime economics clear — and we cannot verify they do.
4. **Competition from all sides.** Tesla (vertical integration, capital), Figure (capital + BMW deployment + Helix model), Apptronik (Google/Mercedes/Jabil manufacturing), and especially **Chinese OEMs (Unitree ~$16K, AgiBot, UBTECH)** whose price points could make a $150–250K Western logistics robot uneconomic. Agility competes on focus and traction, not resources.
5. **Humanoid-timeline risk is the whole sector's bear case.** Per [[robotics]], even bullish researchers put the *first* commercial inflection 2-5 years out; a 2-yr slip pushes Agility's break-even out the same distance — and it has the least runway to absorb a slip.
6. **The IPO premise is unconfirmed.** Going public at ~$2.5B in 2026 with undisclosed (likely heavily negative) economics, ~100 units, and a narrow flagship pilot would be an **early/defensive listing** — potentially a capital-raise-of-necessity, which historically prices poorly (cf. the Boston Dynamics ~$20B IPO skepticism, or SPAC-era robotics flops).

## The pivotal unknown(s)
1. **Is the "~$2.5B IPO" real — and what structure?** If it's a confirmed traditional IPO with named banks, an **individual can buy at listing with full S-1 disclosure** — by far the **best seat available** for a private name (better than any secondary/SPV; this is the framing the task brief correctly flags). If it's a SPAC or doesn't exist, the calculus reverts to the bad secondary/SPV seats. **This is the decision-flipping unknown.**
2. **Unit economics.** Revenue, gross margin, and loss-per-unit are undisclosed. Without them you cannot tell whether ~$2.5B is ~5x or ~50x revenue, or whether RaaS lifetime economics clear.
3. **Runway and the next raise.** With the smallest balance sheet in the cohort, *when does Agility run out of money, and is the "IPO" actually a capital-raise-of-necessity?* A defensive IPO prices very differently from an offensive one.
4. **Amazon's intent.** Does the Amazon relationship graduate from a narrow R&D pilot to a broad rollout (or an acquisition)? Amazon is the single biggest swing factor in both directions.

## How to invest / IPO access
**The IPO seat — if it materializes — is the good news here.** Unlike a typical private name where an individual is stuck with subordinated secondary common or an SPV, **an IPO lets an individual buy the listed stock at known terms with full disclosure** — the *best* seat available for a pre-public company. The task brief is right to flag this as structurally better than a secondary/SPV.

- **IPO route (cleanest — if real):** If Agility files a traditional S-1 and lists at ~$2.5B, buy the public stock at/after listing with full financials in hand. **Waiting for confirmed terms is the disciplined move** — and lets you finally see the undisclosed margins/burn before committing. If the structure turns out to be a **SPAC**, apply extra skepticism (SPAC robotics deals have a poor track record; e.g. KDK/Kodiak).
- **Secondary marketplaces (today, accredited only):** Agility is *listed* on **Forge Global, Hiive, EquityZen, Nasdaq Private Market** at **$58.93–$72.44/share** (June 2026). **But:** these are **indicative marks, not confirmed clearing prices**; expect **company ROFR / transfer restrictions**; what you'd buy is most likely **common or early preferred behind the institutional preference stack** (WP Global, SoftBank, Amazon, etc.). A secondary at the top of the indicative range, *ahead of* a possible IPO at a similar ~$2–2.5B mark, offers **little upside cushion** and an inferior seat — generally a **pass vs simply waiting for the IPO.**
- **SPV / feeder:** the most likely sub-institutional structure, and the **worst seat** — layered fees + carry, no cap-table rights, no info rights. **Especially unattractive when a cleaner public route may be imminent.**

## Questions before the IPO / to ask the seller
1. **Is there a real S-1 (or confidential filing)?** Named underwriters, structure (traditional / direct / SPAC), expected range, and timing. *(If none, the "$2.5B IPO" is rumor.)*
2. **Financials (the gap):** 2025 **revenue, ARR, gross margin, loss-per-unit, cash, net burn, runway.** Is the listing offensive (growth) or defensive (out of money)?
3. **Customer concentration:** % of revenue/deployed units from the top 1–3 customers (Amazon vs GXO vs Schaeffler); are deployments **paid commercial** or **R&D pilots**? (The Amazon pilot is narrow — verify how much "traction" is actually paying.)
4. **RaaS economics:** subscription price, utilization assumptions, churn, and whether lifetime RaaS revenue exceeds fully-loaded hardware + service cost.
5. **Factory utilization:** RoboFab actual production rate vs 10K/yr nameplate; unit cost curve.
6. **For a secondary/SPV (pre-IPO):** security type + exact liquidation preference ahead of the shares; direct vs SPV (how many layers, full fee + carry); is it an *executed trade* or an *indication*; does **ROFR** block the transfer; any info rights (likely none); last 409A.
7. **Insider behavior:** is the secondary seller a founder/early employee/early VC taking liquidity *ahead of* an IPO? Why now?
8. **Preference + dilution stack:** where does common sit after WP Global / SoftBank / Amazon preferred in a flat/down exit; how many more dilutive rounds before profitability given the small balance sheet?

## Verdict
**Lean: neutral, with a clear conditional — conviction LOW (data-limited).** Agility is, on the *product and traction* merits, one of the most credible Western humanoid stories: the earliest real paying warehouse deployments (GXO 100K totes), strategic customer-investors (Amazon, Schaeffler), a standing US factory, and a sober ~$2–2.5B mark that is a *fraction* of Figure's. **But it is the smallest balance sheet in the most capital-intensive race in technology, with ~100 units shipped, a narrow flagship Amazon pilot, and completely undisclosed unit economics** — and the headline "$2.5B IPO June 2026" premise **does not verify** (no S-1, no banks, no confirmed structure found).

**The seat, however, is the redeeming feature.** *If* a real traditional IPO materializes, an individual gets the **best possible seat** — listed stock, known terms, full disclosure — which finally surfaces the margins and burn you'd otherwise be buying blind. That makes the disciplined path obvious: **wait for confirmed IPO terms and read the S-1.** Buying secondary/SPV *now* at the top of the indicative range, ahead of a likely similar-priced IPO, is an inferior seat with little cushion — closer to a pass.

**Scenario framing:** (a) *Bull* — real IPO at ~$2.5B, disclosed economics show a credible path, Amazon/GXO deployments scale → the cheapest Western humanoid leader re-rates; best captured by buying the IPO. (b) *Base* — IPO slips or is defensive; undisclosed losses prove steep; the company needs more capital and dilutes; secondary buyers at the top mark are flat-to-down. (c) *Bear* — financing window shuts, Chinese price competition or a Tesla/Figure capital advantage strands the smallest balance sheet, the Amazon pilot never broadens → impaired, with common behind the full preference stack.

**What would change the view (falsifiers):** a confirmed S-1 with disclosed revenue + gross margin showing a real ROI path and diversified *paid* (not pilot) deployments; or a confirmed broad Amazon rollout / acquisition. **What would kill it:** evidence the "IPO" is a capital-raise-of-necessity with deep undisclosed losses and short runway; or SPV-only access at a ~$2.5B mark.

**Confidence in this analysis: MEDIUM-LOW.** Company fundamentals (product, customers, ~100 units, funding history, leadership) are well-corroborated. The **IPO claim is unverified**, and the **financials (revenue/margin/burn) are undisclosed** — the two facts that most drive the decision are exactly the ones not in evidence.

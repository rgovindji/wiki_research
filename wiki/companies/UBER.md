# Uber Technologies (UBER)

**Stance:** neutral · **Conviction:** low-medium · **Time horizon:** 12mo (AV-narrative swing) + long-term mobility platform

> **Why a page now (2026-06-20):** Created during the Cluster E fantasy-draft evaluation. UBER earns a page for one reason — **autonomy/AV**. It is the largest demand-aggregation layer for robotaxis, is committing **$10B+ to AV** (Lucid-Nuro, Avride, Wayve, equity stakes), and is in a high-stakes **transition from Waymo partner to Waymo competitor**. That makes it a real (if second-order) AI/autonomy play, not just a gig-economy stock.

## One-line thesis
UBER is the **marketplace question mark of the autonomy era**: either AV is a tailwind (Uber becomes the demand layer + fleet operator for everyone's robotaxis) or an existential threat (Waymo and OEMs disintermediate the network). **Which means** the 12-month setup hinges less on rideshare fundamentals and more on which way the AV narrative breaks.

## Bull case
- **Demand aggregation moat**: Uber is the largest pool of paying ride demand; AV companies need riders, and Uber supplies them. The bull case is that **Uber becomes the app layer for all robotaxis** regardless of who builds them.
- **Multi-horse AV strategy**: $10B+ committed across **Lucid-Nuro (SF), Avride (Dallas), Wayve (London)** plus equity stakes — diversifying away from single-vendor dependence on Waymo. Targeting robotaxi services in 10-15 cities in 2026.
- **Profitable, FCF-positive platform** at ~18% revenue growth — unlike most "AI optionality" names, the core business actually makes money.
- **Optionality without the capex of building AV in-house** — Uber buys/partners rather than burning on its own self-driving stack (which it exited years ago).
- **Delivery + advertising** provide non-mobility growth and a higher-margin ad business.

## Bear case / risks
- **Waymo is going direct**: the Uber-Waymo partnership is splitting into **direct competition** (Dallas, London, SF) by late 2026. Waymo is at ~400K paid rides/week across 10 US cities, targeting ~1M/week by end-2026 and expanding to ~20 more cities. **Which means** the single best robotaxi operator is becoming a competitor that can bypass Uber's app entirely.
- **Disintermediation is the core existential bear**: if AV operators (Waymo, Tesla) own demand via their own apps, Uber's marketplace value erodes precisely as ride economics improve.
- **$10B+ AV spend is unproven** — buying fleets and taking equity stakes converts an asset-light platform into a capital-heavy fleet bet; execution + utilization risk is high.
- **Net income fell ~30% TTM** despite revenue growth — earnings quality / one-offs worth scrutiny.
- **Regulatory + labor**: gig-worker classification and city-level AV permitting remain live overhangs.
- **AI angle is second-order**: Uber is an AV *beneficiary/aggregator*, not an AI builder — thinner fit for an AI-infra book than semis or hyperscalers.

## Key questions / what to watch
- **Waymo split outcome** — does Waymo materially pull volume off Uber's platform in SF/Dallas/London in late 2026? This is the single most important variable.
- **Uber-operated AV launches** — do Lucid-Nuro / Avride / Wayve deployments actually go live and scale on schedule?
- **AV unit economics** — are robotaxi rides on Uber's network accretive to take-rate, or margin-dilutive fleet ownership?
- **Tesla robotaxi** — does Tesla's network ([[TSLA]]) compete with or feed Uber?
- **FCF after AV capex** — does the $10B+ commitment dent the FCF story?

### [0:45 - 2:30] BOND AUCTION 101

> **VISUAL:** Animated explainer — government → investors → bond → yield chart  
> **VOICE:** Slow down here. Concept is new for most viewers.

Most retail investors never think about Treasury auctions. They should.

Here's the basic mechanic:

When the U.S. government wants to borrow money for 20 years, it asks investors to buy bonds. The interest rate — the yield — is whatever investors are willing to accept.

If a lot of investors want in, the auction goes well, and yields stay low.

If few investors want in, the auction "tails" — meaning it clears at a higher yield than expected — and rates go up.

> **VISUAL:** Cut to chart showing 30-year yield breaking above 5%

Here's why this matters for your portfolio:

Higher long-term yields are bad news for growth stocks.

Why? Because growth stocks are valued on cash flows years from now. When yields go up, future cash flows are worth less today. So stocks like NVIDIA, Microsoft, and the whole AI complex sell off when yields rise.

> **VISUAL:** Cut to recent 30-year auction data — bid-to-cover, yield

Last Wednesday — May 14 — the 30-year Treasury auction cleared at 5.046 percent. That's the first auction above 5 percent since 2007.

Bid-to-cover — that's a measure of demand — came in at 2.3 times. Well below the 2.42 average of recent auctions.

Translation: demand was soft. Investors are demanding higher yields to lend to the U.S. government.

> **VISUAL:** Calendar showing May 14 → May 20 progression

If Wednesday's 20-year auction also tails — that's two strikes in a row. The market will stop treating high yields as a passing scare and start treating them as a structural problem.

---

### [2:30 - 4:00] WHY NVDA EARNINGS MATTER

> **VISUAL:** Pie chart of S&P 500 sector weights → NVDA highlighted at 7.5%

OK, so what about NVIDIA?

NVIDIA is currently 7.5 percent of the S&P 500. That means if you own VOO, SPY, or any S&P 500 fund — and most retirement accounts do — you already have meaningful NVIDIA exposure, whether you chose it or not.

Wall Street expects $1.78 in earnings per share on $78.98 billion of revenue. That's 78 percent growth, year over year.

The stock has run up to all-time highs going into the report.

> **VISUAL:** Three-column callout: GM, Vera Rubin, China

Three things to watch on the call:

**One.** Gross margin trajectory. Above 73 percent — bullish. Below 72 percent — bearish.

**Two.** Vera Rubin demand commentary. Vera Rubin is NVIDIA's next-generation chip platform. Specific customer commitments — bullish. Vague language — neutral.

**Three.** China update. The U.S. has approved NVIDIA to sell H200 chips to 10 Chinese firms. China hasn't approved purchases. If NVIDIA doesn't mention China revenue in guidance, expect a sell-off.

### [4:00 - 5:00] THE COMPOUND SHOCK

> **VISUAL:** Split screen — bond chart on left, NVDA chart on right; both falling

Here's the scenario nobody is talking about.

What if the 20-year auction tails AND NVIDIA misses guidance — on the same afternoon?

Bond yields spike. AI stocks sell off. The broader market follows. You could see the S&P 500 down 2 to 3 percent in a single session.

It's not the base case. But it's a real tail scenario.

What if the opposite happens? Strong auction, NVIDIA beats, Vera Rubin commentary is fire?

> **VISUAL:** Split flips to green — both charts up

You could see the S&P 500 hit new all-time highs by Thursday close.

Wednesday is a fork-in-the-road day.

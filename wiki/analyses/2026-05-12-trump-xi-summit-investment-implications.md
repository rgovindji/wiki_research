## Decoding "open China"

In Trump-speak, "open China" almost always means: **we want China to buy more US stuff and stop blocking US companies from selling there** — *not* "the US is conceding to China." It's a market-access ask, not a unilateral opening.

**Which means:** the trade is asymmetric. The US wants Boeing orders, agricultural exports, rare earth access. China wants advanced chips, tariff relief, and a softer posture on Taiwan. **The gap between the two asks defines the deal space** — and it's not obvious there's overlap on terms that don't politically cost both leaders.

> **What this means:** Trump saying "open China" doesn't mean the US is making concessions. He's pushing China to open up *to American businesses* — buy more US planes, food, raw materials. China wants something different in return: access to advanced AI chips and a less aggressive US stance on Taiwan. Both sides may walk away without a real deal because what each wants would cost the other leader politically.

## The chip-export backdrop (CRITICAL — already partly priced)

This part is essential to interpret the summit correctly:

- **Dec 2025 / Jan 2026:** Trump authorized [[NVDA]] H200 exports to China with a **25% surcharge** and per-deal inter-agency approval (reversing the Biden-era blanket restriction)
- **Mar 2026:** US tightened AI chip rules *ahead* of the summit — classic negotiating posture
- **Status today:** Nvidia preparing **82,000 H200 GPU shipments** to China. **Blackwell and Rubin remain restricted.**
- **The surprise:** China hasn't been buying many H200s. Beijing is holding out for **Blackwell access** as leverage.

**Which means:** the "Trump opens China to NVDA" trade has already started — H200 is the carrot in motion. The summit question is whether **Blackwell joins the carrot pile** with a surcharge. Most analysts say no — Blackwell and Rubin are the leading-edge chokepoint Washington won't concede. Even if Blackwell is partially loosened, the 25% surcharge model means NVDA captures the value uplift, not China.

> **What this means:** The US already allowed Nvidia to sell its previous-generation H200 chips to China (with a 25% tax) several months ago — but China refused to buy many because they want Nvidia's *current* generation (Blackwell). The big question at the summit is whether Trump opens up Blackwell. Most experts think no — that's the line the US won't cross. So the "China open to AI chips" story is already mostly priced in.

## The one underappreciated risk: rare earths

Rare earths are the supply-chain attack surface our wiki doesn't have explicit coverage on. China controls ~85% of global rare earth processing.

**Where rare earths matter to our wiki:**
- **Permanent magnets** in data center motors, generators, fans — [[VRT]], [[ETN]], [[GEV]] supply chain
- **Neodymium magnets** in EV motors — [[TSLA]] and the broader EV cluster
- **MRI / medical imaging** — healthcare adjacencies (off-wiki)
- **Phosphors / catalysts** in semiconductor manufacturing — long-tail risk to fab operations

**Which means:** if China retaliates (or uses rare earths as leverage), the second-order hit isn't to [[NVDA]] / [[TSM]] directly — it's to the **power and cooling infrastructure** that scales with AI deployment. A 6-month rare earth export squeeze would tighten an already-stretched physical data center supply chain. This is a watch item, not a position recommendation. The base rate for "China actually restricts rare earths broadly" is low (they've threatened many times, executed rarely) — but it's the highest-impact tail risk on the table.

## Historical analogues

| Episode | Lesson |
|---|---|
| **Phase 1 Trade Deal (Jan 2020)** | Trump-Xi agreement on ag purchases. China underdelivered on commitments. Markets rallied on signing, faded over 6 months as headlines died down. |
| **US-Japan Plaza Accord (1985)** | Trade rebalancing through currency, not tariffs. Took decades to reshape supply chains. Reminder that summit-level deals are slow-acting. |
| **US-China Phase 2 (never happened)** | Phase 1 was supposed to be followed by Phase 2 — never materialized. Reminder that announcements ≠ implementation. |
| **TikTok ban negotiations (2024-25)** | Multi-year cycle of bans, extensions, and renegotiations with no resolution. Direct precedent for how the US-China tech-decoupling story actually unfolds. |

**Which means:** even if Scenario B materializes, the implementation timeline is multi-quarter at minimum. Don't extrapolate a one-day announcement into a structural change.

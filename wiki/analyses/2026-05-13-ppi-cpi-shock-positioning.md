# The PPI-CPI Shock (May 12-13, 2026) — Positioning Implications

> **Premise:** April CPI (released May 12) and April PPI (released May 13) both came in materially hotter than consensus, against a backdrop of oil at ~$98 / Brent ~$104 driven by the Iran-US-Israel conflict. The 8-4 Fed split that was tilting toward cuts in April has now likely flipped. This page maps the print to specific wiki positions and gives a clear "what to do this week" framework.

## TL;DR

**Yes, this is bearish for equities — but with nuance.** Long-duration tech compresses on multiple, not on earnings. The wiki's structural compounders (NVDA, TSM, ASML) hold their theses but lose rate-cut optionality. **Wait two weeks before adding** — June 10 CPI is the binary that confirms or breaks the regime shift. Specific rotation: trim leveraged-duration speculative AI ([[CRWV]] / [[NBIS]] / [[OUST]]), hold structural compounders at reduced size, add to backlog-locked industrials ([[FIX]] / [[EME]] / [[PWR]]) and rare earths ([[MP]]).

> **What this means:** Inflation came in much hotter than expected, and the Fed is unlikely to cut rates this year. For most AI/tech stocks, the earnings stories are fine but the multiples will probably compress 5-10%. The right move is to wait two weeks for the next inflation print before buying anything — and in the meantime, lean toward stocks whose backlogs are already locked in (data-center construction) or whose value goes UP with inflation (rare earths, energy).

### Why this is worse than a typical hot print

Three compounding factors:

1. **Sequencing matters.** PPI dropping one day after a hot CPI means the pipeline pressure feeding into the NEXT CPI is even worse than the realized CPI suggests. The June 10 CPI print now has a meaningfully higher floor.
2. **Core is hot, not just headline.** Energy shocks pass through headline fast — that's normal. But **core PPI re-accelerated +120 bps**. This means the oil-driven cost shock has moved past the gas pump into transportation, packaging, freight, intermediate inputs, and services. **No longer a contained energy story — it's a generalized supply-side inflation shock with second-round effects.**
3. **Fed split flips — and market pricing confirms.** The April 2026 meeting was 8-4 — biggest dissent split since October 1992 — between holding (3.50-3.75%) and *cutting*. Today's PPI removes any near-term rate-cut optionality and **CME FedWatch now prices a 37% probability of a HIKE by EOY 2026** (per CNBC, May 12). Cut probability through EOY 2027 is essentially zero — a 24-month dovish bet erased in two prints. **That's a 180-degree rotation in expected forward path, and it's already priced.**

### The Warsh wildcard (NEW)

**Kevin Warsh is the incoming Fed Chair, taking reins May 2026** (confirmed via CNBC May 12). Warsh has been outspoken in favor of cutting and aligns with President Trump's preference for an easing central bank. Now he walks into an inflation regime that demands the opposite. **This creates a Fed-credibility risk vector that did not exist 30 days ago.**

Four scenarios, ranked by probability:

| Scenario | Probability | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Warsh aligns with data → holds** | ~50% | Reluctant pause; modest credibility intact; rate-cut hopes deferred to 2027 |
| **Warsh aligns with Trump → cuts into hot inflation** | ~20% | **Fed credibility shock.** Long bonds sell off violently; dollar weakens; gold rallies; equity vol spikes. Worst outcome for the wiki's long-duration AI complex. |
| **Warsh capitulates → hikes** | ~15% | Bond rally on regained credibility; equity vol short-term but long-term constructive |
| **FOMC overrules Warsh / hawkish dissent dominates** | ~15% | Hawkish hold; communications volatility regime |

> **What this means:** Trump appointed a Chair specifically because Warsh wanted to cut. The Chair is now arriving at the worst possible moment to cut. If Warsh tries it anyway — and there's a 20% chance — bond markets stop trusting the Fed to control inflation, and long-duration assets get repriced *worse than a normal hike cycle would imply*. That's the single biggest wildcard the wiki is now exposed to.

> **What this means:** A monthly miss of 3x consensus on core PPI isn't a rounding error or a base effect. Annualized, a 1.0% monthly core print is ~12.7% annualized core PPI. The previous month was also revised UP (from 0.1% → 0.2% MoM core), so the trend was understated. This is an inflation regime, not noise.

## Honest caveats

- **PPI is volatile.** One hot print doesn't change the policy regime. Two consecutive (May PPI + June CPI) would.
- **Fed targets core PCE, not PPI.** Core PCE has historically been ~30-60 bps below core CPI. So translation to Fed policy is indirect.
- **Markets often overreact to first prints** then mean-revert as components are dissected (e.g., trade margins are volatile in PPI; healthcare can swing core).
- **The wiki is NOT advising sell-everything.** Structural theses (Blackwell demand, $725B capex, memory inflation absorbed by hyperscalers) are unchanged. This is about *positioning velocity* (don't add this week), not stance changes.

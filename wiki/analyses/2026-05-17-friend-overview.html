1. Why this matters: TSMC is becoming an AI company

TSMC is the single most important company you've never thought about. It manufactures the chips for NVDA, Apple's iPhone, AMD, Qualcomm — pretty much every chip designer that doesn't have its own factory. So when TSMC's customer mix shifts, the AI investment story shifts with it.

Three things became concrete this weekend:

  • TSMC's 2026 revenue is on track to grow 25% per year through 2029 — that's the company's own management target, much higher than the 10-15% the chip industry usually grows.
  • TSMC just committed $20 billion to its Arizona fabs — about one-third of its full-year 2026 spending going to a single US site. Big read-through to companies that build data centers in the US: FIX, EME, PWR, AMKR.
  • TSMC's CEO publicly said "Intel and Tesla are still our customers" on the Q1 call, despite their announcement to build the Texas Terafab. Translation: even the headline-stealing diversification moves aren't actually replacing TSMC at scale yet.
What this means for non-investors The AI boom has a physical-world chokepoint that everyone underestimates. It's a 60-mile-radius cluster of factories in Taiwan plus a growing US footprint in Arizona. Every dollar of AI spending eventually has to pass through this chokepoint. That's why TSMC is up so much — it's not luck, it's structurally captured rent.

5. The macro risk nobody is hedging — Bank of Japan June 16-17

This is the one most likely to surprise people. The Bank of Japan meets June 16-17 and is expected to raise rates by a quarter point (from 0.75% to 1.0%). Sounds boring. It is not.

Here's why this matters: Hedge funds and traders around the world have been borrowing yen at ~0% for years to buy high-flying things like tech stocks. This is called the yen carry trade. When BoJ raises rates, the yen strengthens, and those carry-trade positions get squeezed. Forced selling spreads to whatever those trades were funding.

In August 2024, the BoJ raised rates by 0.15% and:

  • The VIX (the "fear index") spiked 180% intraday to 65 (largest single-day spike on record)
  • The Nikkei (Japan's S&P) fell 12.4% in one day (worst since 1987)
  • Tech stocks globally got cratered for about 4 trading days before recovering

Morgan Stanley estimates ~$500 billion of yen-carry positions are still on, post-2024. Only about 10% of the trade unwound in August 2024. The other 90% is still there. If BoJ surprises hawkish in June, the 2024 episode could repeat — or be longer this time, because the US Fed is no longer about to cut rates to soften the blow.

What this means for non-investors If you're holding a lot of US tech in 401(k)s or index funds, the week of June 16-17 has unusual tail-risk. A "boring" central bank meeting in Tokyo could trigger a 5-10% pullback in NVDA, MU, the Magnificent 7, etc. Not predicting it. Just saying it's on the calendar and it's underpriced.

7. Three under-the-radar tickers worth knowing

SOITEC (SOI.PA — Paris-listed)

French company that makes a specialty type of silicon wafer used in co-packaged optics — the next-gen way of moving data inside AI data centers. They're the only company qualified to supply this material to TSMC, GlobalFoundries, and Tower Semiconductor. Every NVIDIA Spectrum-X/Quantum-X CPO switch is built on a Soitec wafer. iPhone 17 mmWave antenna modules are also on Soitec FD-SOI silicon (independently confirmed). Stock has rallied 4-6x off Dec 2025 lows so valuation is uncomfortable; earnings May 27 is binary.

XPEV (Xpeng Motors — NYSE ADR)

Chinese EV company that just committed roughly $13.7 billion to a humanoid robot program called IronMan. They're targeting 1,000+ units per month by late 2026. Stock is meaningfully cheaper than Tesla — and Tesla's 178x earnings already prices in the robot future. XPENG's robot bet is the same direction but at a fraction of the valuation. Most US investors don't know this story.

ISRG (Intuitive Surgical)

If you want exposure to "AI robotics" without speculative humanoid bets, ISRG is the only operationally-proven public play. They make the da Vinci surgical robot. Revenue grew 23% last quarter; their newest da Vinci 5 system placements grew 58% year-over-year. 86% of their revenue is recurring (instruments + services). Already profitable, structurally moated, cash-flow validated — completely different risk profile from "Tesla Optimus by 2027" speculation.

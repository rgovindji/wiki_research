## One buyer, three sellers

At 8:30 this morning the market got the exact number it spent a week positioning for. Core inflation rose 0.2% in May — below the 0.3% consensus, below the line we drew Monday night. The scariest item on June's calendar, a rate hike next week, lost most of its ammunition in one decimal point. Bond traders behaved accordingly: the 10-year Treasury yield eased to about 4.52%, and bets on a June hike were pared within minutes.

The S&P 500 then fell 1.62% to 7,266.99 and closed on its lows. The Nasdaq lost 2%. The Dow dropped 953 points, back below 50,000. Industrials fell over 3%, tech and materials over 2%. Even gold went down — on a day with missiles in the air, which tells you something we'll come back to.

What happened is that the soft number had one buyer and three sellers. Seller one: the war. Iran answered Monday night's U.S. strikes with missile and drone attacks on American bases in Jordan, Bahrain, and Kuwait, and by afternoon the President was promising to hit Iran "very hard," with power plants and bridges on the menu if there's no deal. Seller two: the fine print. As we flagged in this morning's addendum, the soft core number was hiding a hot services number underneath — more on that in the scorecard, because we graded ourselves on it. Seller three, and the newest: supply. Three of the most valuable private companies on earth — OpenAI, Anthropic, and SpaceX — are now all racing toward public listings worth a combined $3.6 trillion, roughly the GDP of France, and analysts at BNP Paribas spent the day warning that the money to buy those IPOs has to come from somewhere. The somewhere is the chip stocks everyone already owns. Nvidia fell 3%, Broadcom 4%, AMD 4%.

One number going your way doesn't help much when the selling has three engines.

## Scorecard: our first graded call is a miss

Monday night we logged this, in public, on purpose: *if core CPI prints 0.3% or below, the market looks through the ugly headline and finishes flat to up.* Core printed 0.2%. The S&P fell 1.62%. The falsifier we wrote — soft core and a drop worse than 1.5% — fired almost to the decimal. That's a wrong call, scored wrong, no asterisks bargained for. The ledger now reads 0 right, 1 wrong.

Here's the autopsy, which matters more than the score. Our call quietly assumed inflation was the only thing that could move Wednesday. It wasn't — we *wrote Monday's letter* about U.S. strikes on Iran and still keyed the whole prediction to one data print. When Iran retaliated overnight, the day acquired a second engine our conditional never accounted for. The second broken assumption: we graded the inflation report by its headline core number, when the composition was the real message. Core services excluding housing — the measure the Fed watches for home-grown inflation — rose 0.55% in May, the worst month since March 2024, and it has been accelerating since last fall. That's before the war. Goods deflation and cooling rents masked it. Our reader desk flagged this at 8:45 this morning, fifteen minutes after the print; the addendum we sent promised tonight's verdict, and the verdict is: the report was hotter than the number we traded on, and the hawks walking into next Wednesday's Fed meeting know it.

What we keep from the wreckage: half the mechanism actually worked. We said the *bond market* would look through a war-driven headline if core was tame, and it did — yields fell on the print. Equities failed for reasons bonds don't care about. The fix is now written into our playbook: on days carrying two live catalysts, key the call to price levels, not to one catalyst. The tape doesn't care which headline pushes it through a floor.

The YOLO desk's morning card gets the same honesty. The trade — buying 7,300 puts — required a *hot* core print plus a break of 7,355, and the soft print killed it at 8:31. The market then broke 7,355, broke 7,300, and closed at 7,267 anyway. The puts we didn't buy would have paid handsomely; the desk made nothing. We're at peace with that. A desk that improvises the same trade off a different excuse after its trigger fails isn't a process, it's a mood. But the lesson is the same one as above — the levels were right, the catalyst key was too narrow — and it's logged. The desk's record: 1 win, 0 losses, 1 no-trigger.

Status on the live calls: our Monday call that the index reclaims roughly 7,440 by Friday's close needs a 2.4% rally in two sessions — possible in this tape, but we're behind and we'll grade it straight if it fails. The oil-is-the-tell call got a murky test: crude rose 2% to $90.03 but stayed well under our $95 line while stocks fell anyway — either the tell missed, or today simply wasn't mainly about Iran. We grade it Monday. And Marvell closed at $252.59, two dollars and change above the $250 line where our index-inclusion call dies, with the forced buying still twelve days out.

## Oracle booked $85 billion in a quarter and fell 7% after dark

Oracle's report tonight was the cleanest test yet of the question this whole market is asking, and the answer came in two parts.

Part one: AI demand is not the problem. Oracle's contracted future revenue — the backlog of signed deals it hasn't yet delivered — jumped from $553 billion to $638 billion in a single quarter. Eighty-five billion dollars of net new commitments in thirteen weeks, almost all of it large-scale AI contracts. Cloud infrastructure revenue grew 93%. Revenue and profit both beat.

Part two: the market no longer pays for demand it has to finance on faith. Oracle spent $55.7 billion on capital equipment this fiscal year against a $50 billion plan, guided next year's outlay to roughly $70 billion, and announced it will raise about $40 billion in new debt and equity to help fund it. The stock fell as much as 7% after hours. Same pattern as Broadcom last week: a record print, sold, because the market's attention has moved from the order book to the balance sheet.

Buried in the call was the detail we found genuinely new: customers have now prepaid Oracle $75 billion toward GPU capacity, and some bring their own chips for Oracle to deploy. That's the buildout being funded by the people buying it — neither debt nor dilution — and it's the best counter-evidence yet against the borrowed-money doom loop people fear in AI infrastructure. It didn't matter tonight. In this tape, the funding plan is the guide, and a $40 billion raise was the only number after-hours traders heard.

We don't own Oracle, by choice. But we own the neighborhood — CoreWeave most directly — and tonight's reaction says the market will keep repricing AI infrastructure name by name, sorted by who needs outside money. That scrutiny is the single biggest near-term risk to our CoreWeave position, and we'd rather say so tonight than after it shows up in the price.

## The portfolio's worst day yet

Down 2.7% today against the market's 1.6% — that's what a supply-side AI portfolio does on a day the AI trade de-rates. The book now sits at $93,841, down 6.2% from our June 1 start. Marvell (−5.4%) and Broadcom (−5.1%) led the damage; Micron fell 4.7% in sympathy with a brutal night in Korea, where the memory-heavy KOSPI dropped 4.5% and Seoul's fear index printed above its 2008 financial-crisis peak.

The honest read: nothing in today's damage touched a thesis. TSMC — the company that physically manufactures the AI buildout — reported a record month this morning, up 30% from a year ago. Oracle just showed demand growing $85 billion a quarter. Nvidia signed a multi-year memory pact with SK Hynix days ago. Every fundamental needle we track pointed up this week while every price went down. That divergence is what flow-driven selling looks like: crowded positioning unwinding, IPO supply looming, war premium. It can absolutely run further — flows don't stop at fair value — but it's a different disease than demand rolling over, and it gets treated differently: we hold, and we get interested in adding, not less.

On the cash: we're sitting on $25,000 we promised to deploy into a 5–10% pullback. The S&P is now a bit more than 4% off its high; the 5% mark lands around 7,200, only sixty-some points below tonight's close. If the market hands us the 7,150–7,200 zone — where that 5% mark, an old price shelf, and the 50-day average all stack up — while oil stays under $95 and the demand data keeps printing like it did this week, the first tranche of that cash goes to work. We said we'd name it before the moment came; that's the shopping list and the trigger.

## One concept worth knowing

**Supply of paper.** Stocks don't only move on earnings and rates — they move on how much *stock* there is to absorb. When OpenAI, Anthropic, and SpaceX list at a combined $3.6 trillion over the next few months, the pension funds and index managers who must own them can't conjure new money; they sell something they already hold to make room. The likeliest sale candidates are the most-owned, most-profitable positions in the same theme — which is a precise description of Nvidia and the rest of the AI complex. This is why a stock can fall while its business sets records: the seller isn't disagreeing with the thesis, he's funding a different trade. It also has a flip side worth remembering — IPO supply is a one-time digestion problem, not a verdict on demand, and markets have historically absorbed even giant listings within a couple of quarters.

## The bottom line

Today the market told us the price of being right about the wrong variable. Core inflation came in soft, exactly as our call required, and it bought about ninety seconds of green futures. The war, the services-inflation trend underneath the soft number, and three trillion dollars of incoming IPO paper did the rest. Our first graded call is a miss, the autopsy is above, and the fix — levels over catalysts — is already in the playbook.

What didn't change today: every demand number in the AI economy that printed this week printed at a record. What did change: the price of admission. We're down 6.2% from inception, our shopping list now has a level on it, and the floor under this market has a name — 7,265 — that we'll all get to watch tested at 9:30 tomorrow.

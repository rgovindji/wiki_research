## The setup

Yesterday Kevin Warsh's first dot plot put a December rate hike back on the table and flushed the S&P through 7,510 — the shelf we'd said would hold — to close at 7,420.10, down 1.21%. This morning the buyers are back. As of early this morning the futures point higher across the board: the S&P-tracking SPY is up about 0.71% and the Nasdaq-tracking QQQ up about 1.59% in pre-market, a tech-led bounce that would open the cash index somewhere around **7,470** — call it forty points under the level that broke. One prediction-market contract was pricing a 98% chance the index simply opens green.

Here's the part that matters before the bell. The thing punished hardest yesterday was the most rate-sensitive corner of tech — the long-duration mega-caps whose value sits furthest in the future, marked down when the cost of money looks like it's going up. This morning those same names are leading the rebound, with the 10-year Treasury yield easing back about three basis points to 4.48%. That's the duration trade whipsawing for the third straight session: chips down / mega-caps up on Monday, the mirror on Wednesday, and now the mega-caps snapping back as yields blink lower. When a single factor flips the leadership three days running, that's not conviction — it's a market being shoved around by the bond tape.

So the open is a relief rally with a specific job to do. To turn yesterday into a one-day event, the buyers have to climb back over 7,510 and *close* there. And they have to do it without the machinery that normally helps. The dealer positioning that quietly buys dips drained roughly 80% last week and never re-stacked — net positioning sat near +$22 billion into yesterday's break, a fraction of the +$80-100 billion that pinned the tape the week before. Translation: this bounce is real money buying the dip, not the automatic dealer-hedging tailwind that powered the grinds higher in early June. That makes it faster and less reliable in both directions.

## On the line today

Last night we drew a line: **the S&P does not close above 7,510 on Thursday or Friday.** The reasoning was mechanical — with the protective positioning gone there's no automatic dip-buying to mean-revert the market up, and the hawkish dots removed the dovish surprise that could carry it there. This morning's pop is the first real test, and it's an honest one: an open near 7,470 leaves 7,510 within a single push. We're wrong the moment any close prints above it — that would say a Fed speaker walked back the dots or the dip-buyers are simply stronger than a thin tape implies, and we'd take the loss and re-rate the regime back toward "bull." Watch the reaction *at* 7,510, not the open. A poke that gets sold is the floor-becomes-ceiling confirmation; a clean close above and our week's read is wrong.

## Nvidia and SK Hynix Wire the Memory Layer Shut

Overnight, Nvidia and SK Hynix announced a multiyear partnership to co-develop next-generation memory for what Jensen Huang keeps calling the "AI factory" — the high-bandwidth memory that sits next to every AI accelerator and feeds it data. On its own it's one press release. Stacked against the Taiwan Semi-Amkor packaging deal we flagged yesterday — ten years of advanced chip assembly locked onto US soil — it's a pattern: the supply side of AI is signing decade-long contracts and welding its supply chain together, name by name.

Why this matters for what we own. Memory is the single tightest link in the AI build right now; the makers — Micron here, SK Hynix and Samsung in Asia — have spent the year as the leverage play on accelerator demand. SK Hynix has roughly quadrupled off last year's lows. A multiyear Nvidia tie-up is exactly the kind of demand-visibility that turns a cyclical memory maker into a structural one, and it lands six days before Micron reports its own quarter. **Our take:** this is supply-side AI doing what supply-side AI has done all month — digging in while the platforms that fund it get asked harder questions. We're heavy the picks-and-shovels for precisely this reason, and nothing overnight argues against it.

## The Whipsaw Inside Tech

The more uncomfortable story is the one playing out in our own book day to day. Monday the chips dumped and Alphabet rose. Wednesday chips rose and Alphabet fell. This morning the mega-caps lead again. That's a duration tug-of-war: when yields jump, the longest-dated growth stories (Alphabet, Microsoft, Meta) get marked down first; when yields ease, they snap back hardest. The near-term-order-book names — Broadcom, up about 2.3% pre-market, plus Vertiv, Marvell, Micron — trade on their own cycle and have been the steadier hold through it.

We made a call Tuesday that Monday's chip dump was a one-day trim, not a leadership change, and it grades tomorrow. Day one went our way hard; this morning's mega-cap-led bounce muddies it slightly, because if the rotation were truly structural the chips wouldn't need the mega-caps to lead the rebound. **Our take:** the leadership noise is the bond market's, not a verdict on AI demand — no negative demand datapoint has landed in either direction. We hold the supply side and let the daily duration chop wash through.

## The game plan

The whole day organizes around one number: **7,510**, yesterday's broken floor, now the ceiling the bounce has to clear. The structure flipped — what held us up Tuesday is what caps us today. Below, the first shelf is **7,400-7,415**, where yesterday's selling came to rest and fresh hedges have stacked. Beneath that, around **7,352**, is the line where the plumbing genuinely changes character: lose it on a closing basis and the big dealers stop cushioning moves and start adding to them, which is how an orderly down day becomes a disorderly one.

The honest caveat: with positioning this thin, none of these levels is the strong magnet it was last week. Walls are only as powerful as the hedging behind them, and that hedging has drained — so treat 7,510 and 7,400 as reference points the tape can slice through, not guardrails. The mechanism today isn't dealer gravity; it's whether real-money buyers can carry the index back over a level it just lost with no automatic help and a Fed that just removed the reason to.

## The YOLO desk

Paper trade, lottery sizing — money you can lose entirely, sized accordingly.

**Structure:** SPX 0DTE 7,450 put.
**Trigger:** the trade doesn't exist until SPX rallies into the 7,500-7,510 zone — yesterday's broken floor — in the first ninety minutes *and* fails to hold it (a 15-30 minute rejection, not a clean break). No poke into 7,500+ means no trade and the desk stands aside; a clean hold above 7,510 also means stand aside — that's our own no-reclaim call breaking, and we don't fight it.
**Target:** the 7,400-7,415 shelf where yesterday's selling rested. Exit there.
**Invalidation:** SPX holds above 7,510 for ~30 minutes after the poke — the reclaim is real, out, no bearish re-entry. Hard time-stop at 3:30 ET if neither target nor invalidation has hit, because in a thin tape theta resolves "almost" against the holder.

The logic is our own house thesis wearing an options ticket: a relief gap into a level that just broke, with no dealer dip-buying engine behind it and Fed speakers more likely to reinforce the hawkish dots than walk them back, is the textbook spot for the bounce to get sold at resistance. The thin-gamma caveat cuts both ways — there's no strong wall to reject the poke, so if buyers do clear 7,510 cleanly this card never triggers, and that's the right outcome, not a missed one.

If the open just rips through 7,510 and holds, the desk is flat and our week's read is on the clock. Either way, the level tells the story. We'll see you after the bell.

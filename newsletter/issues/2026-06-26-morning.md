The market spent a month treating the AI build-out's costs as an accounting detail — a line item on Apple's bill, a dent in Dell's margins. This morning it started treating them as a question about the whole thing. When the company at the literal center of the boom is reportedly thinking about *not* going public yet because investors are asking whether the returns will ever justify the spend, that is a different kind of news than a memory price hike.

And it lands at the worst possible spot on the chart. We close at 7,357 and futures point to an open right on the 7,323–7,350 shelf that cracked intraday yesterday. The floor and the fear are arriving together.

## OpenAI Blinks

Here's what happened. The New York Times reports OpenAI is considering delaying its IPO — once floated for late this year — into 2027, after a stretch of tech-stock volatility spooked its advisers. The numbers underneath explain the nerves: OpenAI has committed to roughly **$1.4 trillion** in data-center and infrastructure spending, expects to lose about **$14 billion this year**, and doesn't forecast profit until 2030. Altman is reportedly holding firm on a $1 trillion valuation — above the ~$730–850B of its last private round.

Translation: the single most important demand source in the entire AI trade just signaled that public markets might not pay its price right now. That's the question that's been lurking under every capex headline made explicit — can the revenue ever catch the spend? SpaceX, the other marquee private name, has already cracked from $202 to $153 since its debut.

**Our take:** this is the capex-unwind worry we've been circling, finally given a face. It doesn't break the supply thesis — Micron's order book is contracted years out and doesn't care about OpenAI's IPO timing. But sentiment is the marginal price-setter below the gamma flip, and sentiment just got a real scare. Own the contracted suppliers; expect the *unprofitable-promise* names (the pre-revenue AI complex) to take the brunt.

## The chips give it back

The cleaner tell is in our own book. Micron ripped 15.7% yesterday on a genuinely re-rating guide — and it's down ~5% this morning anyway, with Marvell, Arm and Intel red beside it. Nothing changed in Micron's contracts overnight. What changed is that "AI demand" stopped being an assumption and became a debate, and the highest-beta expression of that assumption sells first. We stay long the suppliers, but a one-day round-trip on a blowout print is exactly the kind of tape that says positioning, not fundamentals, runs the next few sessions.

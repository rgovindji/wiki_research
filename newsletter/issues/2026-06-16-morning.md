Markets aren't bracing for the rate — a hold at 3.50–3.75% is as close to certain as these things get, roughly 97% priced. They're bracing for the *voice*. More on that below.

## On the line today

The Bank of Japan resolved one of our open calls overnight, and the early read is going our way. We said Monday that the BoJ's near-certain hike was a sell-the-fact non-event for US tech, **not** the yen-carry unwind that's been the market's favorite tail risk for two years. The mechanism: a fully-priced hike needs a *surprise* to break the yen, and the base case wasn't a surprise. Tokyo delivered exactly the base case — a 25bp hike to 1.0%, the highest since 1995, on a 7–1 vote with one dovish dissent. The yen barely flinched (dollar/yen hung near 160, a hair firmer), JGB yields ticked up three basis points, and the Nikkei closed up 0.46%. No spike, no squeeze, no contagion into the cheap-yen borrowing that funds half the global tech trade. The call formally grades Friday — it's wrong only if the Nasdaq drops more than 2% on a sharp yen move in the next two sessions — but the decisive input has landed, and it landed quiet.

## The first Warsh Fed

For four years the Fed-day game was reading Jerome Powell. Tomorrow there's a new man at the table, and the market is repricing what the institution *sounds like*. Warsh is a former governor with a hawkish reputation, installed by a president who has been loudly demanding cuts — a tension that makes his first press conference the real event, not the rate line.

The mechanics worth understanding: the Fed has been carrying an easing *bias* — language that says the next move is more likely down than up. The widely-expected shift tomorrow is to drop that bias and go neutral, which sounds like nothing and is actually a hawkish tilt. With oil cracked to $80 the doves finally have cover to cut; with services inflation still running hot (Monday's supercore was the worst since early 2024) the hawks have cover to wait. Warsh inherits both arguments on day one. The tell we're watching is the dot plot and where year-end hike odds settle — futures are already pricing roughly an 80% chance of a *hike* by December, not a cut.

That's why this is on our scorecard. Last week we called the June hold "hawkish hold" — rates steady, but December-hike odds staying above 50% through the meeting. The 80% the market's carrying into the room is the bond market agreeing with us a day early. The risk to the call isn't the hold; it's Warsh sounding dovish enough in the presser to collapse those odds. We'll know tomorrow at 2 PM ET.

## The game plan

Two lines, one question. The ceiling is **7,555** — the wall the close pinned to. In a tape where dealers are as long "gamma" as they've been all cycle, that's the price where they *sell* every push higher to stay hedged, so a gap into the 7,590–7,600 record zone this morning is most likely to get leaned on, not extended. To make a real new high, that wall has to migrate up first, and walls move on time or a fresh catalyst — and the only catalyst on the board is a Fed chair more likely to inject caution than fuel. The floor that matters is **7,440**: lose it and those same dealers stop cushioning dips and start adding to them, the way they did the whole first week of June. Between the two is a pinned, range-bound grind — which is the base case into tomorrow's decision.

## The YOLO desk

The desk has lost twice in a row making the identical mistake — buying the melt-up and setting the exit one wall too far. So today we flip it. **The structure: an SPX 0DTE 7,575 put.** The trigger: the trade doesn't exist unless SPX pokes the 7,590–7,600 record zone in the first hour *and fails to hold it* — a rejection at the wall, not a break through it. The target is the **7,555 pin**, because in a positive-gamma tape the wall is a magnet on the way back down just as it is a ceiling on the way up, and that's where dealer hedging flips from headwind to tailwind for this trade — exit there. The invalidation: SPX holds above 7,600 for thirty minutes (the wall migrated, the breakout's real — stand aside, no fighting it), and a hard time-stop at 3:30 ET if neither hits, because theta resolves "almost" against the holder every time. The reasoning in one line: fading a record-poke into a hawkish-Warsh Fed is the war game, where chasing it was the mistake. Money you can lose entirely, sized like a lottery ticket with better math.

The wall above, the trapdoor below, a new chair tomorrow. We'll see you after the bell.

We said last night the fireworks were Wednesday and Thursday — Micron's print and the inflation number. The market didn't wait. Overnight, the global memory trade came apart: South Korea's Kospi closed down 10%, tripping a 20-minute trading halt (the fourth this year), with Samsung and SK Hynix — together half the index — both down more than 12%. ASML fell over 5% in Europe. As of early this morning, S&P futures are off about 1.3% and Nasdaq futures down roughly 2.6%, which would put the S&P near 7,370–7,380 at the open — below 7,446.

That 7,446 number is the whole story before the bell. It's the line where the big options dealers stop cushioning declines and start adding to them. For three weeks this market kept its falls shallow because dealers leaned against every move — but that cushion was thin going into last night, and a thin cushion is exactly what a real catalyst slices through. Last week we learned that lesson twice from the upside; this morning the tape tests whether we wrote it down. Below 7,446 there's no automatic buyer underneath the market, and the next shelves are 7,400, then 7,352, then 7,300.

So the one thing that matters at the open: whether this is an overnight panic that gets bought back above 7,446 in the first hour, or a flush the market's own plumbing accelerates. The catalyst is real enough that we're not assuming the bounce.

## A Famous Bear Pokes the AI Trade, and the Whole World Sells Memory

The selling didn't start with a bad earnings report or a Fed surprise — it started with positioning and a microphone. Dan Niles, a long-time tech investor with a following, said publicly he'd cut his chip holdings and warned the AI trade faces a near-term "speed bump." His worry is the one lurking under this whole rally: whether the hyperscalers spending hundreds of billions on AI infrastructure can actually earn a return on it, especially as companies route more work to cheaper models to save money. Add a cautious AI-chip note from Broadcom and fresh higher-for-longer rate worry, and a market at record highs after a 40%-in-two-weeks memory melt-up had every excuse to take profits at once.

Why it cascaded: memory is the most crowded trade in the world right now, concentrated in three names — Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron. When the marginal seller shows up in a trade that crowded, there's no one on the other side, so it gaps rather than drifts. Korea, where two of those names *are* the index, took the full hit.

**Our take:** This is the first real downside test of the bet this letter is built on — that the chipmakers beat the platforms. For three weeks the supply side led up; this morning it's leading down, which is what a crowded trade does when it unwinds. A valuation reset is not a broken thesis — the memory shortage is physical and multi-year, and nothing overnight changed a single signed contract. But "the shortage is real" and "the stock can fall 15% from a record" are both true at once, and today that distinction gets expensive. We own the complex. We're watching, not flinching — and glad we held a quarter of the book in cash.

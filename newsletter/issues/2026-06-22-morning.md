Markets were dark Friday for Juneteenth, so this is the first session in five days — and the tape picks up exactly where it set down. As of early this morning, U.S. stock futures are roughly flat with a mild defensive lean: the S&P and Nasdaq are hovering near unchanged, Dow futures a touch soft, and the only number with any pulse is the fear gauge — the VIX is up about 6% to around 17.4. Nothing in that is a scare. It's the market putting a little insurance back on ahead of a week with two real tests in it.

Here's the one thing to hold in your head before the open: the S&P closed Thursday at 7,500.58, pinned to the wall of hedges at 7,500 and stuck just *under* 7,510 — the level it leaned on all last week as a floor, lost on the hawkish Fed, and has not been able to climb back over since. We reopen at that exact line. Above a close over 7,510, the whole "broken floor" story is finished and the path back toward the June record near 7,600 reopens. Below it, the bounce is still just a bounce. Five days off changed nothing about the math.

The one geopolitical thread that ran all month is now tied off. The U.S.–Iran deal got signed — electronically, before the planned Switzerland ceremony, which was scrapped — as a 60-day framework rather than a final peace. The market had already paid for it: crude is down near $75 and still sliding, the war premium fully drained. That's the lesson that earned its keep this month, working in reverse — oil told you the peace was real long before any signature did. Worth one note of caution: a 60-day ceasefire is a clock, not a conclusion. But that clock is a story for August, not for this morning.

## Micron's Report Card Lands Wednesday

The biggest single-name event we hold all week is **Micron's earnings Wednesday after the close** — and it's the cleanest test yet of whether the memory boom is a durable franchise or a cycle that's run ahead of itself. The setup: company guidance points to roughly $33.5 billion in revenue at an 81% gross margin, with the entire 2026 output of high-bandwidth memory — the stacked DRAM that feeds an AI accelerator — already sold under signed price-and-volume contracts. Margins like that used to be unheard of in memory, a business that historically earned 30–40 cents on the dollar; Micron is now keeping 80.

Here's the part that makes Wednesday genuinely live rather than a victory lap: Wall Street's revenue estimates run from about $33.7 billion all the way to $40.9 billion. A $7 billion spread on a single quarter is the Street openly admitting it has no idea how fast this is accelerating. That gap is the risk in both directions — a strong guide and the whole AI-hardware book (which we own through MU, plus the read-through to Marvell, Taiwan Semi, Astera) gets another leg; a cautious word on 2027 pricing, with Samsung already pushing next-gen memory toward volume, and the most crowded trade in the market trims hard.

**Our take:** We own the memory complex precisely because it trades on order books, not the Fed's dot plot — and two sessions running before the break, that's exactly how it behaved. We're not adding into the print. The position is the position; Wednesday tells us whether to be glad we held it.

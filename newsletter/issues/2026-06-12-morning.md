The largest IPO in stock market history starts trading this morning — SpaceX, ticker SPCX, $135 a share, a $1.77 trillion valuation that makes it bigger than Tesla on day one — and it picked the same Friday the US-Iran war might formally end. Either of those would normally own the tape alone. Together they make today a test of how much good news one market can absorb at once.

The deal first, because everything else is priced on it. Trump says the signing could come this weekend in Europe; a draft of the 14-point document reportedly commits Iran to reopening the Strait of Hormuz within 30 days in exchange for oil sanctions relief. Overnight, Iranian state-affiliated media pushed back — Fars says Tehran hasn't approved any draft text — and US forces shot down two Iranian drones near the strait. The market read all of that as noise on the way to a signature: oil traded below $86 overnight (touching roughly $83 at the low), the 10-year yield eased to about 4.53%, and as of early this morning S&P futures were up between 0.2% and 0.6% depending on when you looked — which, off yesterday's 7,394.30 close, points the open somewhere between 7,410 and 7,440. Keep that last number in your head. It matters to us specifically today.

## On the line at 4 PM: our 7,440 call

Tuesday night we made a conditional call: if Wednesday's core inflation print came in tame — it did, 0.2% — the S&P would close back above roughly 7,440 by today, the level where the big market-making desks flip from amplifying every move to leaning against them. Today is the deadline, and the math is uncomfortably precise: the index needs +0.6% from yesterday's close, futures point almost exactly there, and two obstacles sit in the path — the 7,395 shelf where the market stalled yesterday, and the 7,424 flip itself. If we get the close above 7,440, the calmer, pinned market we predicted into next Friday's giant options expiry is confirmed. If the index spends the day knocking on the ceiling and closes under it, we were wrong — either dip-buyers are weaker than we believed or the framework needs humbling — and tonight's letter will say which. No partial credit for 7,439.

## $75 billion leaves the market and buys a rocket

Here's the part of the SpaceX debut that matters beyond the spectacle: the company is selling 555.6 million new shares to raise about $75 billion in cash. That money comes from somewhere — funds selling other things to make room. Two days ago, when stocks had their worst session of the month, one of the named culprits was the $3.6 trillion pipeline of private AI giants headed for public markets. Today the first and biggest ship in that pipeline actually launches, with xAI folded inside it since February's merger — so this is also, quietly, the largest AI listing ever.

The tell to watch is the close against $135. This was a fixed-price offering, not a bank-built book engineered for a first-day pop. A strong close says public markets can swallow $75 billion of supply and ask for seconds — which de-fangs the pipeline fear for the names behind it. A close below $135 says the market's wallet is thinner than its enthusiasm, and that's a real data point against the whole tape. The spillover is already trading: EchoStar, which sold spectrum to SpaceX partly for stock, is up almost 6% pre-market as the public proxy, and Rocket Lab is up more than 6% on news it joins the Nasdaq-100 on June 22 — the same day Marvell joins the S&P 500. Index doors are opening all over the new space economy at once.

## Korea went vertical. Then the banks raised the rent.

Asia spent the night celebrating the peace: Korea's KOSPI opened up 6.4%, ran as high as 8.6%, and closed up 4.6%; Japan's Nikkei gained 2.8%. But the fade from the highs is the story. SK Hynix went from up 9% to under 4%, Samsung from up 12% to under 8% — reportedly because Wall Street banks including Citi and JPMorgan are tightening the leverage they extend to hedge funds betting on Samsung, SK Hynix, and TSMC. In plain English: the lenders financing the memory trade are demanding more collateral for the same position, which forces trimming and caps how fast a crowded trade can run. When prime brokers pull back before the fundamentals do, the trade isn't wrong — it's crowded, and crowded trades fall harder on bad news. We own that crowd through Micron, which reports in under two weeks at a trillion-dollar valuation; the margin clamp is one more reason the print has to be excellent rather than good. Meanwhile ASML traded at record highs in Amsterdam early this morning — Europe's most valuable company is now a chip-equipment maker, which tells you where the world thinks the decade's profits live.

One more from our board: Adobe is down another 6.8% pre-market, near $204 — the operating-margin miss (44% versus 44.5% expected) giving sellers a second reason after the CFO's exit to Marvell. Last night we drew a line: any close above $233.38 by next Friday means the software de-rate was positioning, not regime. Pre-market has the stock 14% below that line. Nothing grades today, but the reclaim is getting steeper by the session.

## The game plan

Two numbers run today: 7,395 and 7,424. The first is the biggest shelf of sold call options in the market — the desks that sold those calls lean against gains there, which is why yesterday's rally died at 7,394.30, one point underneath it. The second is where dealer hedging flips from amplifying moves to dampening them. Punch through both and hold, and this becomes a calmer market that grinds rather than lurches — and our 7,440 call likely gets paid on top. Stall under 7,395 again and we're still in the twitchy range, with the first real floor at the 7,300 hedging shelf below. One honest caveat: everything above those numbers is collateralized by a signature that doesn't exist yet, and anyone carrying risk through the weekend is carrying the signing binary whether they mean to or not.

## The YOLO desk

The desk is flat today. The reason is the mechanism itself: every trade we've carded this month was built on an amplified tape — dealers short gamma, chasing moves and making them bigger. As of last night that fuel is gone; near-dated dealer positioning turned positive for the first time since June 4, meaning intraday flows now lean *against* moves. On a pinned Friday, a same-day option is mostly a donation to the people we used to trade against, and the one true binary on the board — the signature — trades on Sunday, when today's option is already dust. Flat is a position. The record stays 1 win, 1 loss, 1 no-trigger, and we'd rather keep it honest than keep it busy.

Tonight's letter grades the 7,440 call, scores SpaceX's first close, and — if the weekend cooperates — covers the end of a war. See you after the bell.

## The day the war premium got refunded

For two weeks this market traded a deal it didn't have. Today it got one. Late Sunday President Trump posted that the agreement with Iran is "now complete" — the blockade lifts, the Strait of Hormuz reopens, oil "will flow" once the papers are signed Friday in Switzerland. The shooting that opened June is, for our purposes, over.

The tape did exactly what a refund looks like. The S&P rose 1.65% to 7,554.29. The Nasdaq, where the money actually wanted to be, jumped 3.07% to 26,683.94. Oil went the other way, cracking 4.8% to $80.75 — its lowest since early March — because the war premium baked into every barrel all month just got handed back. The 10-year eased to about 4.42%, and the fear gauge, the VIX, fell to 16.2, a level it hasn't closed at since the early-June selloff began. Overnight, Asia didn't tiptoe: Japan's Nikkei closed up 5% to a record above 69,000 and Korea's Kospi jumped 5.2%, dragging the two memory giants — SK Hynix +6.4%, Samsung +4.5% — straight up with it.

Here's the part that should stick with you, though, because it's the difference between a good day and a clean breakout. We opened the morning brief asking one question: can a peace this thoroughly pre-paid still pay? The honest answer the tape gave is *yes, but with a ceiling.* The gap blew clean through the wall of sold call options at 7,425–7,430 that capped every rally last week — and then the index climbed to 7,577, kissed a level that's been quietly reforming overhead at about 7,555, and slid right back to close at 7,554. Pinned. To the dollar. A 1.65% headline gain that traveled only 0.81% from its low to its high. That is not a market running free toward a record. That is a market on a leash held by the same option-dealer mechanics that have been damping every move for a week — and today, for the first time, that leash caught the *upside.*

## The scorecard: one clean win, one familiar miss

We had two calls on the line today, and they cut in opposite directions.

The win is one we're going to keep. Back on June 9 we argued that this market trades the *oil*, not the war headline — that when crude and the front page disagree, you trust crude. It got tested about as hard as a thesis can get: Iran literally declared the strait closed on June 11 and oil *fell*; Tehran disputed the deal text on June 12 and oil *fell* again. Four straight sessions, the bearish headlines as loud as they come, and crude graded every one of them as noise. Today the deal landed, oil cracked to $80, stocks ripped, and the call resolved right. We're promoting it from a working theory to a standing rule of the desk: on a geopolitics headline, look at the oil tape before you trade the stock move. One honest bound — this held in a market that *wanted* the deal. The rule is "when oil and the headline disagree, trust oil," not "oil predicts everything." Trust the tell; don't deify it.

The miss is one we've now made twice, the same way. The YOLO desk's paper trade — a lottery-ticket call betting the gap would melt up into the record — triggered exactly as drawn. The squeeze ran 60 points off the open and tagged 7,577.92, three points past our 7,575 strike. And then it stalled at that 7,555 ceiling and faded, closing below the strike, worthless by the bell. Sound familiar? It should — it's June 11 almost beat for beat: direction right, trigger right, up nicely intraday, dead short of the target. The lesson this time is sharper than "walls are magnets." It's that we set the *target* (the 7,590–7,600 record zone) *above* the wall the regime was always going to defend. When dealers are this long gamma, they *sell* into the call wall. A trade whose payoff requires closing above the ceiling the market is leaning on isn't a long shot — it's a contradiction. The desk is now 1-2-1, and both losses are the same trade: buy the squeeze, set the exit one wall too far. Noted, in writing, so the third one doesn't happen.

One more, quietly: our call that this would be a *calm* week as long as sessions open above the flip got its first real exam today and passed — a 0.81% range on a 1.65% day is the positive-gamma pin behaving exactly as advertised. Three more sessions to go, including the Fed.

## Memory gets its all-clear — for a day

The loudest winners in our book today were the two memory-adjacent names, and the story behind them is more interesting than "stocks went up."

Micron ripped 10.8% to $1,087.99 — the single best mover we own. Marvell tacked on 10.4% to $308.88. Just Friday, Micron had *faded* from $1,012 to $981 on reports that prime brokers were quietly tightening the leverage they'd extend against the crowded Korea-memory trade — the plumbing that decides how fast a popular position can run, regardless of the fundamentals. Today that same trade was the loudest winner on the planet.

Here's what matters, because it's easy to misread a 10% candle as an all-clear: the peace rally and the leverage clamp answer two completely different questions, and only one got answered. Peace fixes the *mood.* It does nothing about whether the people who lend against this trade think it's too crowded heading into Micron's earnings on June 24, when management's own guidance — $33.5 billion in revenue, 81% gross margins — meets a wall-of-the-Street price target of $1,250 and has to actually print. A gap-up lets a crowded position *breathe.* It doesn't *un-crowd* it. Enjoy the mark; watch the leverage report into the print, not the one-day Kospi candle.

There was a smaller, quieter signal worth your attention too. The companies actually *selling* AI compute — the labs — are now raising prices to *ration* demand rather than cutting them to fill capacity. Reports this week put Anthropic's lease of Elon Musk's "Colossus" supercomputer at roughly $16 billion a year — a single rental contract the size of SpaceX's entire 2025 revenue — and the labs are ending their all-you-can-eat token plans to throttle usage. That's not what the top of a glut looks like. It's a small data point against the loudest bear story in this whole cycle — that everyone is building data centers ahead of demand that won't show up. When the operators are charging more to slow you down, the demand showed up.

## How the day treated us

A green tape across the board lifted the book to roughly $100,700 of notional — call it flat-plus since inception, which understates a good day because a quarter of the portfolio is sitting in cash earning nothing while the other three-quarters ran.

Micron was the star (+10.8%), and it's now up 21% from where we bought it. Marvell (+10.4%) is our biggest winner of all, up 41% since June 1 and riding into its S&P 500 induction next Monday. Astera Labs (+6.0%) and CoreWeave (+6.1%) — our two highest-beta names — did what high-beta names do on a risk-on day. Even the laggards participated: Broadcom and Dell, both still underwater for us, gained 3%-plus.

Nothing today challenged a thesis; a rising tide doesn't test anything. The cash question is the live one. We've held 25% in reserve precisely for a pullback that would let us add to the names we like at better prices — and today the market moved *away* from that entry, not toward it. We're comfortable with that. The reserve is insurance, and insurance feels expensive right up until the week you need it. With the index pinned a hair below a record and three binary events stacked into the next five days, this is not the week to spend it chasing. If the Friday signing slips and the tape gaps back toward the 7,440 area where the dealers' grip loosens, *that's* the conversation.

The whole map now comes down to one ceiling and one question. The ceiling is **7,555** — the shelf of sold call options the close pinned to almost exactly. In a market where the big dealers are as long "gamma" as they've been all cycle, that wall is where they *sell* every push higher to stay hedged, which means the record at 7,600 isn't a magnet pulling us up — it's on the far side of a wall the machinery is actively defending. To make a new high, that wall has to move up first, and walls move on time or on a fresh catalyst. Below, the floor that matters is **7,440**: lose it and the same dealers who've been cushioning every dip stop absorbing and start adding to the move, the way they did the whole first week of June. Between those two lines is a pinned, range-bound tape — which is exactly what a 0.81% range on a 1.65% day already told you.

The catalysts don't obviously supply rally fuel. The Bank of Japan almost certainly raises rates tomorrow for the first time to 1% — but it's so widely expected that the risk isn't the hike, it's the *yen*: if Japan's currency spikes, it squeezes the cheap-yen borrowing that quietly funds a lot of global tech. Wednesday the Federal Reserve almost certainly holds, with cratering oil handing the doves a louder microphone. Both are more likely to inject caution than euphoria. So our call into Friday's expiry: **the same positive-gamma machinery that capped the panic caps the joy — the S&P does not close above 7,600 before Friday.** We'll be wrong if the wall migrates up faster than the mechanics suggest, and we'll say so. We're also logging a second, calmer take: **the BoJ hike is a sell-the-fact non-event for US tech, not the long-feared yen-unwind trigger** — wrong only if the yen spikes hard enough to drag the Nasdaq down more than 2% in two sessions.

## One concept worth knowing

Why did a 1.65% up day feel so contained — and how can a "ceiling" exist in an options market at all? It comes down to who's on the other side of the trade. The big dealers who sell options have to *hedge* — buy or sell the underlying index to stay neutral. When they're "long gamma," that hedging runs *against* the move: they sell as the market rises and buy as it falls, which mechanically squeezes the daily range. Right now they're long gamma in size, and the strike where they've sold the most calls — today, 7,555 — is where that selling pressure concentrates. So the market climbs to the wall, the hedging selling kicks in, and it stalls. It's not magic and it's not manipulation; it's a few hundred desks doing the same defensive math at the same price. The practical takeaway: in a tape like this, the wall above is a real ceiling and the flip below is a real trapdoor, and the boring middle is the base case until one of them breaks.

## The bottom line

Peace arrived, oil got cheap, and the riskiest corners of our book had their best day in weeks — and the market still couldn't take out a record it's sitting half a percent below, because the same plumbing that's protected us on the way down is now leaning on us from above. That's not a complaint. A market that caps its own euphoria is a healthier one than a market that melts up unhedged, and we'd rather own this tape than that one. The deal isn't signed until Friday, the Fed speaks Wednesday, and a quarter of our powder is dry. We're content to be pinned, paid, and patient.

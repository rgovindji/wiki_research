## The setup

The thing the market spent two weeks front-running finally happened. Late Sunday, President Trump posted that the deal with Iran is "now complete" — the naval blockade lifts, the Strait of Hormuz reopens, and the formal signing is set for Friday in Switzerland. The framework extends the ceasefire 60 days and buys time to sort out the harder nuclear questions later. The shooting war that opened this month is, for market purposes, over.

The reaction is exactly what you'd draw up. S&P futures are up about 1.3% and Nasdaq futures about 2.1% as of early this morning; oil did the opposite, cracking roughly 5.6% to around $80 — an eight-week low extended, because the Hormuz premium baked into crude all month just got refunded. The 10-year eased to about 4.42%. And Asia didn't tiptoe: Japan's Nikkei closed up 5% to a record above 69,000, Korea's Kospi jumped 5.2%, and the two names that matter most to our book ripped — SK Hynix +6.4%, Samsung +4.5%. That's the same Korea memory trade prime brokers were reportedly clamping leverage on Friday. One peace headline and the clamp is forgotten.

Here's the one thing that matters before the open. Friday we closed at 7,431, pinned almost exactly on the biggest shelf of sold calls in the market — roughly 7,425–7,430, the ceiling that capped every rally last week. A 1.3% gap opens the S&P somewhere near 7,520–7,530, which is *above* that ceiling and above Friday's 7,456 high. Punch through the call wall and there's almost no price memory between here and the June 1 record at 7,599.96. The pin that made last week feel heavy becomes, on a gap like this, an air pocket. The only real question this morning isn't whether peace is good — it's whether a peace this thoroughly pre-paid can still pay, or whether "complete" plus a Friday signing is the textbook setup for buy-the-rumor, sell-the-news.

## Korea's memory trade gets its all-clear — for a day

Friday the worst tell in our book was Micron fading from $1,012 to $981.61 on reports that prime brokers were tightening leverage on the crowded Korea-memory trade — plumbing that changes no number in Micron's model but caps how fast a crowded position runs. This morning that same trade is the loudest winner on the planet, SK Hynix +6.4% and Samsung +4.5% to records.

Here's the part that matters: the leverage clamp and the peace rally answer two different questions, and only one got answered. Peace fixes the *macro* mood; it does nothing about whether prime brokers think the memory crowd is too levered into a $1,250-price-target stock that reports in nine days. A gap-up open lets a crowded position breathe — it doesn't un-crowd it. Our take: enjoy the Micron mark this morning, but the Friday tightening report is still the thing to watch into the June 24 print, not the overnight Kospi candle. Crowds exhale on green days and get margin calls on red ones; Sunday's deal changes none of that math. (SPCX, for what it's worth, is around $171 on day two — well clear of the $135 line we logged it against, and the IPO-supply fear that helped crack June 10 is, two sessions on, sleeping.)

## The game plan

Last week's map was about a ceiling; this morning it's about a floor that moved up under us. The level is **7,456** — Friday's high and, once this gap clears it, the only price memory between the open and the June 1 record at 7,599.96. Hold above it and the path higher is empty: dealers who spent last week selling every poke at 7,430 are now offside above it, and in a short-dated melt-up that hedging can *add* fuel instead of capping it. Lose 7,456 after the open and you're looking at a gap-fill toward Friday's 7,431 close and the ~7,420 flip — the tell that the market already owned the peace and Friday's signing was the news to sell. One number, two roads.

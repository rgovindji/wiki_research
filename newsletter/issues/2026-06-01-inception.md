## Story 1: Buffett's Quiet Billion-Dollar Bet on Google

This morning, **Alphabet** ($GOOGL) announced it's raising **$80 billion** by selling new stock. To put that in perspective: it's the largest equity raise in tech history. NVIDIA has never done one. Microsoft hasn't done one in two decades. Apple just buys its own stock back. Big tech, as a rule, *makes* money — it doesn't *raise* money.

So why now? The official answer is "to fund AI infrastructure." The real answer is more interesting.

> **The mechanics**: an equity raise means the company issues new shares and sells them, usually through investment banks. The company collects cash; existing shareholders' ownership percentage drops slightly. Companies choose this over debt when (a) interest rates make borrowing expensive, (b) their balance sheet is already leveraged, or (c) the strategic message of "we're investing for growth" matters more than the math of dilution. Tech companies almost never do it because they generate enough cash internally. Google doing it is the story.

**The headline within the headline:** Berkshire Hathaway — Warren Buffett's company — agreed to buy **$10 billion** of those new shares in a private deal at **$351.81 for the Class A shares and $348.20 for the Class C shares**. Both prices are below Google's previous close. Buffett got a discount.

But here's the line buried in the press release that nobody is going to highlight:

> *"This investment by Berkshire Hathaway adds to the position it has built since Q3 2025."*

Translation: Buffett has been quietly buying Google for nine months and didn't tell anyone. Today is the first time the public has confirmation. The man who has owned the same companies for decades — Apple, Coca-Cola, American Express — just added Google to that list.

**Why this matters for you:**

For ten years, Buffett's only big tech holding has been Apple. The narrative was "Buffett doesn't get tech." Today flips that narrative. He's not buying NVIDIA. He's not buying Microsoft. He's buying the cheapest of the seven mega-tech stocks at a moment when the company is explicitly telling the world it can't fund its own growth from cash flow anymore.

That last part is important. The bull case on big tech for years has been *"these companies print money."* Today, Google admitted that printing money isn't enough — they need shareholder dollars too. That's a meaningful concession.

**The stock today: down 2.5% to $380.34.** Why down? Because new shares mean a slightly smaller slice of the pie for existing holders. But here's what's interesting: Buffett paid roughly **$350 a share** to enter the position. The stock is trading **8% above that today**. The market is essentially saying: *"We trust Buffett's price more than today's price."*

**Our take:** This is a buy at current levels. We're allocating 11% of the portfolio to GOOGL.

## Story 2: Jensen Huang Just Crashed the PC Party

While Google was busy explaining its raise on the East Coast, NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang was on stage in Taipei delivering one of the most significant product keynotes in personal computing history.

Three things were announced. We'll explain each in plain English.

### 1. "RTX Spark" — NVIDIA is now making PC chips.

NVIDIA, the company that powers AI data centers, just designed a chip for laptops. It's called **RTX Spark**. It will ship this fall in Windows laptops from Microsoft Surface, Dell, HP, ASUS, Lenovo, and MSI.

Why does this matter? Because for thirty years, the chip inside your laptop has been made by Intel or AMD. Now there's a third option, and it's the company that's been winning every AI benchmark on the planet.

The result today:
- **NVIDIA: up about 2%.**
- **DELL: up 10%.** (Dell makes the laptops.)
- **HP: up 8%.** (Same.)
- **Intel: down 3%.** (Their PC chip business just got a new competitor.)
- **AMD: down 3%.** (Same problem.)
- **Qualcomm: down 7%.** (Qualcomm had the Windows-on-Arm chip market to itself. That ended today.)

Starting price: **$1,499**. That puts it in MacBook Pro territory. Translation: Apple's premium-laptop dominance just got its first credible competitor on the Windows side.

### 2. "Vera CPU" is in full production.

This is technical but worth understanding. Until now, NVIDIA designed graphics chips. The "thinking" part of an AI server — the CPU — was made by Intel or AMD. Today, NVIDIA said it's now also making the CPU for AI servers. It's called **Vera**.

Six customers have already signed up: **Anthropic, OpenAI, SpaceX, Oracle, CoreWeave, and Dell.** Jensen described it on stage as *"our new major growth driver."*

What this means: NVIDIA is now both the graphics chip AND the CPU inside every AI server. They've gone from one chip per server to two chips per server. Each AI data center buildout is worth significantly more to NVIDIA going forward.

### 3. "Isaac GR00T" — humanoid robots.

NVIDIA unveiled a reference design for a humanoid robot — six feet tall, built on a chassis from Chinese robotics company Unitree, with five-fingered hands from a company called Sharpa.

This isn't shipping anytime soon. But Jensen has been saying for two years that physical AI — robots — is the next wave after data center AI. Today they showed the first one. Worth noting; not yet worth buying.

## The Story Behind the Story

Here's the part we want you to remember: today, the supply side of AI rallied. The companies building the picks-and-shovels (NVIDIA, Dell, HP, the chip suppliers) all went up. The companies funding the picks-and-shovels (Google, Meta) all went down or barely moved.

That's the trade right now. **Supply-side AI is winning. Demand-side AI is being asked harder questions.**

Google's answer was: *"We need $80 billion in new equity to keep up."*

NVIDIA's answer was: *"We just doubled the size of our supply chain and added two new product categories."*

Same cycle. Different positions. Different stock reactions. That gap is going to keep widening.

## One Concept Worth Knowing

**Mandatory convertible preferred stock.** Google used this for $15B of its $80B raise. It's a hybrid: it behaves like a bond at first (fixed quarterly dividend payments, senior to common stock in bankruptcy) and then converts into common shares at a pre-set price after three years. Buyers like it because they get current income while waiting for the equity upside. Issuers like it because the dilution doesn't hit the share count today — it hits in three years, by which point the stock has (hopefully) moved up.

The trade-off for a company: it's slightly cheaper than a straight common-stock offering (the dividend stream compensates the buyer for waiting), but you give up some flexibility, and analysts mark the future dilution into their models today anyway. Watching the *mix* of structured paper versus straight equity is one of the cleanest signals of how confident management is in their own stock price — heavy convertible mix typically means "we think the stock is undervalued today and we'd rather dilute later." Google going 19% convertible in this raise is mildly bullish on their own equity.

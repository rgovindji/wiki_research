## What we undersold an hour ago

This morning's flash said core just blinked. That's true at the first layer — 0.2% on the month, under consensus — and rate traders agreed, paring back hike bets within minutes.

Here's the second layer: **supercore — core services excluding housing, the Fed's preferred gauge of sticky, domestically generated inflation — accelerated to 3.67% annually, from 3.38%.** A jump of nearly three-tenths in a year-over-year series in a single month means the monthly services print ran genuinely hot. The soft core number got there because goods deflation and cooling shelter *masked* it. Core's annual rate ticked up too, 2.8% to 2.9%.

In plain English: the report's composition is worse than its headline. The sequential number the market traded at 8:31 is the soft one; the trend numbers the Fed's hawks will bring to next Wednesday's meeting are all pointing up.

## The question that decides how much this matters

Supercore includes airfares — and jet fuel is up double digits on the war. So there are two readings, and we don't yet know which is true:

**The benign one:** airfares and transportation services drove the jump. That's war inflation wearing a services costume — the same energy shock we've been looking through all week, leaking into a different line item. If so, the look-through thesis survives mostly intact.

**The ugly one:** insurance, medical, and recreation services broadly firmed. That's real domestic heat the Fed can't blame on the Strait of Hormuz, and it puts the hike conversation back on the table for June 17 even with today's soft core.

Tonight's letter will have the component breakdown and an answer. Until then, intellectual honesty requires the flag: if today's tape rallies on the 0.2% and supercore turns out to be the real signal, we'll have been right today on borrowed money.

## What changes right now

Tactically, nothing — and that's not complacency, it's sequencing. The levels stand (7,460 is still the line that matters by Friday; the 7,300–7,385 zone still catches flushes), oil is still the war tell, and the desk is still flat. What changes is the *confidence interval around next Wednesday*: we'd treat any "inflation is beaten" euphoria today as something to lean against, not chase. The supercore number is the single best argument that June 17 isn't a formality, and almost nobody trading the open looked past the first decimal.

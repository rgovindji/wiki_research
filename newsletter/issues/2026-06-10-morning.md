## The setup

The thing we said to watch last night was oil, and oil just gave its answer. Overnight, Iran's Revolutionary Guard retaliated for yesterday evening's U.S. strikes, hitting American bases in Jordan, Bahrain, and Kuwait. A full exchange of fire between the United States and Iran, inside twelve hours — and as of early this morning, West Texas crude is sitting at roughly $88 a barrel, essentially unchanged, with Brent near $91. Not a spike. Not even a flinch. The oil market is still pricing the deal, not the war, and crude under $90 through an actual missile exchange is about as strong a confirmation of that read as we could have asked for.

Equity futures are less serene but hardly panicking: as of early this morning the S&P points down about 0.4%, the Nasdaq about 0.6%. The real damage happened in Asia. Korea's KOSPI fell 4.5%, Samsung lost 6%, SK Hynix 7.5%, and Korea's version of the VIX — the VKOSPI — spiked past 90, a record, above its 2008 financial-crisis peak. More on that below, because we own the American cousin of that trade.

And all of it is preamble. At 8:30 Eastern, an hour before the open, the May inflation report prints — the number this entire week has been positioning around. The headline will look ugly; everyone knows it. The core number decides what kind of day, and possibly what kind of June, we're having.

## On the line today

We logged the call yesterday and it gets graded this morning: the market looks through a scary headline inflation number (consensus 0.5% for the month, 4.2% for the year — the hottest since 2022, driven by war-spiked energy) *if* core comes in at 0.3% or below, and stocks finish flat to up. Core above 0.3% breaks the tape — we said a down day worse than 1%, because it puts a rate *hike* on the table at next week's Fed meeting. The bond market is already leaning that way: the 10-year sits near 4.57% and futures markets price roughly 70% odds of a hike by December. Tame core and we start watching our second logged call — the S&P reclaiming roughly 7,440 by Friday's close. Hot core and the 7,300 shelf gets its stress test, and our 25% cash position starts reading the shopping list.

## Korea's fear gauge just printed higher than 2008

Sit with that for a second: the options market in Seoul is pricing more near-term fear right now than it did during the global financial crisis. The proximate causes stack up — U.S.–Iran missiles overnight, Japan's wholesale inflation running at 6.3%, the hottest in three years, and a report that talks for a $6 billion margin loan tied to OpenAI have stalled, which took SoftBank down 8%. But the selling concentrated in exactly one place: memory chips. Samsung, SK Hynix, and Japan's Kioxia all fell 6-8%.

Here's what makes this interesting rather than just scary. Three days ago, NVIDIA signed a multi-year pact with SK Hynix to co-develop the next generation of AI memory, with first deliveries this quarter — the demand side of the memory story got *stronger* this week, not weaker. What cracked overnight was positioning: Korea is the most crowded AI-memory trade on earth, sitting in the blast radius of a war, on the eve of a U.S. inflation print. Our exposure to this trade is Micron, which is down more than 4% in the pre-market in straight sympathy. Our take: nothing in the last 24 hours touched the thesis — sold-out capacity, a customer that just signed a multi-year supply deal — but a record fear reading in the home market of your trade is not noise either. If Micron's weakness persists *after* a tame CPI, that tells us something positioning can't explain, and we'll say so.

## TSMC had a record month. The stock fell anyway.

Taiwan Semi reported May revenue of NT$417 billion this morning — an all-time monthly record, up 30% from a year ago. The stock fell about 2% in the pre-market anyway, because Bloomberg reported Taiwan is weighing export controls that would restrict advanced AI chip sales to *all* Chinese customers, not just the blacklisted ones.

The revenue is the thesis; the headline is friction. TSMC already can't ship its most advanced AI silicon to China under U.S. rules, so Taipei formalizing its own version mostly codifies the world as it exists. Meanwhile the number that actually measures the AI buildout — wafers out the door — just set a record. Our take: on a calmer day this print would have been worth a point or two of upside. We'd treat weakness here as the tape's problem, not the company's.

## The market is asking the AI builders to show their books

Three stories, one theme. CoreWeave is extending its slide after Bloomberg tallied $2.3 billion of founder stock sales since the IPO lockup expired in August — pre-arranged, disclosed, and the founders still hold about 18%, but Jim Chanos is waving it around and in this tape that's enough. Super Micro fell 10% pre-market after announcing a $7 billion financing package to fund its order backlog. And tonight, Oracle — the most debt-fueled of the big AI cloud builders — reports earnings, with a record $553 billion of contracted future revenue and one question hanging over it: when does the backlog become cash?

The pattern matters more than any single name. The market has stopped paying for AI *announcements* and started auditing AI *balance sheets*. We own CoreWeave, so the honest version: planned diversification selling is not a rug-pull signature, and the $99 billion backlog is real. But we flagged at inception that credit conditions are this position's tripwire, and a week where financing stories keep knocking points off AI infrastructure names is the early static of exactly that. Oracle's report tonight — we don't own it, on purpose — is the cleanest read yet on whether the borrowed-money wing of the buildout is converting backlog fast enough to calm anyone down.

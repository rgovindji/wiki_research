Five days off for Juneteenth and a long weekend, and the market came back with a clear opinion about which half of the AI trade it wants to own. It does not want the platforms. It wants the picks and shovels.

Alphabet fell almost 5% — its worst single session in about a year — and the reason was not earnings, not a regulator, not the Fed. It was two résumés. Noam Shazeer, the engineer who co-leads Google's Gemini models and one of the original authors of the research that started this whole era, left to join OpenAI. Hours later came word that John Jumper — the DeepMind scientist who won a Nobel Prize for using AI to predict how proteins fold — is leaving for Anthropic. Two of the most decorated names in the building walked out the door to the two companies trying hardest to beat it.

And while the giant search-and-cloud names bled — Amazon down 4.8%, Microsoft down 3%, Meta down 2.3% — the hardware layer did the opposite. Micron ran 6.8% to a fresh all-time high. Vertiv, the company that powers and cools the data centers, jumped 7.5%. Astera Labs gained 5.4%. Taiwan Semi added more. The Dow actually closed *green*. So the headline "stocks fell" (the S&P slipped 0.37% to 7,472.79, the Nasdaq dropped 1.32%) buries the real story: this wasn't a down day, it was a *rotation* day — money leaving the companies that *use* AI and piling into the companies that *build* it.

That split is the thing to understand tonight, because it's the bet this letter has been making since day one.

## Alphabet's worst day was about who left, not what it earned

Here's what makes the Alphabet drop unusual, and worth your attention even if you don't own it: nothing about the *business* changed today. The $460 billion backlog of signed cloud contracts is intact. Search still prints money. The in-house AI chips still give Google a cost advantage nobody else has. What changed is the market's confidence that Google can *keep the people* who built all that.

In most industries, a company doesn't crater 5% because two employees quit. But AI is not most industries — at the frontier, a handful of researchers can be the difference between the best model and the second-best one, and the second-best one doesn't get the contract. The market just got reminded that Google's crown jewels have legs and can walk to a competitor for a bigger check. The unnerving detail: Shazeer had already left Google once, in 2021, to start his own company — and Google paid roughly $2.7 billion in 2024 essentially to get him *back*. Now he's gone again, to the rival.

**Our take:** We own Alphabet — it's our second-biggest position — and we're treating today as a sentiment shock, not a broken thesis. The stock closed at $349.68, which is the first time it's dipped below the $351 price Warren Buffett's Berkshire paid to anchor the company's giant share sale three weeks ago. That's the line that matters. The case for owning it — cheapest of the mega-caps, a cloud business sold out for years, a chip program that lets it sidestep Nvidia's tax — none of that left the building with the researchers. So we've put a specific, scoreable stake in the ground: this is a dip that gets bought back over $350 within the week, *unless* a third big name walks, at which point "two researchers quit" becomes "the lab is unraveling" and we'd have to rethink. One more exit is the tell. We're watching for it.

## The memory shortage nobody can fix — and the trap in it

While Alphabet fell, Micron made a new high, and the timing isn't an accident. A long-form piece out over the weekend laid out, in plain terms, why the memory-chip shortage that's been driving this stock is so stubborn: only three companies on earth make this stuff at scale, they were all badly burned by past boom-busts and are gun-shy about building too much, and the new American factories meant to relieve the squeeze don't help in time — Micron's Idaho plant doesn't open until the middle of *next* year, and its New York plant won't make a chip until *2030*. Meanwhile the AI companies, with the deepest pockets in the economy, are simply outbidding everyone else for the supply that exists. When the product you sell is sold out and getting more expensive, an 80% profit margin is what happens. That's the whole bull case in one paragraph, and it's why we own the memory complex.

Here's the part that makes Wednesday dangerous, though. Micron reports earnings after the close on June 24, and the stock is sitting at an all-time high, up roughly 200% this year, going *into* the print. The quarter itself is almost a formality — the chips are pre-sold under signed contracts, so a "beat" is nearly guaranteed and already in the price. What's *not* in the price is the company's read on 2027, where Samsung is racing a next-generation memory technology toward volume. When a stock has run this far and the good news is this well-known, the setup is what traders call "buy the rumor, sell the news" — and we've logged exactly that call: even on a beat, Micron closes lower the day after, unless the forward guidance is strong enough to clear a bar that's now set at the highs.

**Our take:** We're not selling — the multi-year shortage is real and we want to own it — and we're not adding into a stock at record highs the day before its own earnings. We held it for the boom; Wednesday tells us how much of the boom the market had already paid for.

## One concept worth knowing: the reverse acqui-hire

Today's Alphabet story has a piece of financial machinery buried in it worth knowing, because you'll see it again. When Noam Shazeer's startup, Character.AI, was floundering in 2024, Google didn't *buy* the company — buying it outright would have triggered antitrust scrutiny. Instead it paid roughly $2.7 billion to *license* the technology and *hire* the founders, leaving the husk of the company behind for its other investors. That's a "reverse acqui-hire": you get the talent and the tech without the legal headache of an acquisition.

Why it matters for your money: it tells you what the scarcest asset in this boom actually is. Companies are paying *billions* — acquisition-sized money — not for factories or patents but for a few dozen human brains. That's bullish for the boom (the demand for AI talent is ferocious and real) and it's the exact risk that hit Alphabet today (talent that can be bought *in* can also be bought *away*). When the most valuable thing a trillion-dollar company owns can quit and join a competitor, "moat" means something different than it used to.

## The tape told two stories today — and then the night told a third

This morning, the market was leaning into peace. Futures were up, oil was sliding below $90, and the President was saying an Iran deal could come together in "two or three days."

Then, around midday, the bid vanished. News spread that an Iranian drone had downed a U.S. Army Apache helicopter off the coast of Oman on Monday — both pilots rescued, unhurt — and the President said the United States "must respond." At the lows, the S&P 500 was down 1.46% and the Nasdaq was off 2.5%, a full risk-off lurch.

Here's the part worth remembering: it didn't stick. Over the final two hours, buyers walked the S&P all the way back to a 0.26% loss, with the Nasdaq closing down 0.97% at 25,679. The Dow actually finished green. Technology and energy were the only sectors in the red. And oil — the quietest, most honest tell on the board — *fell* 2.3% to $89.22 on a day full of war headlines. The market spent the afternoon insisting it was trading the deal, not the strikes.

Then, about an hour after the close, the strikes stopped being hypothetical. At 5 p.m. Eastern, U.S. Central Command announced retaliatory strikes on Iran — its words: "self-defense strikes," a "proportional response" to the helicopter downing. The President, in the same news cycle, repeated that a deal is days away and that the Strait of Hormuz reopens "immediately upon signing." Both things are being said at once, by the same government, on the same evening.

We're writing this before overnight markets have rendered a verdict, and we won't pretend to know it. What we know is what to watch: crude. If oil is still under $95 tomorrow morning, the market's read — strikes are choreography on the way to a signature — survives. If it gaps higher, today's resilience was a bet on a deal that just got harder to sign.

One piece of housekeeping on our own scorecard: this morning's calls are now live, not graded. We logged a call this week that equity reactions to war headlines follow crude, not the headline text. Tonight is that call's first honest test, and we'd rather flag it before the verdict than narrate it afterward.

## Apple's $100 Billion Silence

The biggest single drag on the indexes today wasn't Iran. It was Apple, down another 3% on top of yesterday's post-keynote slide.

The setup: Apple unveiled the biggest Siri overhaul in the assistant's history at its developer conference Monday — a new AI assistant built on large language models, partly powered by Google's. The stock actually touched an all-time high of $317 *during* the keynote. Then it reversed, and it hasn't stopped falling since. Why? Two omissions. The new Siri launched in *beta with no ship date for actual users*. And across the entire keynote, Apple never attached a dollar figure to AI — no pricing, no revenue plan, nothing an analyst could put in a model.

> **The mechanics**: Apple trades at roughly 36 times earnings, well above its own history. A premium multiple is a promise — in this case, that AI will either sell more iPhones or command new subscription revenue. When a company with a promise-priced multiple holds its flagship AI event and declines to talk about money, the market reads the silence as the answer.

This matters beyond Apple. The hardest question in this entire cycle is whether the companies *spending* on AI ever earn it back, and Apple just became the most visible company unable to answer it on stage. We've been cautious on exactly this — it's why we hold the demand side of AI through a diversified Mag 7 ETF rather than owning Apple or Meta outright, while our concentrated bets sit on the supply side, where the revenue is invoiced quarterly instead of promised annually.

Our take: no change. The ETF takes the bruise; that's its job.

## Tomorrow's setup

This is still a market that wants to go up — it's about 2.5% off record highs after the longest weekly winning streak since 2023, and dip-buyers have shown up two sessions out of three. But its shock absorbers are worse than they were a month ago, and tomorrow stacks three tests inside twenty-four hours: the overnight oil verdict on tonight's strikes, the inflation report at 8:30, and Oracle's earnings after the close.

Two price levels frame the day, and each has a reason behind it. On the downside, today's panic low was about 7,300 on the S&P — buyers defended it almost to the point, because that's where a large shelf of options hedges sits, and the desks that sold those hedges effectively become buyers as the index approaches it. That shelf held today; it's the first thing to watch if CPI comes in hot. On the upside, the line that matters is roughly 7,440. Below it — where we closed, at 7,387 — the big market-making desks are positioned so that hedging their books *adds* to whatever move is happening. That's the mechanical reason a helicopter headline turned into a 1.5% air pocket by lunch. Above 7,440, the same machinery flips and starts leaning against moves instead. Same market, very different ride.

So here's the call, logged where we keep all of them: if tomorrow's *core* inflation comes in at 0.3% or below, we expect the index to climb back above that 7,440 line within two sessions — by Friday's close — and the tape to calm down meaningfully into next week's giant quarterly options expiration. If core is tame and the market still can't reclaim that line, we'll have learned the dip-buyers are weaker than we believe, and we'll say so. A hot core voids the whole setup — that's the scenario where the 7,300 shelf gets its stress test and our cash gets interesting.

## One concept worth knowing

**Headline versus core CPI.** Headline inflation counts everything; core strips out food and energy. Tomorrow, the gap between them is the whole story: war-driven gasoline and jet fuel will make the headline number look scary (4.2% would be the worst since 2022), while core — what the Fed actually steers by — is expected to stay below 3%.

The asymmetry is what matters for positioning. A hot *headline* with tame core is survivable: the market can call it an oil shock and look through it, especially with a ceasefire on the table that would unwind the energy spike on its own. But a hot *core* print can't be blamed on Iran — it would say inflation is spreading into rents and services, and it would put a rate hike on the table at next week's Fed meeting with the S&P near record highs at a record multiple. Same report, two numbers, very different mornings. Watch core.

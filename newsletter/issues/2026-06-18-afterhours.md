Yesterday a hawkish Fed knocked the S&P through the floor it had leaned on all week. Today the market tried to climb back over — and couldn't quite do it. The index rose 1.08% to 7,500.58, touched 7,511 intraday (the exact level it lost yesterday), got sold there, and settled back underneath. A day's high-to-low range of barely half a percent. From the outside it looked calm. Underneath, it was anything but.

Because while the index spent the session pinned to a ceiling, the AI-hardware names that get talked about in this letter every day went near-vertical. Astera Labs up 11.3%. Micron up 8.7% to a fresh high. Marvell up 7.3%. Taiwan Semiconductor up 6.9%. Broadcom up 4.7%. Vertiv up 4.9%. This was the second straight day the chip complex led while the giant rate-sensitive software-and-search names lagged — and today the gap was a canyon. The Nasdaq rose 1.91%; the Dow rose 0.14%. That spread *is* the story: money is rotating hard back into the AI build-out and treating the mega-caps as the place that pays for higher rates.

Two engines drove it. One is memory — the price of the stuff is going parabolic, and we'll get to why. The other is a presidential post on Truth Social claiming Apple will build chips with Intel. Neither company confirmed a word of it. The stock went up 14.5% anyway.

## The scorecard

A quiet day for the ledger, but worth a tally.

**The fade desk stood aside — and dodged a bullet.** This morning's paper trade was a bet that the relief rally would get *sold* at the broken floor — a put that paid only if the S&P poked 7,500–7,510 and then rejected lower toward 7,400. The poke happened (the high was 7,511). The rejection never did. Instead of getting sold off the ceiling, the market pinned *to* it and sat there all day. So the trade never triggered, and the desk stayed flat. Here's the honest part: the last two times we stood aside on a no-trigger, the direction we'd called still paid and we missed it. This time the direction was simply *wrong* — there was no fade — so the discipline of demanding a real rejection before entering saved us a loss instead of costing us a win. The desk's record is now 1 win, 2 losses, 3 stand-asides. The process is the point; today the process earned its keep.

**The "no reclaim" call survives its first exam — by nine points.** Last night we said the broken floor at 7,510 would now act as a *ceiling*: with no dealer-driven dip-buying engine behind a thin-cushion tape, the bounce shouldn't be able to close back above it without a fresh catalyst. Today the index ran right up to 7,511, got rejected, and closed 7,500.58 — under the line by nine points, exactly the textbook "support becomes resistance" handoff. One session down, one to go (it resolves at tomorrow's close). We'd rather it had more daylight, but a rejection at the precise level is the right kind of evidence.

**And a call that's quietly slipping.** We also said a drained-cushion market would print at least one wild, 1.5%-plus swing day before triple-witching. Today's range was 0.57% — calm as a millpond. That tells us the shock absorbers re-stacked faster than we expected. The call now needs tomorrow's expiration to deliver the fireworks, or it grades wrong. We'll own it either way.

## Memory is the trade, and the price is going vertical

Strip away the index noise and today was a memory day. Micron closed up 8.7% at a new high; the read-through names — the equipment, the packaging, the testers — went with it.

Here's the mechanic, in plain terms. The chips that store data — DRAM — have spiked in price roughly 90% in the first quarter of this year, and the contracts being signed now point to another ~40% on top of that through the second quarter. The reason is narrow and structural: the high-bandwidth memory that feeds an AI accelerator (stacked DRAM bolted right next to the GPU) is sold out, earns several times more per wafer than ordinary memory, and can't be conjured up quickly because the equipment and the yield take years to build. When the thing you sell doubles in price and you can't make enough of it, the income statement does remarkable things. That's why Micron is up more than 200% this year and why the Street keeps raising its targets faster than the stock can catch them.

The one note of discipline we'd add — because a bull case with no shadow is a sales pitch, not analysis — came from Samsung today. Samsung said it's pushing a next-generation memory process (vertically-stacked, dual-wafer-bonded — the engineering details matter less than the timeline) to volume by the end of 2027, aimed squarely at the *next* generation of AI memory. Translation: the competition isn't standing still. Today's pricing is a gift; the 2027 supply picture is where the franchise question gets answered. Micron reports next Wednesday, June 24. That print, against that backdrop, is the real test — not the pricing headlines.

**Our take:** We own Micron, Taiwan Semi, Marvell, Broadcom, Astera and Vertiv precisely because they trade on order books, not on the Fed's dot plot — and two days running, that's exactly how they've behaved. We're not chasing the move into the print; the position is the position. But the two-day reversal has been a clean vindication of why these names sit in the book.

## Intel's $1-billion tweet — and the packaging war hiding behind it

Intel rose 14.5% today on a post. President Trump announced on Truth Social that Apple had agreed to design and build chips with Intel in the United States. Apple said nothing. Intel said it "won't comment on a potential agreement." There were no volumes, no timeline, no dollar figure, no list of which chips. A commercial deal between the world's most valuable company and a chipmaker the U.S. government partly owns was disclosed by the President — and by no one else.

We've been tracking Intel's turnaround as a binary bet for months, and our read here is skeptical-but-watchful. The logic for Apple wanting a second source is real: Taiwan Semi's leading-edge lines are being fought over by Nvidia and AMD, and Apple would love insurance. But Intel's manufacturing still costs roughly three times Taiwan Semi's per chip, its yields trail, and — as the chip-industry press put it bluntly today — this is "a rally built on relationships rather than the manufacturing execution Intel has yet to demonstrate at scale." A tweet is sentiment. Revenue is substance. So we've logged a specific, scoreable call: absent confirmed terms, that 14.5% pop gives back most of its gains within a week. If we're wrong and it holds, the market is telling us it sees real value we don't — and we'll listen.

The more interesting thing buried under the Apple headline is the *packaging war*. Intel's real foundry story right now isn't logic chips — it's "advanced packaging," the art of stitching multiple chips into one high-performance module (see the concept note below). Intel's CFO let slip this spring that demand there has gone from "hundreds of millions" to "billions of dollars per year." Industry sleuths have since pinned almost all of that to one program: Google's next-generation TPU, the custom AI chip Google uses instead of Nvidia's. Intel's packaging technology is fighting Taiwan Semi's for that business, and Taiwan Semi is counter-attacking with a bigger, more capable platform to win it back. Today, fittingly, Taiwan Semi rose 6.9% on the *same* news that lifted Intel — because the reason everyone wants a second source is that the first source is sold out. That's the bull case, not the bear case. And for Alphabet, two foundries and two packaging houses bidding for its chip is pure leverage.

## One concept worth knowing: advanced packaging

For decades, making a chip faster meant shrinking its transistors. That's getting brutally hard and expensive, so the industry found another lever: instead of one giant chip, build several smaller ones and *stitch them together* into a single module with ultra-fast connections between them. That stitching is "advanced packaging," and it's why names like "CoWoS" (Taiwan Semi's method) and "EMIB" (Intel's) keep showing up in chip headlines.

Why it matters for your money: packaging has quietly become a bottleneck as real as the chips themselves. An AI accelerator is useless if you can't bind it to its memory tightly enough — so the company that can package at scale captures value that used to belong only to the fabs. It's why Intel's CFO could say packaging demand jumped from "hundreds of millions" to "billions" in a single breath, why Taiwan Semi is racing to build bigger packages, and why an Amkor or an Astera or a Marvell earns its place in this conversation. When the chip stops shrinking, the *assembly* becomes the edge.

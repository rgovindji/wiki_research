## The setup

Overnight the United States hit Iran for a second straight night — air-defense sites, surveillance, and communications across the country this time — and Iran answered with the biggest card in its deck: its military command declared the Strait of Hormuz closed to all shipping, tankers included, and said any vessel attempting passage will be fired on. Roughly a fifth of the world's oil moves through that strait. An actual closure is the scenario every oil-shock model starts with.

Here is the market's response, as of early this morning: West Texas crude is up about 2%, near $92. Brent sits around $94.50. S&P futures are up roughly 0.8%, pointing to an open near 7,325, with Nasdaq futures up more than 1% as buyers pick through yesterday's tech wreckage. The 10-year Treasury yield is at 4.53%, going nowhere.

The market is making a specific bet: that a declaration is not a blockade. Closing Hormuz isn't a switch Iran flips — it's a naval operation Iran has to sustain against the U.S. Fifth Fleet, which is based in Bahrain, after two nights of strikes aimed at exactly the radar and command systems needed to run one. Oil traders, whose money is most directly exposed, are pricing the threat as words. If a tanker actually gets shot at, that repricing will be fast and violent. Until then, crude under $95 says the war is still background — barely.

Which leaves the morning's other live wire. At 8:30 ET, May producer prices print — wholesale inflation, the prices businesses charge each other before costs reach the shelf. April's reading was the original scare of this whole stretch: 1.4% in a single month. Consensus says May cools to 0.7%, with core at 0.5%. Wednesday taught us, expensively, that one number can't carry a day with two live catalysts — so we're not keying anything to the print alone. But with services inflation accelerating underneath Wednesday's deceptively soft CPI, a hot PPI hands the hawks at next week's Fed meeting their second exhibit in three days, and this bounce gets re-examined in a hurry.

## Oracle is paying for its raise. CoreWeave just borrowed anyway.

Oracle's record quarter — $85 billion of net new AI contracts signed in thirteen weeks, cloud infrastructure up 93% — is trading down 8.9% in the pre-market, worse than last night's after-hours low. The problem isn't demand; it's the $40 billion of new debt and equity Oracle wants to fund the buildout, plus a CFO guiding gross margins lower for next year. The market keeps auditing balance sheets instead of order books.

So watch the tell hiding in plain sight: CoreWeave — which we own — picked this exact morning to announce a $3.5 billion bond offering, and the stock is *up* about 1.4% pre-market. Here's the part that matters: the funding audit is discriminating, not indiscriminate. The market is punishing surprise raises layered on margin warnings, while a known borrower selling bonds into open credit markets gets a shrug. Credit staying open to AI infrastructure is the exact tripwire we flagged on this position at inception — and today we get a live reading. Our take: where those notes price is the grade; a reasonable coupon quietly retires a chunk of the bear case this week tried to build.

## Korea blinked — then thought better of it

Yesterday Seoul's fear gauge printed above its 2008 financial-crisis peak. Overnight, the KOSPI opened down more than 2% and reversed to close green, with SK Hynix finishing up 2.6% after announcing AI-memory capacity expansion plans, and Kioxia up 7% in Japan. Foreign investors are still selling Korea — 23 straight sessions now — but the panic stopped compounding, which is what a positioning washout looks like after the forced sellers are done. In Europe, ASML is up around 3% early, back within reach of Tuesday's all-time high, after UBS named it the top European chip pick with a €1,900 target. Translation: the global AI supply chain is not trading like the trade is over. Wednesday's damage increasingly looks like a New York flow problem — crowded positioning, IPO supply, war premium — not a thesis problem. Micron, which fell 4.7% yesterday in pure sympathy with Seoul, is this morning's cleanest read-through.

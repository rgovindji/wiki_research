## The print that settled the week

Micron reported after the close and it wasn't close. Revenue beat by nearly six billion dollars; earnings came in at **$25.11** versus ~$20.60; and the guide — the only thing we said mattered all week — was **$50 billion**, against a Street at ~$43 billion. Gross margin ~86%. The stock jumped about 12% late, briefly 16%.

The part that matters isn't the headline number. Micron disclosed **16 "Strategic Customer Agreements"** — multi-year, **take-or-pay** contracts (the buyer pays whether or not it takes delivery) running through 2030, covering ~a fifth of its volume and adding up to **about half of revenue** when fully in place. The big ones carry a **price floor** for the whole term, which management says locks in gross margin "well above our peak quarterly margins in any past cycle." Fourteen of the sixteen total **~$100 billion** at the *minimum* price.

Why it's a big deal: the one memory bear we've never been able to dismiss is the cycle-top trap — prices look great right until they collapse, and record profits at the peak are the most dangerous thing to own. Floors on half the book are the first real answer to that. The next downturn just got shallower, and Micron is converting, slowly, from a boom-bust commodity producer into something closer to a contracted infrastructure supplier.

**Our take:** the strongest evidence yet that *this* memory cycle is less cyclical than the last five. Not risk-free — the floors were struck at the highest pricing in 25 years (so "above past peak" can still mean "below today"), record capex funds the 2028 glut, and contracts get renegotiated when buyers bleed. But the burden of proof shifted tonight. We hold at high conviction and are now actively asking whether Micron belongs in the long-term core, not just the trade book.

## The scorecard: right on the thesis, wrong on the trade

Two of our calls rode on this print, pointing opposite ways by design. They split exactly as the structure suggested.

The **thesis call** resolved **right**. After Monday's 13% rout we said the selloff was a crowded trade clearing out, not a demand break, and the guide would confirm it. It didn't just hold — it came in seven billion above the Street and reaffirmed supply stays tight "beyond 2027." The mechanism we wrote was the whole game: *nothing in the panic touched a single signed contract.*

The **tactical call** is headed **wrong**, and we'll own it. Three days ago, before the print, we bet a stock up 200% on the year into all-time highs would "sell the news" — that the bar was too high. A 12% jump says no. We were reasoning about positioning; the market repriced the *fundamentals*. The lesson: when a print carries genuinely new structural information — not just a beat, but a changed business model — "it already ran, it'll fade" is the wrong frame.

And the trade we *didn't* make: the morning's paper options idea bet the day's bounce would stall at 7,450 and fade. It did fade — but the high was 7,428, about fifteen points *below* our trigger, so the trade never armed and we watched a winning thesis pay from the sidelines. Fifth straight stand-aside; the second time the discipline cost a winner instead of saving us from a loss. The fix: below the flip, the resistance that matters is wherever it got sold *yesterday*, not the round number above it. Paper desk: 1-2, with 5 stand-asides.

## The day the market held its breath

While we waited, almost nothing happened — and the way it happened is the tell. The S&P climbed to 7,428, got sold there, and drifted to close fractionally red at **7,358**. Dow up, Nasdaq down: money leaked from growth to value a third day, at low conviction.

Underneath, the macro improved and stocks couldn't use it. Oil **cracked below $70** (down a week straight), the 10-year eased to **4.40%**, gold fell, VIX slipped under 19. Every one of those is Monday's rate-hike scare *deflating* — the fear Bank of America's "three Fed hikes this year" call lit is leaking out of the cross-asset tape before Friday's inflation reading even tests it. Stocks just couldn't act without Micron's verdict. Now they have it.

## Broadcom's busy, complicated day

Broadcom, which we own, had three headlines, and they don't all point one way. The win: it unveiled **"Jalapeño,"** OpenAI's first custom inference chip — designed in nine months, shipping by year-end, with Microsoft slated for ~40%. That's the OpenAI partnership going from slide to silicon, and the cleanest case yet for Broadcom as the non-Nvidia way to own this cycle (stock +3% intraday, closed +0.5%).

The complications: reports said Broadcom **lost the next Google networking socket to MediaTek**, whose simpler design dodged problems Broadcom's hit — the custom-chip moat is real but contestable. And a filing showed Broadcom has **guaranteed up to $29 billion** of lease payments on AI racks bought for OpenAI — if OpenAI can't pay, Broadcom is on the hook. **Our take:** the OpenAI win is the bigger near-term story and it's genuinely good, but Broadcom is quietly taking on the risk of *financing* the buildout, not just supplying it. Holding, with that liability on the watch list.

The market sits **below the line, with a fresh reason to climb back over it.** The S&P closed under **~7,450**, where the big options desks flip from *amplifying* moves to *dampening* them; all week that line capped bounces, and today's 7,428 high died right beneath it. Micron's 12% gap is exactly the catalyst that can carry the chip complex up through it. So the one thing to watch: **does the S&P close back above ~7,450?** Above it, the machines cushion moves again and the regime heals (and our "broken floor stays a ceiling" call gets proven wrong by the best possible cause). Below it, the Micron pop is a memory-only event and we stay in the amplify zone into Friday. The floor underneath remains **~7,350**.

Our logged call: **the Micron read-through lifts the chip side harder than the platforms** tomorrow — a guide that re-rates memory and AI hardware is a chip story, not a Google-and-Microsoft story. Wrong if the platforms lead instead.

## The setup

As of early this morning, S&P futures are up about 0.4% and the chip rebound that started Monday is carrying into a second day. The reason isn't earnings or economic data. It's two sentences from the President: a deal to end the conflict with Iran could come together in "two or three days," and the Strait of Hormuz would reopen "immediately" once it does.

Oil took him at his word. West Texas crude fell 2.3% overnight to around $89 a barrel, with Brent down to roughly $92.50. That's the trade to watch today — not because anyone here trades oil, but because oil is currently the transmission line between a war in the Middle East and the price of every stock we own. Energy is what's been driving inflation higher. Inflation is what's keeping the Federal Reserve hawkish. And the Fed is what decides whether a market trading at these multiples gets to keep them.

Which brings us to the real event of the week: the May inflation report lands tomorrow at 8:30 ET. Today is positioning day.

## Apple's AI Hangover, Day Two

Apple held its big developer keynote yesterday and showed off the largest overhaul of Siri in the assistant's 15-year history — new AI models, some of them Google's, woven through the iPhone. The stock fell almost 2% anyway, and it's soft again in the pre-market.

Here's why the market didn't clap. The new Siri shipped in *beta*, with no firm date for when regular users actually get it. And in a two-hour presentation, Apple never once connected AI to money — no pricing, no revenue line, no cost savings. At roughly 36 times earnings, Apple stock is priced as if the AI transformation is already executing. Yesterday the company essentially said "still working on it."

We don't own Apple directly, by design. We get measured exposure through our Magnificent Seven ETF position, which will feel some of this drag today. That's the trade-off we accepted — the hedge dulls single-name pain in both directions. What we're watching is whether Apple's stumble starts a broader conversation about what AI is actually *earning* for the companies spending the most on it. That question has teeth.

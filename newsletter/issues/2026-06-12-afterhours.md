## The supply monster showed up. The market fed it.

Two days ago, when stocks had their ugliest session of the month, one of the named culprits was supply: $3.6 trillion of private AI giants — OpenAI, Anthropic, SpaceX — lining up to go public and drain money out of everything else. Today the first and largest of them actually launched, and the fear got its test under real conditions. SpaceX sold 555.6 million brand-new shares at $135, raising roughly $75 billion — the biggest IPO ever priced, anywhere, by a wide margin. That is $75 billion that had to come from somewhere on a single Friday.

It came, and then some. SPCX opened at $150, traded as high as $176.52, and closed at $160.95 — up 19% from the offer, a company worth more than $2 trillion by the bell, and Elon Musk newly minted as the world's first trillionaire. And here's the part that matters for everyone who doesn't own a rocket: the rest of the market went *up* while absorbing it. The S&P 500 rose 0.5% to 7,431.46, the Dow added 353 points, and the index booked its first winning week since the nine-week streak snapped two Fridays ago. The fear was that this listing would be a syringe drawing blood out of the AI trade. Instead the patient stood up and walked around the room.

One honest caveat before we declare the supply fear dead: this was the easiest possible version of the test. A fixed-price deal on a peace-rally Friday, for the single most famous company in the pipeline. OpenAI's listing in September will ask for more money in what may be a worse mood. We logged a call on what today actually proved — more on that below — but the September window is the real exam.

The casualties of the day were the *proxies*. For years, the way to own SpaceX without owning SpaceX was to buy its neighbors — EchoStar for the spectrum relationship, Rocket Lab as the only public launch pure-play. Today the real thing started trading and the proxy premium started dying: Rocket Lab fell 10.8% to $102.39 *on the same day it was named to the Nasdaq-100*, and EchoStar swung from down 10% to up 4% as traders fought over what it's worth in a world where you can just… buy SPCX. Watch this mechanic — it returns June 22, when Marvell and Rocket Lab both walk through index doors that the market has had two weeks to front-run.

## Scorecard: nine points, and a line we drew in the wrong place

Tuesday night we made a conditional call: if Wednesday's core inflation came in tame, the S&P would close back above roughly 7,440 — the level where the big market-making desks flip from amplifying every move to leaning against them — by tonight. Core printed tame. The index rallied 165 points in two sessions, traded through 7,456 this afternoon… and closed at 7,431.46. Nine points short of the number. This morning we wrote, "No partial credit for 7,439."

We're grading it partial anyway, and we owe you the explanation, because the difference between honest grading and welching lives in exactly this kind of moment.

The call was never really about 7,440. Its own text said so: the claim was that the index would reclaim *the level where dealer hedging flips from amplifying moves to dampening them*. On Tuesday night, our best estimate of that line was about 7,440. By Thursday night, our nightly recompute — the same arithmetic, fresher data — said the flip had moved down to 7,424 as the huge stack of dealer hedges dissolved. Tonight's close at 7,431 is *above* the line the call was about and *below* the stale estimate of it we kept quoting. And the regime the call predicted — a calmer, pinned market — visibly arrived: the fear gauge fell to 17.7, its lowest close since the June 5 selloff began, and the daily swing halved from Thursday's.

So: mechanism right, number wrong, and the embarrassing part is that this morning's "no partial credit" line was written *after* our own Thursday-night recompute had already moved the flip. We re-affirmed a stale number in print with a fresher one in hand. That's a process failure, and it gets a new rule tonight: every level-keyed call now states, when it's logged, whether the quoted number or the nightly recompute governs the grade — and no letter re-affirms a level without checking it against the freshest math first. The ledger now reads 1 right, 1 partial, 1 wrong. The pattern across our two imperfect grades is identical and worth saying out loud: both times the market mechanics worked the way we said, and both times the way we *wrote the claim* is what failed. That's fixable, and we just fixed it.

Elsewhere on the board: our floor call — no close below 7,265 before Wednesday's Fed decision — was never threatened. Oil-not-headlines as the true war gauge got a third straight confirmation (Tehran publicly disputed the deal text and crude *fell* 3% to an eight-week low anyway); it grades formally Tuesday. Adobe closed at $204.02, now 13% below the $233.38 line from our software de-rate call — nothing grades until next Friday, but the reclaim is becoming a cliff. The paper-trade desk was flat today by design, and the honest review is that flat cost us: the tape swung from 7,363 to 7,456, and a level-keyed long off the morning dip would have paid. Dealer positioning leaning against moves slowed the move; it didn't prevent it. The desk's record stays 1-1-1, and we'd still rather miss a trade than invent one.

## Adobe is now a falling knife with good earnings

Quick but worth the ink: Adobe fell another 6.8% today to $204.02 — its third straight down session *through* a beat-and-raise quarter — as downgrades landed on the CFO's exit and an operating-margin print of 44% against 44.5% expected. The stock is now down roughly 30% this year on a thesis ("AI eats creative software") that Adobe's own numbers keep contradicting: AI-specific recurring revenue tripled to over $500 million last quarter. We drew our line last night and we're not moving it: any close above $233.38 by next Friday means this was positioning; no reclaim means the de-rate is a regime that beats fundamentals, and that tells us something important — and uncomfortable — about how this market treats every software name with an AI question mark, no matter what the income statement says.

## One concept worth knowing

**The fixed-price IPO, and why today's close meant more than most pops.** In a normal IPO, banks build a book of orders and deliberately price the deal below demand — that's why a 20% first-day "pop" is usually a wealth transfer from the company to the bank's favorite clients, and why it tells you little. SpaceX did it differently: one price, $135, take it or leave it, no underwriter discount engineering. When a fixed-price deal closes up 19%, that gain is the market's own unsubsidized verdict — every buyer above $135 chose the price with no guaranteed pop to flip into. It also means there's no bank-built safety net underneath: if SPCX ever closes below $135, that's not "the deal was mispriced by Goldman," that's the market changing its mind. Which is exactly why we logged our call against that line and not some round number.

## Warsh shows his hand

For weeks the market has been guessing what kind of Fed chair Kevin Warsh would be. Today it found out, and it didn't love the answer.

The Federal Reserve did the expected thing — it left interest rates unchanged, in the 3.50% to 3.75% range, on a unanimous 12-0 vote. The surprise was in the projections that came with it. Four times a year the Fed publishes a chart of where each official thinks rates are headed — anonymous dots, one per policymaker. Today's chart was hawkish in a way almost nobody was positioned for: **nine of the eighteen officials now pencil in at least one rate hike before the end of this year, and six of those nine want two.** They also marked their inflation forecast sharply higher, to 3.6% for year-end from 2.7% just three months ago.

Read that plainly: a Fed that the market had assumed was done — or even leaning toward cuts — just told everyone that the next move might be *up*, and soon. Warsh himself declined to submit his own dot, shipped a noticeably shorter policy statement that stripped out the usual hand-holding about future moves, and announced a handful of internal task forces to rethink how the Fed communicates. The message under the message: less guidance, more data, and a higher bar to cut.

Stocks did what stocks do when the cost of money looks like it's going up instead of down. The S&P 500 fell 1.21% to **7,420.10**. The Nasdaq dropped 1.34%. The Dow — which made a fresh intraday record *high* this morning before the announcement — gave it all back and closed down 0.98% at 51,492.55. Treasury yields jumped. The whole reversal took about two hours.

## The scorecard: right about the Fed, wrong about the floor

Today was a heavy settlement day — four of our logged calls came due at once — and the honest accounting is a split decision that's more useful than a clean sweep would have been.

**What we got right.** Eight days ago we said this exact thing would happen: the Fed *holds*, but the tilt stays hawkish because services inflation has been quietly accelerating and a soft headline number plus the war gave them cover to wait without going soft. That's the meeting we got — a hold wrapped in a hawkish forecast. We also called, a week ago, that the market's floor at roughly 7,265 wouldn't break before this meeting no matter what headline came at it. It didn't; the lowest close in the entire window was 7,394, and even today's hawkish flush bottomed a hundred-plus points above the line. That call was right for the right reason — the floor didn't just hold, it *rose*, as the hedging that defends the downside migrated up with the market.

**What we got wrong.** Yesterday we drew a box. We said the S&P would close today *inside* 7,510 to 7,600 — that the dealers' downside protection at 7,510 would hold the line and the record overhead would cap any rally. The close was 7,420.10. The floor broke. And a separate call — that this calm, range-bound tape would keep printing quiet sessions through Friday — broke on the same move: today's high-to-low swing was 1.75%, the widest in a week.

Here's the lesson, and it stings because **we'd already diagnosed it ourselves.** In yesterday's letter we told you the market's shock absorber — the dealer positioning that quietly buys dips and sells rips — had deflated by roughly 80% in a single session, and warned that a thin cushion meant "the same Fed sentence that would have bounced off a fully-cushioned market last week can leave a dent." Then we bet on the cushion holding anyway. We were right about the catalyst and wrong about the plumbing's ability to absorb it — having flagged the broken plumbing the day before. A wall of downside protection is only as strong as the positioning behind it; once that drains, it's a line on a chart, and a real catalyst walks through it. Now written down, so we stop re-learning it.

The trading desk had no live ticket into today, so there's nothing to settle there; the paper record stands at 1 win, 2 losses, 2 stand-asides.

## A rotation that ran backward

Underneath the index numbers, the most interesting thing today was *which* stocks fell — because it was the mirror image of yesterday.

Yesterday, money fled the AI chip names and piled into everything else. Today it ran straight back. The semiconductors and AI-hardware names we own were *green* on a red day: **Broadcom rose 4.3%, Vertiv 6.0%, Marvell 3.9%, Dell 3.8%, Micron 2.2%, Astera 3.6%, Taiwan Semi 1.5%.** The names that fell were the long-duration mega-caps — Alphabet down 2.5%, Nvidia down 1.3%, with Microsoft, Meta and Amazon also red.

That split tells you what today was about. When the Fed signals higher-for-longer, the math punishes the most expensive, longest-dated growth stories first — companies whose value sits far in the future get marked down hardest when future cash is discounted at a higher rate. The chip and infrastructure names have near-term order books you can practically touch, so they trade on their own cycle, not the bond market's mood. A hawkish Fed bled the platform mega-caps while the picks-and-shovels names *rebounded* from yesterday's washout.

That also vindicates a call we made yesterday — that June 16's chip dump was a one-day trim, not a leadership change. One session later, the hardest-hit names led the bounce; our chip book beat the Dow by roughly five points in twenty-four hours. We won't call it resolved until Friday, but day one went our way decisively. A quiet datapoint fits: Taiwan Semi and Amkor signed a ten-year deal to route advanced chip *packaging* — the final-assembly step every AI accelerator now bottlenecks on — through Amkor's Arizona campus. The most critical step in building an AI chip is being locked up on US soil for a decade. That's a supply chain digging in, not bracing for a bust.

## How the day treated us

This is the rare down day that was good to us. The book finished around **$99,200** — actually *up* about 0.7% from yesterday, even as the S&P fell 1.21%. The reason is the rotation above: we're heavy the AI-hardware names that rallied, and they more than offset the mega-cap drag. Vertiv led (+6.0%), the power-and-cooling supplier whose whole business is selling into the data-center build — a name that trades on its order book, not the Fed; Broadcom, Marvell, Dell and Micron chipped in. The biggest drag was Alphabet (−2.5%), the most rate-sensitive name we hold, with Nvidia and our Mag-7 ETF also leaning against us. The gainers won comfortably.

Our 25% cash reserve sat unchanged, and it stays there. We've said for weeks the conversation about deploying it starts on a real flush toward the low-7,400s-to-7,300s, where downside support clusters — we touched the top of that zone intraday and bounced. With the cushion thin, the Fed freshly hawkish, and both a triple-witching expiry and the Iran signing landing Friday, this is not the afternoon to spend insurance.

## One concept worth knowing

A single anonymous chart moved a trillion dollars today, so it's worth knowing what it is. Four times a year, alongside the rate decision, the Fed publishes the "dot plot" — a scatter of dots where each of the eighteen policymakers marks, anonymously, where they think the interest rate should be at year-end. No names, no votes, no commitment. It's a forecast, not a promise.

Why does a non-binding doodle move markets so violently? Because it's the closest thing to a peek inside the room. Today's hike-rate decision was unanimous and fully expected — zero new information. The dots were the news: they revealed that half the committee privately wants to *raise* rates this year, a stance the market had no idea was that widely held. The lesson for reading any Fed day: the rate decision is usually theater you've already priced; the projections and the press conference are where the surprises live. Watch the dots, not the headline.

## The bottom line

A new Fed chair told the market the easy-money lean is over, and a market with its shock absorber already deflated did what physics says it should: it broke the floor and swung hard. We saw the Fed coming and got caught betting the plumbing could absorb it — a fair miss, fully owned. But the day carried a quieter signal in our favor: the moment rates became the worry, money rotated *back* into the AI-hardware names that build the future, and our book rose on a red day because of it. We're a touch below where we started the month, a quarter in cash, watching a broken floor turn into a ceiling. The next two days are about whether the cushion rebuilds before Friday's expiry forces the issue. We'll see you after the bell.

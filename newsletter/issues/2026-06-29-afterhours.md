## The bounce we doubted this morning

This morning we called the relief rally suspect — green futures led by a Comcast breakup and a Rocket Lab deal, with the chips that actually drove last week's selloff still weak in Asia. Our line: watch whether semis turn green, because that's the difference between a real turn and a dead-cat carried by spinoff headlines.

They turned green and then some. The chip complex *led* the tape: SOXX up 4.1%, Taiwan Semi up 5.3%, Astera up 16.4%. The names that got sold on the AI-payoff doubt were the names institutions bought back first. That's not the texture of a hollow bounce — when the riskiest part of the market leads a relief rally, it's usually a vote, not a head-fake.

## The scorecard: two wins, one fade we missed, and an honest asterisk

**We were right twice, and we'll take it — with the fine print.** We said GOOGL would reclaim Buffett's $350 by today: it closed $353.65, up 4.8%, on its first day in the Dow. And we said the S&P would not close above 7,450: it closed 7,440.43, ten points under. Both graded right.

Now the honesty both deserve. The GOOGL call was a *level* read — the price that drew Berkshire would draw dip-buyers — but it took five straight closes *below* $350, plus a mechanical Dow-inclusion bid, plus a market-wide risk-on rally, to get the reclaim. A floor that needs all three to hold is softer than "Buffett's price is a floor" implied. We're right on the scoreboard; we won't pretend the $350 mark held tight, because it didn't.

The "no close above 7,450" call is the *shakiest* of our recent level wins, and we'd rather flag it than spike it. The S&P cleared the 7,424–7,428 shelf that had capped it for eight sessions and printed a 7,444 high — six points short of the line. The letter of the call held; the spirit ("the ceiling stays overhead, this is distribution") is now one good session from being wrong. We've gone three-for-three calling the market's failure to reclaim this flip. The next time we make that call, the index is sitting *at* the ceiling, not fifty points below it — and we'll price that.

**And the miss:** our YOLO fade idea this morning was built to short a relief rally that stalled at resistance. The rally didn't stall — it blew through 7,424–7,428 and held. The trade never triggered (no loss taken), but the read behind it was wrong, and the tell was our own: we conditioned the fade on the chips *lagging*. They led. We've written that down as a rule — don't fade a relief gap that the highest-beta names are leading; that's a regime bid, not a setup to short.

## Astera Steals the Day

**Astera Labs closed up 16.4% at $455.96, our best position on the board, after two desks re-based their price targets in one morning** — Stifel to $460 from $260, BofA to $450 from $240. The stock had already run roughly 200% in three months and joined the Nasdaq-100 last week; it gapped 16% anyway.

Here's what that move tells you. Astera makes the unglamorous connective silicon — the retimers and fabric switches that move data between GPUs inside an AI rack — and the sell-side just admitted its models were a cycle behind the order book. PCIe Gen 6 is now more than a third of the company's revenue, and the next-gen Nvidia and AMD racks need more of this content per box, not less. Our take: the thesis is now *in the price* — Astera trades above even the raised targets, and BofA's rating is only Neutral — so the easy multiple-expansion leg is spent. From here the August print has to validate the order book to hold this level. We own it, we're glad we do, and we're not chasing it higher.

## Toll-Takers Up, Toll-Payers Down

The cleaner story under the indexes is *who* led. The supply side — the companies that sell the picks and shovels — ripped: Taiwan Semi +5.3% (on reports of a foundry price hike and a quarterly tracker showing its revenue up 41% year-over-year), Marvell +4.1%, Dell +3.8%, Micron +1.1%. The demand side — the platforms that *buy* all that hardware — went red: Apple −0.7%, Microsoft −1.2%, Qualcomm −0.4%.

That split is the trade we've run since June 1: own the suppliers, underweight the platforms. It's had a rough two weeks — on the days the market doubted AI's payoff, the high-beta chips fell hardest and the split ran *against* us. Today it ran *for* us, cleanly, because a risk-on day rewards the names with the most torque. The pattern is now obvious enough to name: our thesis wins on the up days and loses on the doubt days. The September scorecard will tell us which regime dominated.

One name worth a flag for tomorrow: Qualcomm is reportedly in talks to buy AI-chip startup Tenstorrent for $8–10 billion — its third data-center acquisition in months. The prize is software that lets customers run their AI models on non-Nvidia chips without rewriting code: a direct chip at Nvidia's biggest moat. It's a third would-be challenger funding an attack on CUDA. Years from shipping, but worth watching.

Just under that sits the flip — roughly 7,420 to 7,450, depending on whose math you use — the zone where dealer hedging switches from amplifying moves to dampening them. The S&P closed *inside* that zone for the first time in nine sessions. Translation: the market spent eight days unable to even approach this ceiling, and tonight it's leaning on it. That's progress, but it's a boundary, not a breakout.

Our read: **the ceiling holds its first test.** An eight-session ceiling rarely breaks on the first touch, and tonight's bounce was narrow — the platforms lagged, and quarter-end window-dressing (which bids the quarter's winners into tomorrow's close) reverses Wednesday. We'd need a fresh catalyst — a dovish Warsh, a cool jobs number — to carry a real break, and we don't have it yet. So we'll call it: **no S&P close above 7,450 on Tuesday or Wednesday.** Wrong if it closes above — in which case the cap we've leaned on for three weeks finally broke, and we'll say so. ## One concept worth knowing: window-dressing

Tomorrow is the last day of the quarter, and that changes how the tape trades for mechanical reasons. Many fund managers are judged on what their holdings look like *at quarter-end* — the snapshot that shows up in client statements. So into the final day, there's a tendency to buy more of what already worked (so the report shows you owned the winners) and clean out the losers. This quarter the winners were chips. That's part of why the semis caught a bid today and may catch another tomorrow — and it's exactly why we discount some of it: those flows stop on July 1. A rally that needs the calendar to keep going isn't the same as a rally the fundamentals are driving.

## The bottom line

The market got the relief it was set up for, and the chips — the part we doubted this morning — led it. That's a genuinely more bullish tape than we've had in three weeks: breadth confirmed, fear faded, the suppliers we own back in front. But the S&P is leaning on a ceiling it hasn't cleared since June, the platforms still aren't joining, and the AI-payoff doubt that started the selloff hasn't been answered — only out-shouted by an Iran headline. One close above 7,440 changes the regime. We don't think it comes tomorrow. We'd love to be wrong.

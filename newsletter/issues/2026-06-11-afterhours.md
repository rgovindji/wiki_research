## The bluff got called before lunch

This morning the question was whether Iran's declared closure of the Strait of Hormuz was a blockade or a press release. By mid-afternoon the President had answered it from the other side: Trump canceled the strikes planned for tonight, said the settlement with Iran is "largely done," floated a signing as early as this weekend in Europe, and promised the strait "officially opens" the moment ink hits paper. His commentary on what that does to crude — "you'll have oil prices dropping like a rock" — was the rare presidential market call that executed within the hour. West Texas crude fell more than 4% to settle at $85.94, its lowest since April, through a strait that is still, officially, declared closed by the country that declared it.

Stocks did what stocks do when the worst headline on the board gets withdrawn: the S&P 500 rose 1.75% to 7,394.30, the Nasdaq gained 2.54%, the Dow rose 930 points back above 50,000, and small caps — the most rate-and-recession-sensitive corner of the market — led everything at +3%.

Here's what makes the day genuinely interesting rather than just green: the rally ran straight through an ugly inflation report. May wholesale prices rose 1.1% in a month against a 0.7% forecast — 6.5% over the year, the hottest since 2022. The market's first reaction was real (futures faded, the index opened down and touched 7,257) and its second reaction was smarter: nearly 80% of the surge was goods, and nearly 80% of that was energy — wholesale gasoline up 23.4% in a month of war. That's a report describing May's war premium on the very day June's peace was refunding it in the crude pit. The 10-year Treasury yield *fell* to 4.46% on a 6.5% wholesale inflation print. Bonds traded the direction prices are going, not the direction they came from.

One number in the report doesn't wash out with the war, and we're not letting the rally bury it: core wholesale prices rose 0.4% — the second firm domestic-inflation reading in three days, after Wednesday's hot services number. The Fed meets in six days holding both.

## Scorecard: one right, one expensive lesson

The ledger moved twice today, so here's both — the win briefly, the loss in full, as is the house rule.

The win: last night we logged that this stays a wide-swing market until the index reclaims roughly 7,420–7,450, with at least one of the next three sessions swinging 1.5% or more top to bottom. Today did it on the first try — a 2.1% range, low 7,257 to high 7,413, while staying below that ceiling all day. The right-reason check matters here: we deliberately wrote that call direction-agnostic, and the day vindicated exactly that part — the same amplification that turned Wednesday into a one-way slide turned today into a 155-point upside chase. We didn't predict peace breaking out. We predicted the market would exaggerate whatever showed up. Ledger: 1 right, 1 wrong.

The loss: this morning's paper trade — a same-day 7,400 call, triggered if the index reclaimed 7,355 — did almost everything right and still went to zero. The trigger fired, on the Iran headlines rather than the inflation print, which is precisely why we'd rewritten the rules to key on the level and not the catalyst. The squeeze ran 155 points, exactly the mechanism we described. At the day's high of 7,412.68 the trade was solidly in the money with hours to expiry. And then the rally stalled eight points short of our 7,420 target, drifted to a 7,394.30 close, and a same-day option that was right about everything expired worthless — six points out of the money. The autopsy is uncomfortable in a useful way: our card had two exits, the target and the stop, and the market chose *neither* — and with an expiring option, "neither" always pays the house. The fix is in the playbook tonight: every same-day structure now carries a third exit, out by 3:30 ET if neither line has been hit. Walls are magnets, not promises. The desk's record: 1 win, 1 loss, 1 no-trigger.

The rest of the board: our call that oil — not headlines — is the true gauge of this war just survived its hardest conceivable test (Iran declared the strait shut; crude fell 4%; we grade it formally Monday). Marvell closed at $280.71, twelve percent above the $250 line we drew — that test has become an easy pass that proves little, and the real question moves to whether June 22 is a sell-the-news date. And our Monday call that the index closes above ~7,440 by tomorrow needs 0.6% in one session, with new resistance in the way — alive, barely, graded tomorrow either way.

## Adobe beat everything and the market shrugged. Then its CFO joined a chip company.

After the close, Adobe reported the quarter the AI-eats-software crowd said it couldn't: revenue up 13% to $6.62 billion, earnings beat, full-year guidance raised, and the headline stat — AI-specific recurring revenue *tripled* from a year ago to more than $500 million. The stock fell about 5% after hours anyway, on top of 6% during the regular session, to levels last seen years ago. The stated reason: CFO Dan Durn is leaving on Sunday.

Where he's going is the actual story. Durn is joining Marvell — the AI-networking chipmaker we own, eleven days from joining the S&P 500, up 11% today. A top-tier finance executive walked out of one of the great software franchises into the silicon layer, and on the same day the market did the same trade in size: Oracle fell another 8.5% to $184.10 the day after booking $85 billion in new AI contracts, Salesforce scraped 52-week lows, Adobe sold off through a beat — while Micron rose 11.7%, Marvell 11.1%, Astera Labs 11.1%, Vertiv 6%, Dell 5.9%.

Here's the mechanism underneath, because it's the most important thing in this market right now: every dollar of the AI buildout is *spent by* one set of companies and *collected by* another. Oracle's $95 billion of coming capital expenditure terrified Oracle's shareholders — and a meaningful slice of it lands, eventually, as revenue at Dell, Vertiv, and Micron. The market is paying the toll-takers and de-rating the toll-payers, and today it did both at maximum volume. Micron got a fresh push from Wolfe Research: a $1,250 price target built on memory prices roughly tripling by year-end — math that gets tested in public when Micron reports in about two weeks.

Our take, both barrels: we're positioned on the right side of this rotation on purpose, and today paid for it. But we'd note what the crowd isn't pricing — Adobe's $500 million of tripling AI revenue is the first hard evidence that software can *charge* for AI, and it got sold on a personnel headline. We logged it: if Adobe can't close back above $233.38 — where it sat before the print — by next Friday, the software de-rate is a regime, not a mood. If it can, the whole "AI eats software" trade is more crowded than correct.

One more grade we owe from this morning: CoreWeave priced its $3.5 billion of bonds — 9.625% on the dollar notes, 8.5% on the euros. The honest read is a pass, not honors. The door to credit markets is open for AI infrastructure — that's the tripwire we watch on this position, and it didn't fire. But nine-and-five-eighths is not cheap money, and the stock's dead-flat close on a +2.5% Nasdaq day says the market noticed the price tag too.

## One concept worth knowing

**Stale on arrival.** Inflation reports are photographs of last month; futures markets are video of right now. May's wholesale-price report — gasoline up 23% — printed at 8:30 this morning, while six feet away on every trading floor, July crude futures were collapsing 4% because the war that caused the gasoline spike was ending. Bonds rallied *on* a hot inflation day because traders price the derivative, not the level: what matters isn't where prices were, but where the thing that drove them is going. The practical rule for reading any economic release: before reacting to the number, ask whether its cause is still alive. Today the cause was being negotiated away in real time, so a 6.5% inflation print bought a 930-point Dow rally. The exception that keeps this honest — and the Fed's eyes on the 17th — is the part of inflation with no war to blame: services prices, hot two readings running, with no off-ramp to trade against.

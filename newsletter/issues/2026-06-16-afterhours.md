## A record high you didn't feel

Here's a sentence that captures the whole day: the Dow Jones Industrial Average closed at an all-time record, and our portfolio had one of its worst sessions in two weeks. Both of those are true, and the gap between them *is* the story.

The Dow rose 0.64% to a record 51,999.67, touching 52,190 intraday. Underneath that headline, the S&P 500 actually *fell* 0.57% to 7,511.35 and the Nasdaq dropped 1.15%. That divergence — the blue-chip average up at a record while the broad and tech indexes slide — only happens when investors are doing one specific thing: rotating. Selling what's worked and buying what hasn't. Today, what's worked is the AI chip complex, and money walked out of it with purpose. Marvell, which we own, fell 9.8% — handing back nearly the entire 10.4% it gained Monday. Micron dropped 6.2%, Broadcom 4.4%, and outside our book AMD lost 5.9% and Intel 7%. The proceeds went into oil-sensitive cyclicals and value names that have lagged all year — the stuff that fills out the Dow.

The temptation is to read a 10% drop in your biggest winner as the bubble finally cracking. We don't think that's what this is, and the reason matters. Nothing happened today to challenge the *demand* for AI compute. Quite the opposite: the day's two biggest AI headlines — SpaceX agreeing to buy the AI-coding company behind Cursor for $60 billion, and a reported $30 billion compute deal between SpaceX and Google — were both arrows pointing *up* on demand. When the sector falls on a day with positive demand news, that's a positioning move, not a fundamental one. People sold chips because chips were the crowded, profitable trade to trim the day before a Fed meeting — not because they stopped believing in them.

## The scorecard: a fade we never got to make

No prediction came due today, but the trading desk had a live ticket, and the honest read on it is instructive.

This morning we deliberately flipped our recent pattern. The desk had lost twice in a row buying melt-ups and setting the exit one wall too far, so today's paper trade was the opposite: a bet *against* a record. The plan was to fade a poke into the 7,590–7,600 zone — to sell the rejection if the market tried the record and failed. The rule was strict: no poke into that zone, no trade.

The poke never came. The morning's small futures gain evaporated by the opening bell, and the S&P's high for the entire day was 7,564.96 — about 25 points short of the trigger. So the desk stood aside and did nothing. Status: no-trigger. The record record is now 1 win, 2 losses, 2 no-trigger.

And here's the part we're obligated to tell you, because it's the uncomfortable one: the *direction* was dead right. The market faded all day and closed at 7,511 — below the level our trade was built to target. A put bought at the open would have paid. But the entry we'd defined required a record-poke to fade, and the tape gave us a straight slide lower with no record to reject. This is the second time in a week the desk has correctly *declined* a trade that would have made money, because the specific setup didn't appear. That's not a bug — it's the whole point of keeping score. A desk that improvises an entry every time it "feels" right isn't a desk, it's a mood. We'd rather miss a winner we didn't have a signal for than take a loser we talked ourselves into.

Quietly, the slower-moving calls are aging well. Our bet that the market couldn't close above the 7,600 record before Friday looks comfortable — the high didn't even reach the wall today. Our "calm week" call has now passed its second test (a 0.75% range on a down day). And the Bank of Japan, which raised rates overnight for the first time since the 1990s, did so without breaking the yen — exactly the non-event we said it would be. Three for the price of patience.

## The cushion drained

Now the part that actually changes the setup, and it's invisible on a price chart.

We've spent all week explaining that this market has been *pinned* — that big options dealers, positioned a certain way, have been mechanically buying dips and selling rips, squeezing the daily range and protecting the tape in both directions. That protection has a size, and you can measure it. On Monday it was at a cycle high. Today it collapsed by roughly 80% in a single session — from about +$100 billion of dealer "gamma" to barely +$13 billion.

In plain terms: the shock absorber is still there, but it's most of the way deflated. The same selling that hit the chips also unwound a big chunk of the hedging that had been damping every move. So the market that walks into tomorrow's Fed decision is far less protected than the one that pinned so calmly last week. The close even landed right on the level — 7,510 — where dealers have stacked the most downside protection, the floor that now matters most. Below it, with so little cushion left, the machines that have been leaning *against* moves have much less firepower to do it.

That's the tension into Wednesday: a calm volatility reading (the VIX barely budged, at 16.4) sitting on top of a structurally thinner market. The options market isn't pricing much drama. The plumbing says there's room for some.

## How the day treated us

A rotation day is, by definition, a bad day for a portfolio built on the thing being rotated *out of*. The book slipped to roughly $98,500 of notional — back just below where we started on June 1. The 25% cash reserve we've defended for weeks did exactly its job: it sat there earning a placeholder while three-quarters of the book took the hit, and it's the reason a brutal day in semis cost us about a percent and a half instead of double that.

The bright spots tell the rotation story from the inside. Our two *demand-side* names were the two that rose: CoreWeave jumped 9.7% — the AI-cloud landlord catching a bid from those very SpaceX compute headlines — and Alphabet added 1.1%, the cheapest mega-cap and a beneficiary of the reported Google compute deal. Everything that *makes* the chips fell; the names that *rent out* or *use* the compute held or gained. That's worth sitting with, because it cuts against one of our standing bets — that the supply side of this trade (the chipmakers) beats the demand side (the platforms) over time. For one day, it went the other way, decisively. One day doesn't resolve a call we've given until September, and the logic — that the picks-and-shovels capture the cycle's economics — is intact. But intellectual honesty means saying plainly: today the scoreboard favored the other team.

What we'd do with the cash: nothing yet. We've said the conversation starts if the tape gaps down toward the 7,440–7,450 area where the dealers' grip loosens. We're at 7,511, above that, with a Fed meeting that could break either way tomorrow. Adding into the day before a coin-flip event, when our names just took a 5-to-10% haircut that might keep going for a session, is how you spend your insurance early. We wait.

The floor is **7,510** — the strike where dealers hold the most downside protection, and where today's close pinned almost to the dollar. The catalyst that decides which wall gives is tomorrow at 2 PM Eastern: the Federal Reserve's decision and **Kevin Warsh's first press conference as chair.** Almost nobody expects a rate change. What the market is bracing for is the *voice* — whether Warsh drops the Fed's easing lean and signals a more neutral, slightly hawkish posture, with oil at $80 handing the doves cover and hot services inflation handing the hawks theirs. With the dealer cushion drained, his tone can move this tape more than the last week of quiet grinding would suggest.

So two calls into it. First, the boxed range: **we expect the S&P to close Wednesday inside that 7,510–7,600 band** — neither the floor breaks nor the record falls on Warsh's debut. We're wrong below 7,510 (the thin cushion let a hawkish read flush it toward the 7,450 trapdoor) or above 7,600 (a dovish read took out the record). Second, the rotation: **we think today's chip dump was a trim, not a turn** — our semis should outperform the Dow over the next three sessions. We're wrong if the chips keep lagging into Friday, or if any real AI-demand bad news shows up to justify the selling.

## One concept worth knowing

Today is the cleanest example yet of why we keep talking about dealer "gamma." Think of it as the market's suspension system. When option dealers are positioned the way they were last week, their required hedging runs *against* every move — they sell strength and buy weakness automatically, which smooths the ride. But that cushion isn't fixed. It's a quantity, and it can drain. When a big, one-directional sell-off hits — like today's chip dump — a lot of that protective positioning gets unwound at once. The suspension doesn't vanish, but it goes soft. The lesson for reading tomorrow: the same Fed sentence that would have bounced off a fully-cushioned market last week can leave a dent in a market whose shock absorber just lost 80% of its travel. Calm price, thinner protection — that's the setup, and it's why we're watching 7,510 more closely than the VIX.

## The bottom line

A record-high Dow and a portfolio in the red is not a contradiction — it's a rotation, and rotations are how bull markets rest without rolling over. Nothing today said the AI trade is broken; the demand headlines said the opposite, and the names that *use* the compute rose even as the names that *build* it fell. What did change is mechanical: the market's protective cushion deflated right before the one event this week that can actually move it. We're a hair below water, comfortably in cash, and content to let the new Fed chair show his hand before we do anything with it. The wall above, the floor below, a new voice at 2 PM. We'll see you after the bell.

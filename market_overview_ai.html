Investing in the AI Capex Cycle

A reading-mode synthesis of the wiki's investment view — what we're betting on, what we're worried about, and which names the market hasn't caught onto yet. From ./ — full source in wiki/

The AI capital-expenditure cycle is the dominant equity story of 2026 — roughly one trillion dollars of supply-chain capex this year alone, and about 40% of S&P 500 earnings growth attributable to it. Our base case is that the cycle has years left. But the binding physical constraint moves down the stack each year — and the right portfolio rotates with it. Pricing power lives wherever the binding constraint is.

What follows is the working synthesis as of mid-May 2026. Four shifts have reshaped the wiki's thinking since the last refresh — they're worth understanding before the section-by-section detail.

Four shifts that define this cycle now

1. TSMC has become an AI company

The single most important data point of the past month is buried in TSMC's 2025 annual report. NVDA overtook AAPL as TSMC's number-one customer: 19% of 2025 revenue versus Apple at 17%, down from 22% the prior year. Apple lost five percentage points of TSMC's wallet in a single year. The structural deprioritization the wiki has been writing about since [[2026-05-09-dwarkesh-dylan-semianalysis]] has now happened at observable speed, with Apple bleeding share at roughly five points per year. The corollary is just as significant: TSMC's roadmap is now optimized for AI customer requirements — A14 in 2028, the new COUPE photonic engine in 2026, a $20B injection into TSMC Arizona equal to roughly a third of FY26 capex. The wiki's AAPL bear case is no longer forward-looking; it is mid-event.

2. Smart money has gone defensive — but not on AI

Q1 2026 13F filings (mid-May) show a remarkable convergence among multiple tier-one hedge funds. Berkshire trimmed Apple, exited Amazon and UnitedHealth entirely, sold heavy Chevron, and pushed its top five holdings to 68% of portfolio — maximum defensiveness. Dan Loeb established a large SPY put position. Druckenmiller exited Alphabet (385K shares) and slashed Amazon 97%. Yet two of those same funds — Ackman and Loeb — initiated or massively expanded MSFT positions in the same quarter (Ackman +$2.09B, Loeb +175%). And among AI-stack insiders, TSM is the only Mag-7-adjacent name showing CEO buying (C.C. Wei accumulated through ESPP April 9). Everyone else — NVDA, AMD, MU, AVGO, GOOGL — is on systematic 10b5-1 selling. The smart-money read is not "exit AI" — it is "rotate within AI, hedge the macro tape, raise cash for what's coming."

3. The bottleneck is fragmenting into parallel constraints

For three years the bottleneck thesis had a single sequence: packaging in 2023, power in 2024, memory in 2025-26, fabs in 2027, EUV in 2028-30. That sequence still holds — but in 2025-26 multiple parallel constraints have emerged. x86 CPU supply is now bottlenecked alongside memory (Intel's Q1 server ASPs +27% YoY on -5% volume; AMD up 38% YoY in revenue vs Intel +7% — both supply-limited). The substrate layer has split into three parallel bottlenecks: photonic-SOI for the passive silicon photonic chip (SOITEC at 95% share), InP substrates for the active laser source (AXTI ~36%; Sumitomo ~42%), AND — added 2026-05-17 PM after Taiwan media reporting — ABF substrates for advanced IC packaging (AJINY = Ajinomoto 95%+ film monopolist; UNIMICRON + Ibiden the leading substrate makers; supply shortfall projected 42% by 2028; Ajinomoto raising film prices 30% Q3 2026 — first major price action in years). And the cross-cycle physical commodity layer — rare-earth NdFeB magnets — moved from theoretical concern to active production constraint when Musk publicly confirmed Tesla Optimus production has been hit by China's export controls (3.5kg of magnets per humanoid; MP Materials is the only fully integrated US producer). The bottleneck framework still works — but the right portfolio now holds six constraint-layer plays simultaneously, not one.

4. The macro tail risk is BoJ June 16-17

The single most underpriced scheduled event of 2026 is the Bank of Japan's June meeting. A 25-basis-point hike to 1.0% is priced into swaps. The risk is what happens after — Morgan Stanley estimates roughly $500B of yen-carry-trade positions remain on, meaning only about 10% of the trade unwound in August 2024's vol shock. If June 16-17 produces either a 50bp surprise or hawkish forward guidance, the carry-trade unwind dynamic that briefly took the VIX to 65 in August 2024 could reset — but this time without an automatic Fed-cut differential to dampen it. The wiki has built a dedicated [[bank-of-japan]] macro page and a three-tier [[2026-06-16-boj-decision]] hedge-and-profit playbook (pure hedge / asymmetric / aggressive) with explicit instruments. This is the position where most US tech holders will discover they're under-hedged.

What the market hasn't fully priced

Three name-level data points have surfaced in the past month that we believe the market has not fully digested:

GOOGL has a hidden $100B SpaceX exposure. Google bought roughly 5% of SpaceX for $500M in 2015. At the $1.75-2T valuation in SpaceX's confidential S-1 (filed April 1; public S-1 May 15-22; roadshow June 8; IPO June 18-30), Alphabet's stake is worth approximately $100 billion mark-to-market. Even at a conservative $1.2T post-IPO trading valuation it remains a $60B+ position. Sell-side models of Alphabet do not currently incorporate this. The Other-Bets segment is about to add a single line worth more than most public companies.

The AI-accelerator IPO market has effectively closed. NVIDIA acquired Groq for $20 billion (assets only) in December 2025 — the largest M&A in NVDA's history. SambaNova is in talks to be acquired by Intel at $1.6B, a down-round from its $5B 2021 peak. Tenstorrent is expected to be acquired by AMD or Intel. After CBRS's May 13 IPO (priced $185, opened $350, closed $311 day one; back to ~$280 by day two; lock-up Nov 16, 2026), the only standing pure-play AI-accelerator alternative to NVIDIA is publicly tradeable — and is therefore a single point of failure for that thesis. The implication is that the only credible non-NVDA path now runs through AVGO, which Counterpoint projects will hold 60% of the custom hyperscaler ASIC market by 2027 in a $100B+ TAM.

The MATCH Act is a material structural risk to ASML that isn't in consensus. The bipartisan US legislation (House Foreign Affairs advanced April 22) specifically targets ASML and Tokyo Electron equipment sales and — critically — services to named Chinese fabs (CXMT, SMIC, YMTC, Huawei, Hua Hong). Probability of 2026 passage is estimated at 55-70% per the wiki's research synthesis. If passed, expected ASML revenue impact is -10 to -15%. The mitigating factor: ASML's China revenue has already dropped to 19% in Q1 2026 (down from 36% in Q4 2025), meaning the marginal impact is smaller than headline numbers suggest. ASML remains a high-conviction multi-year hold, but the downside tail is explicit and not currently priced into the multiple.

Six Working Theses

1. The picks-and-shovels remain the highest-conviction holds

The infrastructure layer is where demand is contracted rather than extrapolated. NVDA's Blackwell platform is sold out through mid-2026 with backlog cascading into Vera Rubin; the company has secured $90B in long-term supply contracts and is set to capture over 70% of TSMC's 3nm wafer capacity by 2027. TSM guides 2026 revenue growth near 30% in USD terms; ASML's EUV monopoly underpins the entire advanced-node manufacturing base. These three remain the cleanest long-term semis holds despite their valuations.

2. Hyperscalers buy AND monetize — pick your spot carefully

MSFT, AMZN, GOOGL, and META together account for the largest share of 2026 AI capex but also represent the customer base that will determine whether the spend pays back. GOOGL trades at the cheapest multiple in the group (forward P/E around 29.7) and has the only credible NVIDIA alternative through TPU — designed in partnership with AVGO — which means part of the TPU economics flow to Broadcom. Among the hyperscalers, GOOGL is the most defensible long-term hold; META carries the most option-value through its parallel AMD 6 GW deal and AVGO MTIA partnership.

3. Be cautious on consumer tech at premium multiples

TSLA trades at roughly 178x 2026 earnings on a valuation that depends almost entirely on robotaxi and Optimus optionality — neither of which has been validated commercially. We have flipped AAPL from neutral to bear in May 2026: TSMC is structurally deprioritizing Apple on the leading edge (the A16 node's first customer is an AI accelerator, not Apple), and the memory crunch is set to halve global smartphone unit volumes from 1.4B to roughly 550M by 2027, adding ~$150 to iPhone BOM. The services flywheel and capital return discipline are intact, but the secular setup is materially worse than it was at the start of 2026.

4. The non-NVIDIA hedge is real and concrete

The single most material non-NVDA AI play is AVGO. Broadcom designs custom AI accelerators for six confirmed hyperscaler customers and has now disclosed Project Titan — a 10 GW OpenAI custom inference engine targeting mass production in late 2026. AI revenue grew 106% year-over-year to $8.4 billion last quarter, and management targets $100 billion in AI sales by 2027. Whenever a hyperscaler shifts incremental dollars away from Nvidia, Broadcom is the disproportionate beneficiary. AMD is the credible second GPU vendor: a 6 GW OpenAI deal, a 6 GW Meta deal with a 160-million-share warrant, and Oracle deploying 50,000 MI450s in new AI superclusters. The Helios rack ships in Q3 2026 on TSMC 2nm — the first credible answer to Nvidia's NVL72 rack-scale moat.

5. The overlooked downstream: data center construction

While the market obsesses over what goes inside the racks, the contractors physically building the data centers carry roughly $76 billion of combined contracted backlog across three names. FIX (Comfort Systems) is the pure-play mechanical and HVAC contractor — Q1 2026 backlog reached $12.46B (+81% year-over-year), and 45% of revenue is data center work. EME (EMCOR Group) is the largest US mechanical-electrical contractor with $15.62B in remaining performance obligations and a raised FY26 EPS guide of $28.25–$29.75. PWR (Quanta Services) handles transmission and grid — a record $48.5 billion backlog, including a NiSource engagement to build approximately 3 GW of generation capacity for a major load customer. These names are classified as industrials, not technology — they don't appear in AI ETFs, and they don't get covered by AI-focused analysts. The labor bottleneck the wiki's Bottleneck Roadmap describes for 2030+ is monetized here.

6. The invisible fab layer

Outside the wafer-fab equipment bucket — and therefore outside the obvious AI screens — three companies quietly capture content from the 2027 fab buildout. TER (Teradyne) is the dominant Western chip-test equipment vendor; Q1 2026 revenue rose 87% year-over-year to a record $1.282 billion, with roughly 70% tied to AI and compute now accounting for 75% of SoC product revenue. ENTG (Entegris) supplies the FOUP">FOUPs (front-opening unified pods), ultra-pure materials, and gas-and-liquid filtration systems that every advanced fab needs — content per wafer rises mechanically with chip complexity. APD (Air Products) just announced its largest semiconductor investment in company history: industrial gas plants for Samsung's Pyeongtaek fab expansion under 15-20 year build-own-operate contracts, with new facilities coming on phased between 2028 and 2030. Three different industry classifications, same demand driver.

ASML's EUV tooling becomes the binding constraint in the 2028-2030 period — and possibly the most strategically important bottleneck of the entire decade.

The ASML caveat matters. That is below what Sam Altman and Elon Musk have publicly aspired to. What Could Go Wrong

Four counter-narratives deserve serious weight, not dismissal.

First, the bubble framing. CAPE at 39.8 is the highest reading since the 2000 peak; the Buffett indicator sits between 217% and 228% of GDP. Top-10 stock concentration in the S&P 500 matches the dot-com peak. Vanguard's framing — "economic upside, stock market downside" — captures it: AI may be GDP-positive and stock-negative if priced for monopoly outcomes that fragment. The Q1 2026 13Fs filed mid-May suggest tier-one smart money is taking this seriously: Berkshire at maximum defensiveness, Loeb's SPY put position, multiple funds rotating from giants to picks-and-shovels.

Second, the yen-carry tail. The single most underpriced scheduled risk for AI/tech is the Bank of Japan's June 16-17 decision. A 25bp hike is consensus, but the structural setup — roughly $500B of yen-carry positions still on (Morgan Stanley), no Fed differential support to dampen the unwind, NVDA at ~280x forward PE — means a hawkish surprise could trigger a multi-week reset of the August 2024 vol shock without the rapid recovery. Hedges are cheap relative to the position size of most tech portfolios.

Third, the depreciation question. The wiki has surfaced a genuine analytical contradiction. Dylan Patel argues GPU useful life is 7-8 years based on secondary-market resigning behavior. A Google data center architect, quoted in industry forum discussion, puts operational service life at 1-3 years. NVIDIA's warranty reserves have crept to ~1.8% of revenue and are rising. The reconciliation is probably that GPUs are physically capable for 7-8 years but economically retired earlier on generational obsolescence. The empirical test is NVDA's warranty disclosure in Tuesday's Q1 FY27 10-Q. If that line keeps rising, the Burry-style depreciation bear case is back on the table.

Fourth, the demand-side question. Dylan Patel's view is that even Nvidia is building "X-1" while OpenAI and Anthropic want X — the supply chain consistently underestimates demand. The bear framing is that hyperscaler capex is itself the demand and could pause. The single most important leading indicator we watch is the first hyperscaler quarterly capex cut. None has come yet — the Q1 2026 revisions actually went the other way, with MSFT and GOOGL guiding 2026 capex up to $190B each.

Open Questions

  • Does ASML finally take pricing power in 2028–2030, or remain the generous monopolist? Q1 2026 evidence (53% gross margin vs TSMC 66.2% and NVDA 75%) suggests not yet. Single highest-leverage variable for the multi-year semi thesis.
  • What does NVDA's warranty reserve trajectory in Tuesday's 10-Q tell us about GPU useful life? Empirical resolution of the Dylan-vs-Google-architect contradiction.
  • Does the MATCH Act pass in 2026? Probability ~55-70% per the wiki's research synthesis. If yes, what's the actual realized ASML revenue impact?
  • Does BoJ deliver a vanilla 25bp June hike or surprise hawkish? Tail-risk magnitude is asymmetric — pricing implies mostly priced-in; size of yen-carry remaining suggests it isn't.
  • When does the first hyperscaler cut AI capex guidance? Most important single leading indicator of cycle peak. None yet.
  • How to size NVDA + GOOGL + MSFT + META given correlated AI exposure? Owning all four is one bet, not four.
  • If AI timelines are long (2035+), does China dominate semis? Indigenized DUV is coming; EUV later. Fast-timelines, the US wins; long-timelines, China wins.

Where This Lands

The core long-term holds are unchanged: NVDA, TSM, ASML, MU, GOOGL, AVGO, and AMD on the obvious side, with FIX, EME, PWR, TER, ENTG, and APD as the under-followed contracted-backlog and fab-content names. The two bears are TSLA (valuation extremity, unproven optionality) and AAPL (TSMC deprioritization now quantified at five percentage points per year).

The most useful incremental additions from this month's research are at the seams between the AI-infrastructure cycle and adjacent themes. SOITEC is the substrate-layer chokepoint for the photonics transition — a single qualified volume supplier at 95% market share, binary on May 27 earnings. ISRG is the only operationally-proven, recurring-revenue robotics franchise in public markets — a cleaner risk-reward than speculative humanoid bets. PLTR and AVAV represent the defense-AI software and hardware sides — PLTR's Maven is now a $13B Pentagon program of record; AVAV trades at half PLTR's multiple on faster growth. MP Materials has graduated from theoretical to confirmed bottleneck after Musk publicly named China rare-earth controls as a binding constraint on Optimus.

None of these change the central thesis. They sharpen which picks-and-shovels best capture the rents at each constraint layer, and they extend the framework into adjacent domains that ride the same physical-buildout dynamics.

The bottom line

AI infrastructure is the multi-year engine. The cycle is real, contracted, and not yet peaking. But the binding constraint moves — and so should the positioning. Memory now, fabs in 2027, EUV in 2028-2030, labor and modularization in 2030+. Photonic-SOI substrates and x86 CPU shortage have emerged as parallel constraints in 2026. Rare-earth NdFeB is the cross-cycle commodity bottleneck now empirically validated (Musk publicly confirmed May 2026). The best returns over the next five years will go to investors who hold the picks-and-shovels through the cycle but rotate which picks-and-shovels as the bottleneck moves. Tactical near-term tail: BoJ June 16-17 is the most underpriced scheduled event of 2026.

Investing in the AI Capex Cycle

A reading-mode synthesis of the wiki's investment view — what we're betting on, what we're worried about, and which names the market hasn't caught onto yet. From ./ — full source in wiki/

The dominant equity story of 2026 is the AI capital expenditure supercycle. Roughly one trillion dollars of supply-chain capex is being deployed this year alone — about 40% of S&P 500 earnings growth is attributable to it. Our base case is that this cycle has years left, but the binding constraint moves down the stack each year: from chips in 2023 to memory in 2026 to ASML's EUV tools by 2028-2030. Pricing power lives wherever the binding constraint is.

What follows is the working synthesis. The full per-ticker theses, citations, and watch items live in the wiki itself — this is the reading-mode artifact.

The Macro Setup

Markets enter mid-May 2026 with the S&P 500 just above 7,400 — its first close above that level on May 10. The PHLX Semiconductor Index is up 66% year-to-date and is the leading sub-sector by a wide margin. The Federal Reserve held rates at 3.50–3.75% in April with an 8-4 dissent — the largest split since October 1992 — in what may have been Chair Powell's final meeting. Energy is sticky on US-Iran tensions, with WTI at $97.89 (+2.59% on the day). Forward S&P P/E is 20.9x against a five-year average of 19.9; CAPE sits at 39.8, the highest reading since the 2000 dot-com peak.

These valuation levels would be alarming in a slower-growing economy. They're tolerated because earnings growth is real: consensus calls for ~21% S&P EPS growth in 2026, and AI is doing most of that work. The structural question — to which we return below — is what happens when that engine slows.

Six Working Theses

1. The picks-and-shovels remain the highest-conviction holds

The infrastructure layer is where demand is contracted rather than extrapolated. NVDA's Blackwell platform is sold out through mid-2026 with backlog cascading into Vera Rubin; the company has secured $90B in long-term supply contracts and is set to capture over 70% of TSMC's 3nm wafer capacity by 2027. TSM guides 2026 revenue growth near 30% in USD terms; ASML's EUV monopoly underpins the entire advanced-node manufacturing base. These three remain the cleanest long-term semis holds despite their valuations.

2. Hyperscalers buy AND monetize — pick your spot carefully

MSFT, AMZN, GOOGL, and META together account for the largest share of 2026 AI capex but also represent the customer base that will determine whether the spend pays back. GOOGL trades at the cheapest multiple in the group (forward P/E around 29.7) and has the only credible NVIDIA alternative through TPU — designed in partnership with AVGO — which means part of the TPU economics flow to Broadcom. Among the hyperscalers, GOOGL is the most defensible long-term hold; META carries the most option-value through its parallel AMD 6 GW deal and AVGO MTIA partnership.

3. Be cautious on consumer tech at premium multiples

TSLA trades at roughly 178x 2026 earnings on a valuation that depends almost entirely on robotaxi and Optimus optionality — neither of which has been validated commercially. We have flipped AAPL from neutral to bear in May 2026: TSMC is structurally deprioritizing Apple on the leading edge (the A16 node's first customer is an AI accelerator, not Apple), and the memory crunch is set to halve global smartphone unit volumes from 1.4B to roughly 550M by 2027, adding ~$150 to iPhone BOM. The services flywheel and capital return discipline are intact, but the secular setup is materially worse than it was at the start of 2026.

4. The non-NVIDIA hedge is real and concrete

The single most material non-NVDA AI play is AVGO. Broadcom designs custom AI accelerators for six confirmed hyperscaler customers and has now disclosed Project Titan — a 10 GW OpenAI custom inference engine targeting mass production in late 2026. AI revenue grew 106% year-over-year to $8.4 billion last quarter, and management targets $100 billion in AI sales by 2027. Whenever a hyperscaler shifts incremental dollars away from Nvidia, Broadcom is the disproportionate beneficiary. AMD is the credible second GPU vendor: a 6 GW OpenAI deal, a 6 GW Meta deal with a 160-million-share warrant, and Oracle deploying 50,000 MI450s in new AI superclusters. The Helios rack ships in Q3 2026 on TSMC 2nm — the first credible answer to Nvidia's NVL72 rack-scale moat.

5. The overlooked downstream: data center construction

While the market obsesses over what goes inside the racks, the contractors physically building the data centers carry roughly $76 billion of combined contracted backlog across three names. FIX (Comfort Systems) is the pure-play mechanical and HVAC contractor — Q1 2026 backlog reached $12.46B (+81% year-over-year), and 45% of revenue is data center work. EME (EMCOR Group) is the largest US mechanical-electrical contractor with $15.62B in remaining performance obligations and a raised FY26 EPS guide of $28.25–$29.75. PWR (Quanta Services) handles transmission and grid — a record $48.5 billion backlog, including a NiSource engagement to build approximately 3 GW of generation capacity for a major load customer. These names are classified as industrials, not technology — they don't appear in AI ETFs, and they don't get covered by AI-focused analysts. The labor bottleneck the wiki's Bottleneck Roadmap describes for 2030+ is monetized here.

6. The invisible fab layer

Outside the wafer-fab equipment bucket — and therefore outside the obvious AI screens — three companies quietly capture content from the 2027 fab buildout. TER (Teradyne) is the dominant Western chip-test equipment vendor; Q1 2026 revenue rose 87% year-over-year to a record $1.282 billion, with roughly 70% tied to AI and compute now accounting for 75% of SoC product revenue. ENTG (Entegris) supplies the FOUP">FOUPs (front-opening unified pods), ultra-pure materials, and gas-and-liquid filtration systems that every advanced fab needs — content per wafer rises mechanically with chip complexity. APD (Air Products) just announced its largest semiconductor investment in company history: industrial gas plants for Samsung's Pyeongtaek fab expansion under 15-20 year build-own-operate contracts, with new facilities coming on phased between 2028 and 2030. Three different industry classifications, same demand driver.

The Bottleneck Roadmap

The most useful single frame we've found this cycle is Dylan Patel's (SemiAnalysis): the binding physical constraint on AI compute moves down the stack each year, and pricing power lives where the binding constraint is. Memory is the constraint right now. Cleanroom space (new fabs) will be the constraint in 2027. ASML's EUV tooling becomes the binding constraint in the 2028-2030 period — and possibly the most strategically important bottleneck of the entire decade.

The ASML caveat matters. By the wiki's math — roughly 3.5 EUV tools support 1 GW of AI compute, and cumulative ASML output by 2030 is projected at around 700 tools — the theoretical maximum AI capacity by end of decade is approximately 200 GW per year. That is below what Sam Altman and Elon Musk have publicly aspired to. The question for the multi-year semi thesis is whether ASML, historically the "generous monopolist" that hasn't taken price commensurate with its position, finally does — or whether the bottleneck rents stay diffused across the customer base. We hold high conviction on ASML over the multi-year horizon while flagging this pricing question as the single highest-leverage variable.

"ASML has never raised the price more than they've increased the capability of the tool. They've always provided net benefit to their customers." Dylan Patel, SemiAnalysis · 2026

What Could Go Wrong

Three counter-narratives deserve serious weight, not dismissal.

First, the bubble framing. CAPE at 39.8 is the highest reading since the 2000 peak; the Buffett indicator sits between 217% and 228% of GDP. Top-10 stock concentration in the S&P 500 matches the dot-com peak. The historical track record of these signals is poor for timing but strong for warning about long-horizon returns. Vanguard's framing — "economic upside, stock market downside" — captures it: AI may be GDP-positive and stock-negative if priced for monopoly outcomes that fragment.

Second, prior-gen depreciation. A May 2026 GQG (Rajiv Jain) channel check across more than a dozen NVIDIA resellers found H200 secondary-market discounts exceeding 50% versus a year earlier, with Blackwell units beginning to appear in secondary channels. This tensions Dylan Patel's "an H100 is worth more today than three years ago" framing for the previous generation specifically. The argument cuts both ways: current-gen demand remains contracted, but if newer-gen ramps faster than expected, n-1 silicon becomes a fast-depreciating asset rather than a long-duration revenue stream. We track GPU rental indices as a tactical signal but caution against single-source reads (Ornn diverges methodologically from Vast and RunPod).

Third, the demand-side question. The supply chain is over-supplying or under-supplying based on which framing you accept. Dylan Patel's view is that even Nvidia is building "X-1" while OpenAI and Anthropic want X — meaning the supply chain consistently underestimates demand. The bear framing is that hyperscaler capex is itself the demand and could pause. The single most important leading indicator we watch is the first hyperscaler quarterly capex cut. None has come yet.

Open Questions

  • Does ASML finally take pricing power in 2028–2030, or remain the generous monopolist? Single highest-leverage variable for the multi-year semi thesis.
  • Does SNDK's HBF (High-Bandwidth Flash) — vertically stacked NAND with 8-16x HBM capacity at lower cost — ship and scale? First samples expected 2H 2026. If it works, it threatens the HBM supercycle on a 2-3 year horizon.
  • When does the first hyperscaler cut AI capex guidance? Most important single leading indicator of cycle peak.
  • How to size NVDA + GOOGL + MSFT + META given correlated AI exposure? Owning all four is one bet, not four.
  • If AI timelines are long (2035+), does China dominate semis? Indigenized DUV is coming; EUV later. Fast-timelines, the US wins; long-timelines, China wins.

Where This Lands

Our highest-conviction long-term holds are NVDA, TSM, ASML, MU, GOOGL, AVGO, and AMD on the obvious side — with FIX, EME, PWR, TER, ENTG, and APD as the under-followed contracted-backlog and fab-content names. Our two bears are TSLA (valuation extremity, unproven optionality) and now AAPL (TSMC deprioritization, smartphone volume collapse).

The two conviction upgrades this week — BE on Oracle's $2.45 GW Project Jupiter primary-power contract, and SNDK on the HBF product — reflect the same pattern: real customer commitments that move a name from "alternative" to "primary at scale." The wiki tracks these qualitative shifts more carefully than quarter-to-quarter price action.

The bottom line

AI infrastructure is the multi-year engine. The cycle is real, contracted, and not yet peaking. But the binding constraint moves — and so should the positioning. Memory now, fabs in 2027, EUV in 2028-2030, labor and modularization in 2030+. The best returns over the next five years will go to investors who hold the picks-and-shovels through the cycle but rotate which picks-and-shovels as the bottleneck moves.

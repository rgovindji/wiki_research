The bottleneck trade is ending. The next game is franchise value.

A risk-off session before the first Warsh Fed handed us a live preview of the rotation Gavin Baker just called — the AI supply chain's scarcity rents are cyclical, and the market started pricing it.

As of 2026-06-16 (post-close) · synthesis of June 8–16 wiki ingests · data as dated inline · not investment advice

For a year, the winning trade was simple: own whatever the AI buildout was short of. HBM memory, CoWoS packaging, photonic substrates, power. Scarcity created pricing power, and the bottleneck names re-rated violently — MU to a trillion dollars, AXTI up 600%, AAOI twelve-fold. This week the most-cited voice in our own research called time on it.

"The bottleneck trade is nearing its end. That was the game for the last year. The next game is what has enduring franchise value on the other side of these bottlenecks — whenever that is."

— Gavin Baker, Atreides Management

He's right in principle, and the principle is old: bottleneck rents are self-liquidating. The same high prices that reward the scarce supplier fund the capex that ends the scarcity. A bottleneck trade is a cyclical trade wearing a secular costume — and you can tell the crowd has stopped noticing the difference when cyclical rents start getting secular multiples. MU is now valued as "infrastructure, not commodity." AAOI trades at ~14× forward sales as a module maker that CPO eventually disrupts. That's the tell.

June 16 was the rotation in miniature

On a day the BoJ hiked to 1% (first time since 1995) and the desk de-risked into Warsh's first FOMC, the split was almost too clean: the supply-side book was routed, while demand-side platforms, franchises, and value held. The Dow set a record the same afternoon the Nasdaq fell more than a percent.

Don't over-read one day
One risk-off session before a Fed is a positioning trim, not a proven trend — no negative demand datapoint landed (the week's AI news was net-positive). But it's the strongest single-day evidence yet for Baker's rotation, and it lines up with a structural call from a source the wiki already trusts. Treat it as a reason to start tilting, not to flip the book.

The actionable split: not "sell the bottleneck," but sort it

The useful move isn't dumping every scarce-input name. It's separating, within the bottleneck basket, the companies that keep pricing power after supply catches up from the ones whose entire thesis is the shortage.

Durable franchise — also bottlenecked

Keeps pricing power on the other side

  • TSM — process lead + capacity discipline + customer lock-in outlast the wafer shortage
  • ASML — EUV monopoly is structural, not cyclical
  • MU — but only the HBM4 franchise (Vera Rubin lock-in), not commodity DRAM

Pure cyclical rent-capturer

Owns little once the input is abundant

  • AAOI — pluggable optics; the layer CPO disrupts
  • AXTI — InP substrate scarcity play
  • Commodity DRAM / NAND — beta to the memory cycle

And the genuine open question: NVDA. Is CUDA + systems + pace a durable franchise, or is NVDA itself the bottleneck — GPU scarcity — that compresses as custom ASICs and the new Intel-foundry diversification add supply? Baker's framing puts the most important stock in the market on trial.

The week's supporting signals

  • UBS calls a "generational" boom — and that's the warning. Industry sell-in $1.62T (2026) → $2.38T (2027); memory $961B → $1.638T; cycle "green into late 2027." Bullish on the demand lens — but when the structural-shortage call becomes Street consensus, the contrarian edge is gone. Filed as contradicting evidence against the wiki's late-cycle warnings; both views kept.
  • Diversification off TSMC has begun. Google ordered >3 million TPUs from Intel Foundry for 2028 (via EMIB-T packaging); Nvidia is evaluating Intel too. First real external AI-foundry win for Intel — and a slow, multi-year share-of-marginal-growth risk for TSMC. Exactly the kind of "other side of the bottleneck" question Baker means.
  • Photonics is the next bottleneck moving closer to compute. New photonics sector coverage maps the pluggables → LPO → CPO roadmap — where today's module franchise (AAOI, COHR, LITE) gets disrupted by co-packaged optics even as the optical TAM grows. Own the layer, not just the name.
  • Correction worth flagging. CRWV's +9.7% wasn't generic "AI demand" — it was Nasdaq-100 inclusion (June 22) + a $2.2B 15-year Chicago data-center lease + a Cantor $167 target. Demand-side, contracted, company-specific catalysts on a day the supply side bled: the rotation, again.

What tells you the rotation is real

Baker's own hedge — "whenever that is" — is doing the heavy lifting. The wiki's evidence argues against being early: HBM sold out through 2027-28, GPU rental booked out, the whole supply chain still building "X-minus-1." The price peak typically front-runs the fundamental resolution by quarters, so don't wait for supply to visibly catch up. Watch the second derivative instead:

  • Memory contract prices decelerating (the first negative month-over-month HBM/DRAM ASP print)
  • CoWoS / advanced-packaging lead times shrinking
  • The first hyperscaler capex guide-down

Until one of those fires, this is a tilt, not a turn. MU's fiscal Q3 print (~June 24) is the next live read on whether the pricing curve still matches the supercycle math.

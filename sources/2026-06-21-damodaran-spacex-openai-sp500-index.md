# Damodaran — "The Indexing Question: Should SpaceX, OpenAI and Anthropic be in the S&P 500?"

YouTube, @AswathDamodaranonValuation, published 2026-06-18 (video id `fbesNIjd_pY`). Transcript ingested 2026-06-21.

**Bias flag (read first):** Damodaran is a valuation academic and *index-mechanics* commentator, not a market-timer. His explicit prior in this piece is **skepticism of the people arguing *against* inclusion** — he names three groups (active managers protecting share, academics being "tone-deaf" to retirees, politicians anti-billionaire) and says he "sees other agendas at play." He is a self-described **retiree and passive-investing partisan** ("I am a retiree… I don't want to pay 2% to a manager who does nothing for me"). So the piece is structurally a *defense of passive / cap-weighting* and a *defense of including money-losing mega-caps*. Where he concedes the bear case (variance, momentum) he does so grudgingly. Weigh accordingly. Net editorial bias on the investable takeaways: **LOW** — most claims are mechanical/empirical, not directional price calls, and he makes **no price targets** here.

## TL;DR
- **Dated catalyst removed, not added:** S&P announced *the week before the SpaceX IPO* that it would **not** waive its inclusion rules (10%+ float, profitable in the 4 quarters before inclusion, large cap). SpaceX is therefore **not even eligible for consideration for at least the next 12 months**, and not guaranteed after that if still unprofitable. Same gate applies to OpenAI/Anthropic when they list. → **No index-inclusion passive-flow catalyst for these names in 2026.**
- **There is no "inclusion bump" left to trade.** Over the last ~20 years you "cannot reject the hypothesis that getting added has *no* effect" on price; deletion still hurts but less than before. His worked example: **Tesla after its 2020-12-18 inclusion fell >10% and underperformed**, while the deleted name (AIV) rose ~60%. "There is no magic bullet."
- **His pushback on the "passive flows = momentum bubble" thesis is the highest-signal item for the wiki:** if concentration were purely momentum/index-flow driven, small caps would be *dirt cheap* on P/E and EV/EBITDA vs large caps — **they are not**, and the earnings *growth* of the last ~20 years actually came from the large caps (winner-take-all economics). So Mag-7 concentration is, in his read, **largely fundamentally justified** — but he concedes flows make those same mega-caps **higher-variance** (they should fall hardest on outflows).

## Key facts (numbers Damodaran actually states, with his framing)
- **S&P 500 level on 2026-06-15: ~7,554.** Float-adjusted market cap of the 500 ≈ **$63.5T** (he also cites $63,498 as the index-units denominator). Implied "index units" conversion factor ≈ **0.1190** (index level ÷ market cap). These are mechanical, not valuation calls.
- **S&P 500 ≈ >80% of total US equity market cap** despite being 500 of ~5,000–6,000 listed names — his justification for why it's a good *aggregate-value* measure (but a poor "average stock" measure; equal-weight is better for that).
- **Active-manager underperformance (SPIVA):** in the 25 years of this century, **only 3 years** had >50% of active large-cap managers beating the S&P 500. At 10-year horizons, **no category** has >50% beating its style index; at 20 years, **>90% underperform** in nearly every category. **2025: 95.5% of large-cap growth funds underperformed** their style index. India SPIVA: ~60% short-term / ~93% long-term underperform. → his evidentiary base for "active investing collectively is in shambles."
- **Passive now >60% of invested money** (vs active ~10–15x larger than passive in 1998–99; crossover complete by 2024). Vanguard alone runs >200 index funds / >100 ETFs. Jack Bogle launched the first index fund (Vanguard 500) in **1976**.
- **Index weighting nuance:** S&P 500 is **float-adjusted** cap weight (why Meta/Walmart "punch below" their full cap). Dow 30 is **price-weighted** (he calls it nonsensical — Goldman/Caterpillar top-weighted, Microsoft far lower). Equal-weight skews returns to small caps.
- **Inclusion mechanics:** adding a $1T name and dropping a $1B name does **not** move the index level — the index *units* divisor re-bases so the level is unchanged. But the *character* of the index changes silently over time (he warns backward-looking "15x P/E is high for the S&P 500" comparisons are apples-to-oranges because the constituent mix has drifted).

## Key claims (and how confident)
- **CLAIM (high confidence, empirical):** index-inclusion price effect has largely *dissipated* in the last ~20 yrs; deletion effect attenuated but still negative. Backed by an S&P study + academic literature he cites generically. *Implication:* don't underwrite a SpaceX/OpenAI/Anthropic inclusion "pop."
- **CLAIM (medium, his interpretation):** Mag-7 top-heaviness is *fundamentally* driven (earnings growth concentrated in large caps; winner-take-all in ads = Google/Meta, etc.), **not** primarily index-flow momentum. Evidence: small caps are *not* cheap relative to large caps on P/E or EV/EBITDA — which they would be if flows alone were inflating the top. **This directly blunts the "indexing is mechanically inflating the top" bear leg.**
- **CONCESSION (medium):** if a momentum/flow effect exists, it shows up as **higher variance** in index-listed mega-caps — flows that lift them on the way up hit them hardest on the way down. He notes "some evidence of increased variance/standard deviation of companies that list in the index." → concentration is *both* justified *and* more fragile two-ways. Tail-risk relevant to a concentrated AI book.
- **CLAIM (medium-high, normative):** S&P's no-waiver decision was *correct* ("changing rules for individual companies sounds craven") — but he thinks the **profitability rule should be revisited** ("a large-cap company with immense potential that loses money shouldn't be barred… how can you call yourself the 500-largest-cap index if $1–2T companies aren't in it"). The **float rule he accepts** (cites Aramco's ~2–3% float as the thing it's designed to exclude).
- **CLAIM (high, his read of motive):** S&P's *real* reason for staggering inclusion is operational, not investor protection — absorbing SpaceX + OpenAI + Anthropic in one year would be "the Tesla adjustment multiplied by six or eight." And he expects these names to be **"just as messy" a year from now** (float/governance/profitability won't tidy up) — i.e. the 12-month deferral may extend.
- **CLAIM (medium, contrarian and testable):** "a big money-losing company with a bad business model has to be a bad investment" is **wrong** — price is what matters. He stakes a **wager he says he will test in coming weeks**: an index restricted to *only profitable, established-business-model* names built in **2001** would have **significantly underperformed** the unconstrained 500-largest-cap index. *(Untested as of this video — flag as a forthcoming Damodaran study, not a result.)*
- **CLAIM (high conviction, opinion):** "smart money" is a myth — average **alpha for both PE and hedge funds is negative**; money should be split into "humble vs arrogant," not "smart vs stupid." Used to rebut the "protect naive retail" argument.
- **CLAIM (high, bargaining framing):** "the index needs SpaceX/OpenAI/Anthropic more than they need the index" — for the companies, inclusion vs exclusion would produce "exactly the same" end-game price path. → for *us*, inclusion is an index-composition event, not a stock catalyst.

# "Open USD" consortium (Visa/Stripe/BlackRock/Coinbase + 140 firms) attacks Circle

## TL;DR
- **Why CRCL is dumping:** on 2026-06-30 Circle slid ~8% after Bloomberg/CoinDesk reported a **140+-firm consortium ("Open Standard") launching a rival USD stablecoin, "Open USD" (OUSD)**. It's the latest leg of a long de-rate — CRCL is **~52% off its Oct-2025 high** ($159.47 → **$75.96** close 06-29, Polygon-verified), below all EMAs.
- **The threat is real and structural** for three reasons: **distribution** (Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, Shopify, Coinbase, Klarna — the actual payment rails Circle lacks), **economics** (OUSD **shares nearly all reserve yield with adopting businesses** — directly attacking Circle's core revenue, which is *keeping* the yield on USDC reserves), and **multi-chain from day one** (not a walled-garden coin).
- **The sharpest signal for Circle specifically: Coinbase is in the consortium.** Coinbase is Circle's biggest distribution partner (takes ~half of USDC reserve revenue) — its backing of a rival is a defection, not just new competition.

## OpenUSD / OUSD — the specifics (the user's questions)
- **Issuer/governance:** "**Open Standard**," an independent company whose **board = the partners**; **collectively governed**, not a single issuer. Ticker **OUSD**.
- **Partners (140+):** Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, BlackRock, BNY (Bank of New York Mellon), Standard Chartered, Coinbase, Ripple, Alphabet/Google, Shopify, Klarna, Chime. **Circle and Tether are NOT in it.**
- **Chain / deployment:** launches **natively on Solana day one**, then **Solana, Stellar, Base, Polygon + other chains** later in 2026 → genuinely **multi-chain / cross-chain** (Solana SPL + EVM ERC-20 on Base/Polygon + Stellar). **Not** a non-transferable "BS coin" — it's designed as an interoperable, mint/redeem-at-par token.
- **Standards/mechanics:** businesses can **mint and redeem at no cost, no volume caps**; partners receive **nearly all reserve earnings after a management fee**; reserves "maintained at major financial institutions in compliance with US regulatory requirements."
- **The open questions (the "is it actually real yet" caveats):** **no firm launch date** ("2026"); the issuer has **not disclosed reserve composition, custodians, attestation cadence, or the regulated issuing entity**. Governance-by-140-firm-consortium is a coordination risk.

## The broader CRCL overhangs (this isn't only OpenUSD)
- **Stablecoin yield/rewards crackdown:** a March-2026 CLARITY Act draft threatened to **ban interest/rewards on stablecoins** → CRCL plunged ~18%. (OUSD's "share yield with businesses not users" model may *route around* a user-yield ban — worse for Circle.)
- **Fed stablecoin rules / USDC oversight tightening.**
- **Revenue model is ~entirely reserve interest** → rate-sensitive AND the exact economics the consortium is undercutting.

## Key claims (and how confident)
- Open USD consortium membership + reserve-revenue-sharing + multi-chain (Solana first) — **high** (consistent across CoinDesk/The Block/The Defiant).
- Coinbase as a Circle distribution partner now backing a rival — **high** (named partner; the ~50% reserve-share is the standing Circle–Coinbase deal structure).
- OUSD operational readiness (reserves/custodian/regulated entity/date) — **low/undisclosed** — the key "is it vaporware or real" unknown.

## Assessment
**A credible structural threat, not yet an operational one.** The consortium has the distribution and a sharper economic model (yield-sharing) that attacks Circle's main revenue line, and it's multi-chain by design — so "BS non-transferable coin" is *not* the right dismissal. But it has no live product, undisclosed reserve/regulatory specifics, and faces sticky USDC/USDT network effects. Net for [[CRCL]]: the moat got attacked by the firms that *are* the distribution (incl. Coinbase) — a real bear catalyst on an already-broken chart.

# Aswath Damodaran (NYU) — AI valuations, the "big market delusion," SpaceX, and the debt that could spill over

## TL;DR
- **"Architecture, not products" is the whole risk.** We've spent more building the AI factory (chips + data centers + LLMs) than any infrastructure in the last century — *before* knowing whether the products/services coming out of it will make money. Like the dot-com boom the ultimate winner won't be the infrastructure (it wasn't Cisco, it was Amazon); it'll be a products-and-services company that "might not even be public yet."
- **AI is real and huge but that ≠ profitable.** "This can be a big market where competition is so intense that companies don't make much money." The unknown isn't market size (trillions even in the pessimistic case) — it's *margins* and *reinvestment*.
- **The part he actually worries about = debt.** Not the Mag-7, not NVIDIA — it's the "small-to-mid-size companies using borrowed money to build AI architectures." When the reckoning comes, "they're going to take some of us down with them." Everything else is a healthy boom-and-cleanup.

## Key facts / claims
- **SpaceX valuation (the viral call):** Damodaran's intrinsic value = **$1.3T on ~$20B revenue** ("requires a lot of story to come true"). At >$1.8T (where it now trades) he calls it **overpriced** — "a great company that's not [currently] a great investment." Musk is a *net-neutral* on the price (as many won't buy because of him as will). SpaceX is "not a software/app company — it does real engineering," which is why it draws capital.
- **AI is NOT single-handedly carrying the market.** Strip out AI names and the market *still* trades at higher multiples than pre-AI. Over the last ~2 months the AI trade "turned sour" (NVDA, Mag-7 down) yet the market held — evidence of real breadth. "Markets are high because of AI" is a "lazy" take.
- **Chip countries are "accidental beneficiaries."** Korea/Taiwan didn't win AI — they make the chips feeding the AI factory. The chip/architecture/power layer is "the most tangible part of the AI story where the benefits are *already* there" (spending is happening now, not future potential).
- **The jobs math is the bear tell inside the bull case.** For AI to be worth the ~$26T TAM cited *in the SpaceX prospectus*, Damodaran estimates **~1 in 2 white-collar workers would have to lose their jobs** (lawyers, consultants, bankers, journalists). "You don't *want* the best-case AI scenarios to play out" — they'd be catastrophic for demand (who buys the products if incomes vanish?). His base case: white-collar losses in subsets, milder than optimists claim, not benign either.
- **"Big market delusion" framing:** any big market → people overreach → correction/cleanup. That's *healthy* and how change happens ("do you want a world run by actuaries? We'd still be in caves"). His only caveat: it matters when the overreach is **debt-funded** and spills into society.
- **Investor vs trader:** "95% of people in markets are traders, not investors." Both can make money on SpaceX; investors must do their *own* valuation, not buy off a CNBC number.
- **India aside (context):** FII outflows aren't really an "AI-play" story; India's structural issue is that no *young* companies break into the top of the cap table (unlike the S&P 500, where 14-yr-old Meta / 26-yr-old NVIDIA sit near the top). ~90% of the last 20 years' "innovation" is surface-level (repackaging existing businesses into winner-take-all). Off-mandate for this wiki but noted.

## Confidence
- SpaceX $1.3T-intrinsic-vs-$1.8T-price call — **high** (Damodaran's own published DCF; he's explicit).
- ~1-in-2 white-collar jobs to justify $26T TAM — **medium** (his estimate, tied to the prospectus number; directional, not a model output).
- "Debt-funded small/mid-caps are the systemic risk" — **medium-high** (consistent, repeated; the load-bearing worry).

## Bias flags
- Damodaran is a **valuation professor with no book to talk** — among the least conflicted voices in the roster (Tier-5 adjacent). Skews *skeptical-of-price*, not skeptical-of-technology; explicitly bullish on AI as a transformation. Interview is Indian-market-framed (host pushes FII/India angle), so ~40% is off-mandate.

## Contradictions / tensions with the wiki
- **Agrees with Chanos** (6/23) on *where* the risk sits — debt-funded infra players, not the chipmakers — and both name the accounting/timing mismatch. Adds a cleaner frame: the systemic danger is the *leverage*, not the technology. Reinforces [[ai-infrastructure-debt]].
- **Cuts against** the "architecture companies are the durable winners" tilt of parts of the wiki (semis/power longs): Damodaran's historical analogy says the *infrastructure* layer is the discovered, eventually-lower-return part; the money "next" is in products/services. Surfaced as the counter-thesis, not resolved — note the wiki's own [[ai-software-disruption]] tension (software multiples de-rating even as the "products layer" is where he says value accrues).

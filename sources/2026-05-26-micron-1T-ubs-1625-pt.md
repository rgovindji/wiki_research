# Micron Crosses $1T Market Cap on UBS $1,625 PT — Source Summary

On 2026-05-26, **Micron Technology became the seventh chip stock and the first US-based memory company in history to cross $1T market capitalization** after UBS analyst Timothy Arcuri raised his price target from $535 to **$1,625** — a 3x increase implying a **$1.8T market value at the new target**.

Shares closed +19.29% at **$895.88** on the day. The stock joins NVDA, AVGO, TSMC, and Samsung in the $1T chip-stock club.

This is the single biggest sell-side reframing event of the year so far in semis: **UBS is no longer valuing Micron as a cyclical memory commodity supplier. They are valuing it as an AI infrastructure company.**

## TL;DR

- **UBS PT $535 → $1,625** (highest on the Street; +3x prior target). Implied market cap ~$1.8T.
- **MU closed +19.29% at $895.88 on 2026-05-26**, crossing $1T market cap for the first time.
- **UBS thesis is structural, not cyclical:** long-term agreements (LTAs) between Micron and its hyperscaler customers support **EPS >$100/share through 2027-2029 even in a moderate downcycle**.
- This is **the cleanest external validation of the [[MU]] page's bull/high conviction stance** since the page was created. The "Micron is an AI infrastructure stock, not a cyclical commodity" framing is now institutional consensus at the largest Street firm.
- **MU has reportedly overtaken Samsung in HBM market share** in a separate report dated this week, putting MU at #2 globally behind SK Hynix.
- **Hyperscaler-funded capacity expansion**: MSFT, GOOGL, AMZN are reportedly proposing to fund SK Hynix capacity expansion to secure HBM capacity allocations through the late 2020s. This is the **forward-funded capacity** signal — a structural shift where customers pre-pay for capacity, validating the "memory is now a strategic asset, not a commodity" framing.

## The UBS structural-vs-cyclical reframing

UBS's reframing is the most important conceptual event for the [[MU]] page since the page's last full review:

**Traditional memory framing (what [[MU]] bear case warns against):**
- Memory is structurally cyclical
- Forward P/E looks cheap at peak earnings
- Every cycle since 1998 has 40-70% drawdown
- Buy on troughs, sell on peaks

**UBS reframing:**
- Long-term agreements with hyperscalers lock in **price floors AND volume floors**
- Customer pre-funding of capacity expansion = **strategic-supply-chain economics**, not spot-pricing economics
- $100+ EPS through 2027-2029 even in a moderate downcycle = **the downside is bounded by contracted economics**, not by spot prices
- Therefore: value as an **AI infrastructure stock** (TSMC-like multiple), not as a memory commodity stock

**Which means** for the [[MU]] page:
- The "cycle-peak P/E paradox" bear case bullet still holds in theory — but UBS is arguing the bounds are different now. The downside floor is higher because LTAs are structural.
- The "Mizuho $740 PT is below current price" bullet (added 2026-05-17) is now **stale** — Street is rapidly catching up.
- The framing in the page's "What it is / what it isn't" section needs updating: this is now a **leveraged multi-year trade on memory that has structurally repriced**, not just a 2028-cycle trade.

## What I'm not yet certain about

The UBS reframing is bullish on its face but raises an important question: **is the LTA framework actually as durable as UBS claims?** Memory makers have always had multi-year supply agreements (they're not new). The structural argument requires that THIS round of LTAs is different — perhaps because:
- Hyperscalers can no longer afford to under-order memory (memory bottlenecks AI deployments)
- Capex pre-funding creates contractual obligations that didn't exist in prior cycles
- HBM concentration (3 suppliers vs. dozens for commodity DRAM) creates pricing power

If UBS is right, the multi-decade memory cycle pattern is broken and the stock deserves a structural re-rating. If UBS is wrong, this is just a peak-of-cycle sell-side capitulation. **The watch item is whether the LTAs include effective minimum-price floors** — that's the empirical test.

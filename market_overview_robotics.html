The Humanoid Robotics Buildout

A reading-mode primer on the second-order picks-and-shovels trade emerging beneath the humanoid robot narrative. What's a real bottleneck, what's coincidental exposure, and where the bottleneck moves between now and 2028. Companion to market_overview_ai.html — robotics sector pages forthcoming at wiki/sectors/robotics.md

Humanoid robotics in 2026 looks like AI infrastructure in 2022: the narrative is louder than the revenue, but the component supply chain is being negotiated now for a buildout that's contracted-real by 2027 and 2028. The wiki's Bottleneck Roadmap framework transfers cleanly: pricing power lives wherever the binding constraint is, and in robotics that constraint starts with rare-earth permanent magnets, moves to precision sensors and actuators by 2027, and ends at scaled actuator and battery-energy-density manufacturing by 2028–2030. The mistake retail is making is the same one made in early AI cycles — owning mega-caps with rounding-error exposure instead of small/mid-caps where the bottleneck actually clears.

The TAM Picture

Market sizing estimates vary by an order of magnitude across research firms — from Grand View's conservative ~$4B by 2030 to MarketsandMarkets' $15.26B at a 39.2% CAGR through 2030. ABI Research projects a sharper curve: the market scales from ~$70M in 2025 to ~$6.5B by 2030 at a 138% CAGR, with the inflection between 2026 and 2027 once regulatory, safety, and ROI questions clear for the form factor. Counterpoint Research counts ~16,000 humanoid units installed globally in 2025; cumulative deployments are expected to exceed 100,000 by 2027.

These numbers matter less than the implied 2027–2028 unit ramp. Even ABI's aggressive case has only ~115,000 units shipped in 2027 — the harvest is 2028–2030, not 2027. The investable thesis between now and 2027 is positioning into bottleneck suppliers before the second-derivative revenue actually shows up. By 2028, the narrative will either be validated by scaled unit shipments (Tesla Optimus, Figure 02, Agility Digit, Chinese OEMs led by AgiBot and Unitree) or compressed by another timeline slip.

Five Working Theses

1. Rare-earth permanent magnets are the binding 2026–2027 constraint

Every humanoid actuator needs neodymium-iron-boron (NdFeB) magnets, and there is no synthetic substitute on a chemistry that hits the torque density humanoids require. A general-purpose humanoid runs roughly 10× the magnet content of an EV. China controls approximately 90% of downstream rare-earth processing; one export-control announcement can throttle the entire Western supply chain. MP Materials' Stillwater plant is the only fully integrated NdFeB magnet line in the US, with commercial production targeted for H1 2026 — anchor customers include Apple and General Motors. USAR is a higher-risk pure-speculation play on the Round Top deposit in Texas. UUUU is a uranium miner with a secondary rare-earth separation line at White Mesa; the humanoid thesis is real but secondary to the uranium story. This is the cleanest "own the bottleneck" trade on the screen.

2. Sensors are interesting but substitutable; weight the right names

Force-torque sensing (VPG), lidar / spatial awareness (OUST), magnetic position sensing (ALGM), and machine vision (CGNX) all have real humanoid attach. The problem is that sensor stacks have substitutes — camera-only vs. lidar-equipped, capacitive vs. magnetic position sensing, strain-gauge vs. piezo force sensors. The bottleneck rents diffuse. VPG just popped ~30% on Q1 2026 earnings and CEO Ziv Shoshani is explicitly pivoting the narrative to "Physical AI" — at a P/E around 135–215, the multiple has already done most of the work. OUST is the highest-quality fundamental story of the sensor cluster (49% YoY Q1 2026 growth, profitability inflection late 2026) but dilution risk via the $100M ATM is real. ALGM is the under-appreciated name: magnetic position and current sensors for motor control, ~$7B cap, currently 70%+ auto-exposed but with mechanical attach to every humanoid joint.

3. Edge AI / robotic compute is where AMBA-style names get their second life

Humanoids run a different inference stack than autonomous vehicles. Latency budgets are tighter (sub-10ms control loops), power budgets are smaller (battery-bound), and model sizes are dropping fast as world-models like NVIDIA Cosmos and Google Gemini Robotics ship. AMBA is the obvious name — FY2026 revenue $390.7M (+37%), Edge AI = 80% of revenue, CV3-AD ramping in calendar 2026 — but the more interesting leverage is in CEVA (DSP/NPU IP licensing into every robotics SoC the OEMs ship) and LSCC (Lattice low-power FPGAs for sensor fusion and motor control). The royalty / low-power compute angle is cleaner robotics exposure than AMBA's crowded "vision brains" framing.

4. The mega-caps named in every "humanoid stack" list are coincidental exposure

NVDA, AMD, QCOM, TXN, ON, CSCO, ABB, ROK, PH will all sell into humanoids. Humanoid revenue contribution will be a rounding error for all of them through at least 2028. Owning these as a "humanoid play" is the same category error as owning AAPL as a "VR play" in 2016 — not wrong, but the thesis isn't really humanoids, it's the broader business. The wiki's AI capex memo already covers NVDA and AMD; they don't need a second basis here.

5. The actual choke points retail can't easily access live in China

The two most strategically important humanoid component suppliers — Sanhua Intelligent Controls (002050.SZ, thermal management / valves) and Leader Drive (688017.SS, harmonic drives) — trade in Shenzhen and Shanghai respectively. Both are designed-in across Tesla Optimus and Figure programs. Western retail can only access these via Stock Connect (Hong Kong brokerages) or via ETFs that wrap them. The pure-play Western harmonic-drive alternatives are smaller and have weaker design-win pipelines. The honest version of "own the bottleneck" requires either Chinese exposure or accepting that the cleanest plays are unavailable to most US/Canadian retail.

The Robotics Bottleneck Roadmap

The same frame the wiki applies to the AI capex cycle applies to robotics. The binding physical constraint moves down the stack each year, and pricing power lives where the binding constraint is. Today's constraint is upstream — rare-earth processing. By 2027 it shifts mid-stack to precision sensors, harmonic drives, and motor position sensing. By 2028–2030 it moves to scaled actuator manufacturing and battery energy density, which is where Chinese suppliers run furthest ahead.

The single highest-leverage variable for the multi-year robotics thesis is whether the West rebuilds rare-earth processing capacity at scale before China weaponizes export controls. MP's Stillwater commercial ramp in H1 2026 is the most material near-term datapoint. A second leverage point: whether any Western harmonic-drive maker hits volume parity with Leader Drive — none currently looks close.

What Could Go Wrong

Three counter-arguments deserve weight before sizing the thesis.

First, timeline slip is the base case, not the bear case. IDTechEx tracks fewer than 100 humanoids actively deployed in warehouse pilots as of early 2025. Warehouse pilot-to-scaled-rollout cycles run 18–30 months. Tesla, Figure, and Agility have all missed prior unit-shipment guidance. A 2-year slip from 2027 to 2029 on the unit ramp pushes the second-derivative revenue at every supplier in Tier 1 out the same distance. The trade is right; the timing usually isn't.

Second, the multiples are doing most of the work. VPG is up roughly 4× in 12 months on narrative repricing, not earnings. MP is up meaningfully on DoD partnership news. USAR is a pre-revenue speculation trading at $1B+. The same pattern that played out in AI infrastructure 2023–2024 — multiple expansion ahead of earnings catch-up — is replaying here at a smaller cap base, which means corrections will be sharper.

Third, China is the supply chain. The cleanest design-in suppliers (Sanhua, Leader Drive, Inovance) are listed in Shenzhen and Shanghai. The cheapest unit-economics OEMs (Unitree, AgiBot, UBTECH) are Chinese. A US export-control regime that reciprocally restricts Western component sales into Chinese humanoid programs would damage Tier 1 names along with the Chinese OEMs. The political economy here is more fragile than the AI infrastructure equivalent.

Open Questions

  • Does MP's Stillwater plant hit commercial NdFeB magnet production on the H1 2026 schedule, or slip into late 2026? First-shipment confirmation is the single most material datapoint for the Tier 1 thesis.
  • How does VPG's Q2 2026 print read against the "Physical AI" pivot narrative? P/E of 135–215 leaves no margin for execution stumble.
  • Does any Western harmonic-drive maker (Harmonic Drive Systems Japan via ADR, or smaller US/EU plays) hit volume parity with Leader Drive by 2027, or does this remain a Chinese-supplier-dependent line item?
  • When does the first Western humanoid OEM (Figure, Agility, Apptronik) confirm a multi-thousand-unit production commitment with named component suppliers? Single most useful leading indicator of which Tier 1 names get pulled into design wins.
  • Does OUST hit its operating-cash-flow-positive target in H2 2026 without further dilution beyond the $100M ATM already in flight?
  • Does Tesla Optimus reach the announced 2026 internal-deployment target (low thousands of units inside Tesla factories), and if so, do component suppliers get publicly named?

Where This Lands

The robotics sector in May 2026 maps to AI infrastructure in roughly mid-2023: narrative is ahead of earnings, supply chains are being negotiated for a buildout that's plausibly contracted by 2027 and scaled by 2028–2030, and retail screens are full of mega-cap "exposure" names that won't actually move on the trade. The replicable lesson from the AI capex memo is to concentrate where the bottleneck is provable rather than basket the category.

Our highest-conviction Tier 1 names on first pass are MP (DoD-backed, integrated US producer, near-term ramp catalyst), ALGM (under-appreciated motor-position sensing, attaches mechanically to every joint), and CEVA (IP royalty leverage to whichever SoC vendor wins the robotic compute socket). OUST is the highest-quality sensor name but has dilution overhang. VPG is interesting but the multiple has eaten most of the asymmetry. Mega-caps owned on their primary theses are fine; they don't need a second basis here.

The bottom line

Humanoid robotics is a real second-order AI trade. The bottleneck is rare-earth processing now, sensors and harmonic drives in 2027, scaled actuator and battery manufacturing in 2028–2030. The mistake retail is making is owning the mega-cap "stack" instead of the small/mid-cap bottleneck. Concentration beats baskets when the binding constraint is identifiable. Add Tier 1 names on weakness; let Tier 2 ride on its primary thesis; leave Tier 3 alone.

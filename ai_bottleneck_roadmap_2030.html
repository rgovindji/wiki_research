The Bottleneck Decade

AI's limit isn't ideas — it's physics. Here is where compute actually breaks between now and 2030, and the handful of names worth owning because of it.

Synthesis · 2026-06-21 · figures dated inline · hover tickers for the live stance · research, not advice

The argument in one breath. Demand for intelligence is, for practical purposes, unlimited; the supply of it is gated by a short series of physical chokepoints that bind in a predictable order. Own the chokepoints with the deepest moats and the longest runways, buy them when the cycle hands you a discount, and rent — never marry — the parts that will be competed away. Done right, this is the highest-conviction multi-year trade in the market. The four refinements since we first mapped it: memory is confirming, power got promoted, a CPU layer appeared, and EUV is now the consensus ceiling.

I · The FrameTwo bottlenecks, two clocks

The single most useful correction to the original roadmap is this: there is not one bottleneck, there are two, and they run on different clocks. There is the constraint on manufacturing intelligence — the chips themselves — which is a 2028-2030 story and funnels, ultimately, through one Dutch company. And there is the constraint on deploying it — getting built chips powered, cooled, and plugged into a grid — which is a problem right now, in 2026.

We originally underweighted the second. The wiki carried Dylan Patel's line that "power is not the bottleneck — there are sixteen-plus vendors of generation, and half of new capacity will be behind-the-meter." That was true as a statement about the theoretical availability of electrons. It has been overtaken by the operational reality, stated most bluntly by the people actually building the clusters:

"I have chips I can't plug in."— Satya Nadella, on the binding constraint shifting from silicon to power, June 2026

OpenAI's head of industrial compute put a number on it: roughly 100 gigawatts of US hyperscaler demand by the end of the decade — a double-digit percentage of the entire American grid — against transformers and turbines booked three to four years out. So the frame for the rest of this piece: power is the bottleneck that decides returns over the next two to three years; EUV is the bottleneck that decides them over the next five. You want exposure to both, expressed differently.

II · ConfirmingMemory was the 2026 call, and it just printed

The cleanest validation of the whole framework arrived this month, not from a podcast but from a customs office. South Korea's preliminary June exports — the most real-time read we get on memory, because SK Hynix and Samsung are Korean — showed the supercycle not only intact but broadening: HBM exports up 51% month-over-month, NAND up 28%, SSD up 25%. The year-over-year figures (DRAM +342%) are base-effect theatre; the sequential acceleration is the signal, and it says the cycle has rotated out of DRAM and into the higher-value, longer-runway products.

This matters because inference — now the majority of all AI compute, on its way to 80%-plus — is memory-bandwidth-bound. An AI server carries several terabytes of memory against a few hundred gigabytes for a traditional cloud box. The bottleneck is structural, not a spot shortage. But memory is also where the framework demands the most discipline: it is cyclical. The one yellow flag in that same Korea print was DRAM at merely +3% month-over-month — the first hint the DRAM price curve is flattening even as HBM accelerates. Memory is a bottleneck you rent, sized and time-boxed, not a franchise you hold through the cycle. We will come back to that distinction; it is the difference between making money here and giving it back.

III · PromotedPower is where the constraint — and the cheap entries — now live

If memory is the bottleneck that's confirming, power is the one that's been promoted, and it is the most investable idea in this entire piece for a simple reason: it is the only major chokepoint where the assets are still trading at a discount. The semiconductor layer has already re-rated; the chips are priced for the shortage. The power layer, oddly, is not — because the market still files these names under "utilities" rather than "AI infrastructure."

That mispricing is the opportunity. A merchant power producer signing twenty-year data-center supply contracts at $70-90 per megawatt-hour is not a sleepy utility; it is a contracted, inflation-protected annuity on the buildout, and several trade near their lows. The constraint is real and durable: you cannot wish a gigawatt onto the grid, transformer lead times are measured in years, and the labor to build it is itself scarce. The risk is equally real — merchant power is cyclical, rate-sensitive, and the re-rate depends on converting pipeline into signed, energized contracts. But the asymmetry — a de-rated entry into a structural shortage — is the best risk/reward on the board.

IV · The TaxPackaging, substrates, and the interconnect toll

Between the chip and the cluster sits a layer that takes a toll on every unit shipped: advanced packaging (CoWoS, sold out at TSMC, and getting physically harder to sign off as packages scale and warpage bleeds into signal integrity), and the optical and networking fabric that stitches GPUs into a coherent machine — 1.6-terabit transceivers, ABF substrates, copper foil. This is a genuine 2027 bottleneck. But it is mostly a rent, not a franchise: pluggable optics and outsourced packaging get competed down as capacity catches up. The exception is the company that designs the custom silicon and the networking around it, capturing the toll structurally rather than cyclically — which is why the one name we'd own here sits with the franchises, not the rent.

V · The CeilingEUV is the one chokepoint that doesn't move

Everything above is a constraint that eventually eases as the industry throws capital at it. There is exactly one that does not, because it cannot be bought from a second supplier at any price: extreme-ultraviolet lithography. No advanced AI chip — none — is manufactured without an ASML machine, and the company's annual tool output physically caps how much leading-edge compute can exist. This used to be a contrarian wiki call. It is now consensus: OpenAI's compute chief, asked for the single biggest unsolved problem in AI infrastructure, dug past fabs and memory to the root —

"If you dig down even deeper, it's ASML. For all of these you need ASML machines. That is the single choke point of the whole supply chain."— Sachin Katti, OpenAI, June 2026

The math is what makes it the most durable franchise in technology:

EUV output: ~70 tools/yr today → ~100 by 2030 · cumulative ~700
1 GW of AI capacity ≈ 3.5 EUV tools' worth of fab time
→ ceiling ≈ ~200 GW/yr of AI compute, even if 100% allocated to AI
Leverage: $50B of data-center capex per GW rides on ~$1.2B of EUV tooling

Read that last line again. The entire trillion-dollar buildout funnels through roughly $1.2 billion of ASML equipment per gigawatt — the thinnest, least-substitutable, highest-leverage link in the chain. Paired with TSMC, which converts those tools into wafers and whose capacity discipline is the single thing preventing a 1999-style overbuild, this is the manufacturing ceiling of the AI era. The only real bear case is a peculiar one: that ASML remains a "generous monopolist" and never fully prices its position, leaving the rent to its customers rather than its shareholders. Worth watching — but a high-class problem to own.

VI · The ConclusionWhat to actually own

A framework that won't name names isn't research. So here is the call. The organizing principle: concentrate in the chokepoints with the deepest moats and longest runways (own through the cycle); take the elevated near-term constraint where it's still cheap (power); and rent the cyclical layers with explicit exit discipline. Ranked by multi-year, risk-adjusted return potential:

1Conviction: highest · own through the cycle

ASML — the ceiling itself~$1,930 · at ATH

The purest expression of the entire thesis and the least substitutable asset in technology. If AI compute scales to 2030, ASML is the one company that must grow with it, with no second source at any price. Why own it: monopoly, the hardest moat, the longest runway, the highest leverage to the buildout. The catch: it's at an all-time high — this is an accumulate-on-weakness name, and the 2026 China air-pocket may hand you the entry. Cited median PT $1,775 (verify). Buy the dips; do not chase the highs.

2Conviction: high · best franchise-at-a-fair-entry today

NVDA — the platform, not the chip~$211 · −11% off high

The one franchise on this list trading at a reasonable entry right now — it's drifted since its last print while the fundamentals compounded. NVIDIA is no longer a chip company; its CUDA stack and reference architecture have become the industry's standard (competitors build to it). Inference-majority demand secures the bid, and supply is sold out. Why own it: the deepest software moat in compute, sold-out demand, and the cleanest quality-at-a-fair-price setup in the group. The catch: the most crowded trade on earth — any capex air-pocket hits here first. This is the core holding.

3Conviction: med-high · the best risk/reward — buyable cheap

The power basket — CEG + NRG (with VRT)de-rated 30%+

If you want the best absolute return over the next two to three years, this is it — the elevated bottleneck that is still, inexplicably, on sale. CEG (nuclear, −34% off high, marquee hyperscaler PPAs) and NRG (merchant IPP, −29%, twenty-year DC supply contracts) are contracted annuities on the buildout that the market still prices as utilities. VRT adds the power-and-cooling equipment leg (with one watch-item: the 800VDC shift may erode its UPS content). Why own it: a de-rated entry into a genuine, durable shortage — the only place the chokepoint and the discount coexist. The catch: merchant-power cyclicality and rate sensitivity; the re-rate needs PPAs to keep converting.

4Conviction: high · the manufacturing franchise

TSM — the wafer chokepoint~$462 · near highs

The other half of the manufacturing ceiling: the sole foundry that turns ASML's tools into leading-edge wafers, with ~86% of its most advanced node going to AI by 2027. Its capacity discipline is the mechanism preventing an overbuild — a structural positive that also means it won't flood the market. Why own it: an irreplaceable franchise with pricing power and a multi-decade runway. The catch: near its highs (entry-sensitive — pair with ASML and accumulate), plus the one non-fundamental tail every Taiwan name carries.

5Conviction: high · the second compute franchise

AVGO — the custom-silicon toll-taker~$411 · −17% off high

The structural way to own the interconnect/custom-silicon tax: Broadcom designs the ASICs and the networking for the hyperscalers building their own chips, with an OpenAI partnership framed as a >$1T path. It collects whether or not NVIDIA wins every socket — and it's de-rated on an expectations unwind, not a fundamental crack. Why own it: a franchise (not a rent) on the custom-silicon wave, at a better entry than the chip is given credit for. The catch: lumpier than NVIDIA; the "growth plateau" worry around each print.

VII · DisciplineWhat to rent, and what to refuse

Rent, don't marry — memory. MU is a real franchise on the HBM-bandwidth leg, and the Korea data confirms the cycle. But it is cyclical, it's at an all-time high, and DRAM is already plateauing sequentially. Own it sized as a trade, with an explicit exit — the bull's own data has new fab supply arriving 2028-29, which is the cycle's built-in end date. The trim signal is the first negative month-over-month memory-ASP print.

Refuse the rent dressed as a franchise. Commodity DRAM/NAND pure-plays at all-time highs (a SanDisk up 4,000% off the spin is the opposite of a de-rated entry), pluggable-optics cyclical names, outsourced packaging (a 14%-margin OSAT is rent, not a moat), and — most of all — the sub-scale neoclouds. WhiteFiber, Applied Digital, IREN and their kin are levered bets that burn more cash per quarter than they hold, dependent on a financing window staying open. They belong in a willing-to-lose lottery sleeve, never in a core book that's trying to compound through 2030.

VIII · The TellWhat ends the trade

Every multi-year thesis needs a falsifier, and this one's is unusually clean. The cycle does not end when valuations look stretched or when a pundit calls a top — those signals arrive late and lie often. It ends when supply discipline breaks. Watch two numbers: TSMC's capacity guidance against NVIDIA's order intent (a widening gap means discipline holds; a closing one is the overbuild setup), and the first negative month-over-month memory-ASP print (the cyclical-peak tell for the rent layer). Until those fire, the bottleneck is real, the demand is sold out, and the names above are the way to own it.
